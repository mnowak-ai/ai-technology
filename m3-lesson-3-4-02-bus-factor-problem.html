Lesson 3.4 — Knowledge Sharing & Collaboration

The Bus Factor Problem: What Happens When the Expert Leaves

The bus factor is the number of people who would need to be hit by a bus before a project is in serious trouble. For most critical systems, that number is one. Here's why that's a real engineering risk — and what to do about it.

What the Bus Factor Actually Measures

The bus factor (sometimes called the "lottery factor" — what happens if someone wins the lottery and quits) measures how concentrated critical knowledge is in your team. A bus factor of 1 means a single engineer's absence — vacation, illness, resignation, or reassignment — would leave your team unable to maintain, debug, or evolve a critical system.

Most engineering teams underestimate how many of their systems have a bus factor of 1. It's rarely obvious in normal operations — the expert is always there, always available. It only becomes visible during incidents, when they're unavailable, or after they've left.

The bus factor isn't a hypothetical risk. It's an incident waiting for the expert to take a vacation.

Bus Factor Risk Scale
How many people need to be unavailable before this system is in serious trouble?
1
Critical risk
One person's absence — vacation, sick day, or resignation — puts the system at risk. This is more common than most teams realize, especially for legacy systems and ML pipelines.
2
High risk
Better, but still fragile. Two simultaneous absences — which happen during incidents, holidays, and layoffs — expose the same gap.
3+
Acceptable
Three or more people can maintain the system independently. Knowledge is distributed enough that normal attrition and absence don't create crises.

The Two Types of Knowledge — And Why One Is Much Harder to Transfer

When a senior engineer leaves, they take two types of knowledge with them. Understanding the difference is critical to knowing what to prioritize in knowledge transfer.

Explicit knowledge — can be documented
The "what" and "how"
• Architecture diagrams and system design docs
• Runbooks and SOPs
• API documentation
• Deployment procedures
• Configuration files and their meaning
• Incident histories and postmortems
Tacit knowledge — hardest to transfer
The "why" and "when"
• Why a particular design was chosen over alternatives
• Which edge cases to watch for under what conditions
• How the system actually behaves vs. how it's documented to behave
• Which vendors/services are reliable and which aren't
• Unwritten team norms and decision-making patterns
• Which warnings can be safely ignored and which can't

Most knowledge transfer efforts focus on explicit knowledge — the docs, the runbooks, the diagrams. That's necessary but not sufficient. The tacit knowledge — the judgment, the context, the institutional memory — is what actually makes an expert irreplaceable. And it's almost never written down, because experts don't realize they have it.

A Four-Step Bus Factor Reduction Plan

1
Audit — identify your bus factor 1 systems
For every critical system, ask: if this engineer were unavailable for two weeks, could the rest of the team maintain it? Debug an incident in it? Deploy a fix? If the honest answer is "no" or "maybe," that system has a bus factor of 1. List them — don't assume you already know all of them.
2
Capture tacit knowledge through structured interviews
The most effective knowledge transfer technique is not asking experts to "write documentation." It's interviewing them with specific questions: "What would a new engineer get wrong about this system?" "What's the worst thing that could happen that isn't documented?" "What do you check first when something goes wrong?" Record the answers and turn them into documentation. Experts know the answers — they just need the right questions to surface them.
3
Shadow and reverse-shadow critical work
For each bus factor 1 system, schedule shadowing sessions: a second engineer watches the expert work on real tasks. Then reverse-shadow: the second engineer does the work while the expert watches and corrects. This transfers both explicit procedures and tacit judgment — you can't learn the "feel" of a system from a document alone.
4
Distribute on-call ownership deliberately
The fastest way to force knowledge distribution is to rotate on-call responsibility. Engineers who are on call for a system they don't fully understand quickly identify the documentation gaps — because they feel the gaps acutely when they're paged at 2am. Pair this with postmortem practices that build documentation from every incident.

The Bus Factor Audit — Know Your Risk

Run through these questions for a critical system you work on. Check each one that's currently true.

Bus Factor Audit

The runbook for this system was last reviewed within 90 days
The "why" behind key design decisions is documented — not just the what and how
Known edge cases and failure modes are documented, not just held in one engineer's memory
On-call rotation includes more than one person who can respond to incidents independently
A new team member could onboard to this system using existing documentation without needing extended expert guidance

If you checked 0–2: bus factor is likely 1. If you checked 3–4: moderate risk. If you checked 5–6: good distribution. The unchecked items are your knowledge transfer priorities.

📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

The bus factor measures how many people need to be unavailable before a system is in serious trouble. Most critical systems in most teams have a bus factor of 1 — which means a single vacation, illness, or resignation creates an incident. This is not hypothetical; it's a predictable failure mode.

Explicit knowledge (what and how) can be documented. Tacit knowledge (why and when — the judgment, context, and edge cases) is much harder to transfer and almost never written down. When an expert leaves, the explicit knowledge usually survives. The tacit knowledge usually doesn't.

The most effective bus factor reduction techniques: audit which systems have a factor of 1, use structured interviews (not free-form documentation requests) to surface tacit knowledge, shadow and reverse-shadow critical work, and distribute on-call ownership deliberately. AI tools can help capture structured interviews but still cannot extract what the expert doesn't know they know.
