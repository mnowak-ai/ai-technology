Lesson 3.3 — Creating SOPs & Guides

Writing a Technical SOP From Scratch: A Full Walkthrough with a Real Example

SOPs aren't just boring documents IT teams are forced to write. They're the instructions that keep things from going catastrophically wrong at 2am. Here's how to build one that actually works.

What Is an SOP, Really?

SOP stands for Standard Operating Procedure. In plain terms: it's a document that tells someone exactly how to do a specific task, step by step, so they get the same correct result every time — whether it's their first time or their hundredth.

In IT and tech, SOPs cover things like: how to deploy a new version of software, how to respond when a server goes down, how to onboard a new team member, or how to handle a security alert. A good SOP removes guesswork. A bad SOP creates it.

The goal of an SOP is not to document what happened last time. It's to give the next person a clear path forward — even if that person is you at 3am after four hours of sleep.

The Seven Sections Every Technical SOP Needs

There's no single universal SOP format, but every effective technical SOP covers the same core territory. Here's each section — what it is, why it matters, and what it looks like in practice.

We'll build this SOP together using one real example throughout: SOP for Responding to an AI Model Degradation Alert in Production.

1
Purpose

One or two sentences. What does this SOP exist to accomplish? Why does the procedure matter? Don't write a paragraph — write a sentence that even a new team member would immediately understand.

A weak purpose statement says what the document is. A strong one says why it exists.

❌ Weak
This SOP describes the procedure for AI model degradation alerts.
✅ Strong
This SOP gives on-call engineers a consistent response process for AI model degradation alerts — reducing time to resolution and preventing cascading failures in dependent services.
2
Scope

Who does this SOP apply to? What systems, environments, or situations does it cover? What does it not cover? Scope prevents the SOP from being used in a situation it wasn't designed for.

📄 Our Example
Scope: This SOP applies to all on-call engineers responding to PagerDuty alerts from the ai-inference-prod monitoring policy. It covers degradation events in the production inference cluster only. It does not apply to staging or development environments, or to complete service outages (see SOP-INC-002 for outage response).

That last sentence — "it does not apply to..." — is just as important as what it does cover. An engineer under pressure grabs the first SOP they find. Clear scope stops them from following the wrong procedure.

3
Roles and Responsibilities

Who does what? Name the roles (not individual people, since people change) and describe each role's responsibilities in this procedure. This section prevents two engineers from doing the same thing simultaneously — and prevents nobody from doing something critical.

📄 Our Example
On-Call Engineer (Primary): Owns the investigation and coordinates response. Makes the call on escalation.

ML Platform On-Call: Consulted for model-specific diagnostics. Paged if the primary engineer cannot identify root cause within 30 minutes.

Incident Commander: Engaged only if the event is escalated to a P1. Coordinates across teams and handles stakeholder communication.
4
Prerequisites

What does the engineer need before they can follow this procedure? Access to which systems? Which credentials? Which tools installed? Don't assume — list it. A person following an SOP for the first time at 2am cannot go find a system access request form mid-incident.

📄 Our Example
Prerequisites:
  • PagerDuty on-call access for ai-inference-prod policy
  • Read access to Grafana dashboard: AI Inference Service — Production
  • kubectl access to prod-us-east-1 cluster (request via go/k8s-access)
  • Slack membership: #ai-oncall, #ml-platform
5
Step-by-Step Procedure

This is the heart of the SOP. Number every step. Use active voice. One action per step. Include exact commands, not vague directions. Include "why this step matters" notes for any step that an engineer might be tempted to skip.

📄 Our Example — First 5 Steps
1
Acknowledge the alert in PagerDuty.
Click Acknowledge within 5 minutes of the alert firing. This stops the escalation timer and signals to the team that someone is on it.
2
Post in #ai-oncall: "Investigating [ALERT NAME] — [timestamp]"
This creates a paper trail and prevents a second engineer from starting a parallel investigation.
3
Open the AI Inference Service dashboard in Grafana.
Check the following panels: Request Latency (p99), Error Rate (5xx), Model Throughput, GPU Utilization. Note which metrics are outside normal range and by how much.
Dashboard: https://grafana.internal/d/ai-inference-prod
4
Check recent deployments in the deployment log.
kubectl rollout history deployment/inference-api -n ai-prod
Why: most model degradations happen within 30 minutes of a deployment. If a deployment occurred recently, that's your first hypothesis.
5
Check pod health and restart counts.
kubectl get pods -n ai-prod -l app=inference-api
Look for pods in CrashLoopBackOff or with high restart counts. If 2+ pods are in CrashLoopBackOff, proceed to Step 7 (Emergency Rollback).
6
Escalation Path

When should the engineer stop trying to solve this themselves and get help? Be specific — don't say "escalate when appropriate." Name the conditions, name the people or channels, and name the method.

📄 Our Example
Escalate to ML Platform On-Call if: You cannot identify root cause within 30 minutes, or model accuracy metrics have dropped more than 15% from baseline.

How: Page via PagerDuty policy ml-platform-oncall. Also post in #ml-platform with the Grafana link and a summary of what you've checked.

Escalate to P1 Incident if: Error rate exceeds 20% for more than 10 minutes, or more than 3 dependent services are impacted. Engage Incident Commander via #incident-response.
7
Review and Revision Schedule

When does this SOP expire? Who owns it? When should it be reviewed? SOPs without review schedules go stale — and a stale SOP is sometimes worse than no SOP, because it creates false confidence.

📄 Our Example
Owner: ML Platform Team (primary contact: on-call rotation lead)
Review Cycle: Every 90 days, or immediately after any incident where this SOP was used and found to be incomplete or incorrect.
Next Review Date: [YYYY-MM-DD]
Last Updated: [YYYY-MM-DD] by [Name/Role]

What the Finished SOP Looks Like

Here's a compressed view of our complete example SOP. Notice how every section is specific, scannable, and action-oriented. An engineer who has never followed this procedure before should be able to do so correctly on their first try.

SOP-ML-001: AI Model Degradation Alert Response
Owner: ML Platform | Review cycle: 90 days | Version 1.2
Purpose
Give on-call engineers a consistent response process for AI model degradation alerts — reducing time to resolution and preventing cascading failures.
Scope
Applies to: on-call engineers responding to PagerDuty ai-inference-prod alerts. Does not apply to: staging/dev environments, complete outages (see SOP-INC-002).
Prerequisites
  • PagerDuty on-call access (ai-inference-prod)
  • Grafana: AI Inference Service — Production
  • kubectl access to prod-us-east-1
  • Slack: #ai-oncall, #ml-platform
Procedure
1
Acknowledge alert in PagerDuty within 5 minutes.
2
Post in #ai-oncall: "Investigating [ALERT NAME] — [timestamp]"
3
Check Grafana dashboard. Note which metrics are out of range.
4
Run: kubectl rollout history deployment/inference-api -n ai-prod. Check for recent deploys.
5
Check pod health. If 2+ pods in CrashLoopBackOff → go to Step 7 (Emergency Rollback).
Escalation
30 min without root cause → Page ml-platform-oncall. Error rate >20% for 10 min → P1. Engage #incident-response.

The Most Common SOP Writing Mistakes

Even experienced engineers make these mistakes when writing SOPs for the first time.

❌ Mistake 1: Assuming Knowledge
"Restart the inference service." — Which service? How? Via kubectl, a script, the cloud console? What if the restart fails?
✅ Fix It
"Restart the inference-api deployment: kubectl rollout restart deployment/inference-api -n ai-prod. Wait 2 minutes. If pods don't reach Running state, go to Step 9."
❌ Mistake 2: Vague Escalation
"Escalate to the appropriate team if the issue persists." — Which team? How? When is "persists" too long?
✅ Fix It
"If you have not identified root cause within 30 minutes, page the ML Platform on-call via PagerDuty policy ml-platform-oncall."
❌ Mistake 3: No "What If"
Steps that assume everything works perfectly and offer no guidance when a step fails.
✅ Fix It
After critical steps, add: "If this command returns an error, see the Troubleshooting section" or "If this fails: [specific next action]."
🧠 Quick Check
A team writes an SOP for deploying a new ML model. They include Purpose, Prerequisites, Steps, and Review Cycle. During a real deployment, an engineer gets stuck because they don't know whether to proceed if a specific health check fails — the SOP says nothing about it. What critical section was missing?
📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

Every technical SOP needs seven sections: Purpose, Scope, Roles and Responsibilities, Prerequisites, Step-by-Step Procedure, Escalation Path, and Review Schedule. Skipping any of these creates gaps that surface at the worst moment — during an incident.

Steps must be specific: one action per step, active voice, exact commands, and "what to do if this fails." Vague steps like "restart the service" or "verify success" are the most common causes of SOP failure under pressure.

Write the SOP for the engineer who has never done this before, at 3am, after a PagerDuty alert woke them up. If they can follow it correctly on the first try without asking anyone, it's a good SOP.
