Lesson 3.4 — Knowledge Sharing & Collaboration

Community of Practice: How Tech Organizations Build Learning Communities That Outlast Tools

A community of practice is a group of people who share a domain and learn together over time. It's one of the most durable knowledge-sharing mechanisms in tech — and it survives the tool migrations, reorgs, and turnover that kill wikis.

What a Community of Practice Actually Is

The term "community of practice" (CoP) comes from organizational learning research by Etienne Wenger and Jean Lave in the 1990s. In tech, it describes something familiar: a group of engineers, data scientists, or other practitioners who share a professional domain — security, ML, platform engineering, frontend — and who learn from each other across team and organizational boundaries.

The key distinction from a team: a community of practice crosses formal organizational lines. The security CoP at a large tech company includes security engineers from every product team, not just the central security team. The ML CoP includes ML engineers from every platform and product domain. Members participate because of shared domain interest, not because of org chart proximity.

Communities of practice survive tool migrations, reorgs, and leadership changes because they're built on shared identity and mutual learning — not on a platform or a process that someone decided to implement.

🏷️
Domain
A shared area of expertise that members care about: ML ops, API security, frontend performance, database reliability. The domain gives the community its identity and reason to exist.
👥
Community
The people — practitioners across teams and departments who participate. Members don't have to work on the same team; they share the domain. Participation is typically voluntary.
🛠️
Practice
The shared activities, artifacts, and knowledge that members develop together: design patterns, shared libraries, review processes, standards, postmortem learnings. Practice is what makes the community more than a social group.

Why Communities of Practice Outlast Wikis

Wikis die when people stop updating them. That happens quickly in most organizations because the structural barriers (no time, no ownership, no incentive) overwhelm good intentions. Communities of practice have a different dynamic: they're sustained by the members' own professional interest in the domain.

An SRE who participates in the reliability engineering CoP doesn't attend because they were told to. They attend because other experienced SREs are there, discussing problems they're actively working on, sharing solutions they're actually using. The knowledge sharing is intrinsically motivated — which is far more durable than mandate-driven documentation.

When the company migrates from Confluence to Notion, the wiki may be lost. When the company migrates from Zoom to Teams, the meeting infrastructure changes. But the community — the people, the relationships, the shared standards they developed — persists. That's the resilience advantage.

How to Start One — Practically

1
Find the people who already care
Every domain in a tech organization already has informal practitioners who care more than their job requires. The security engineer who shares vulnerability write-ups that nobody asked for. The ML engineer who's building tooling for the whole org. The platform SRE who runs a personal runbook library. Find two or three of them. They're already doing what a CoP formalizes.
2
Start with a recurring format — not a platform
Most failed CoPs start by setting up a Slack channel or Confluence space before there's anything to put there. Start with a recurring event instead: a biweekly 30-minute "share something you learned this sprint" meeting, or a monthly "we each bring one problem we're stuck on" session. The platform comes after the habit, not before it.
3
Keep management sponsorship light
Management support — protected time, visible endorsement, budget for guest speakers or tools — is valuable. Management control is fatal. Communities of practice that become top-down initiatives with attendance requirements and structured agendas lose the voluntary participation that makes them work. The lead should be a senior practitioner who is passionate about the domain, not a manager who was assigned to run it.
4
Build shared artifacts over time
The most lasting CoP output isn't a meeting series — it's the artifacts the community develops: shared design patterns, a standard approach to postmortems, a vetted list of tools for the domain, an onboarding guide for engineers joining the domain. These artifacts are more durable than any single member's presence and more trusted than top-down standards because they were developed through peer consensus.
5
Measure participation, not output
CoPs aren't project teams with deliverables. Measuring them by "number of wiki pages produced" or "number of standards published" misses the point. The right metrics are participation (are more engineers joining over time?), knowledge spread (are practices from the CoP appearing in different teams' work?), and problem velocity (are CoP members solving domain problems faster?).

What Makes a CoP Survive — and What Kills Them

❌ Communities that die within 12 months
• Started with a platform, not a community
• Mandatory attendance — participation is a job requirement
• Run by a manager, not a passionate practitioner
• Agenda driven by management priorities, not member problems
• Evaluated by documents produced, not knowledge spread
• No shared output that members feel ownership of
• Dependent on one champion who eventually leaves
✅ Communities that last for years
• Started with a recurring event and 3 people who cared
• Voluntary attendance — people come because it's useful
• Led by a senior practitioner with domain credibility
• Agenda driven by member problems and learnings
• Evaluated by whether members learn things they use
• Produces shared artifacts that outlast individual members
• Has multiple champions — leadership can be passed on

A company's VP of Engineering decides to formalize the company's informal ML knowledge-sharing practice into an official "ML Community of Practice" with mandatory monthly attendance, a structured agenda, and quarterly deliverables. 📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

A community of practice has three elements: a domain (shared expertise), a community (practitioners across teams), and a practice (shared artifacts and standards developed together). It differs from a team in that membership is voluntary and crosses organizational boundaries — which is why it's more resilient to reorgs and tool migrations than top-down knowledge management initiatives.

Start with people and a recurring event — not a platform. Find two or three engineers who already care more than their job requires, start a recurring format (a biweekly "share something you learned" session is enough), and let the platform emerge from the habit. Platforms without communities produce empty Slack channels and unread Confluence spaces.

Communities of practice die when they're over-formalized: mandatory attendance, structured agendas, management-driven priorities, and deliverable requirements replace the voluntary participation that makes them valuable. Management support should be light sponsorship (protected time, endorsement, resources) — not control. The lead should be a practitioner, not a program manager.
