Lesson 3.4 — Knowledge Sharing & Collaboration

Real-World Spotlight: How GitLab Runs a Fully Documented, Async-First Engineering Organization

GitLab operates with 2,000+ employees across 60+ countries, fully remote, with no offices. Their entire operation runs on one principle: if it isn't written down, it doesn't exist. Here's what that looks like in practice.

🔦
Real-World Spotlight
About GitLab
GitLab is a DevOps platform with 2,000+ employees across 65+ countries — fully remote since founding, with no offices. Their operational handbook, which covers everything from engineering practices to company values to how to run a meeting, is publicly available at about.gitlab.com/handbook. This spotlight draws on their public handbook, engineering blog, and publicly documented practices. GitLab represents the most extensively documented example of async-first, documentation-first engineering culture in the industry.

GitLab has an unusual transparency philosophy: almost everything about how they operate is public. Their company handbook — covering engineering, product, people operations, finance, and everything else — is open source and publicly viewable. This makes them a uniquely studied example of what documentation-first culture looks like at scale.

GitLab's core insight is that remote work doesn't work unless documentation works. And documentation doesn't work unless you build a culture where writing things down is the primary act of work — not a secondary record of it.

Six Practices from GitLab Worth Applying

1
Handbook-first: the written document is the decision

GitLab's most distinctive practice is "handbook-first" — meaning that decisions are made by updating the handbook, not by making a verbal agreement and then documenting it later. A process change isn't official until it's in the handbook. A decision isn't made until it's written down. The document is not the record of the decision; it is the decision.

This inverts the typical sequence in most organizations, where decisions happen in meetings and documentation (if it happens at all) is a trailing record. GitLab writes the documentation first, reviews it asynchronously, and the approval of the document is the act of making the decision. This produces documentation that is always current by definition — because you can't change a process without changing the handbook.

In practice
When a GitLab engineering team wants to change their deployment process, they submit a merge request to the engineering section of the handbook. The MR describes the proposed change, the rationale, and the new procedure. Stakeholders review and comment asynchronously. When the MR is approved and merged, the process change is official. There is no separate meeting where the change was "really decided."
Apply this: For your next process change, write the documentation first. Share it for async review instead of scheduling a meeting. Approval of the document is approval of the change. See how much meeting time it saves — and how much better the documentation is when it was written before anyone tried to implement the process.
2
"Low context" communication — write for someone who wasn't there

GitLab explicitly trains its engineers to write "low context" documentation — documentation that doesn't assume the reader was in the meeting, knows the background, or shares the writer's mental model. Every document should be self-contained enough for someone who joined the company last week to understand and act on it.

This is directly counter to how most documentation is written — usually by people who assume the reader knows what they know. GitLab's low-context standard is enforced during document reviews: if a reviewer has to ask a clarifying question because the document doesn't answer it, that's a documentation gap to fix.

Apply this: Before publishing any important document, have someone who wasn't involved in the relevant meetings read it and note every question they have. Those questions are documentation gaps. The low-context standard — write for someone who wasn't there — eliminates the curse-of-knowledge problem that makes most internal documentation unusable for new team members.
3
Async by default — meetings require a written document to exist

GitLab's operating principle is that most work should be async: decisions made through written MRs and issues, not meetings. When meetings are necessary, they require a pre-read document — an agenda that isn't a bullet list but a written document that states the context, the decision being made, and the options being considered. Attendees review the document before the meeting. The meeting is for deciding, not briefing.

The specific knowledge-sharing benefit: decisions that are made in writing, reviewed in writing, and approved in writing produce documentation as a natural byproduct of the decision process. There's no separate "we should document this" step — the documentation is how the decision was made.

Apply this: For your next decision that would normally be a meeting, write a one-page decision document first. Share it for async review with a 48-hour window. Only call a meeting if async review produces genuine disagreement that requires real-time discussion. Track how many meetings you were able to eliminate.
4
Transparency by default — most information is public internally

GitLab's default is that information is accessible to all employees unless there's a specific reason to restrict it. Engineering decisions, postmortems, salary bands, performance metrics — much of this is either publicly available or available to all GitLab employees by default. Restricted information is the exception, not the rule.

The knowledge-sharing benefit is significant: engineers don't have to navigate permission systems to find information they need. Context that would normally live in a manager's head or in a leadership meeting is documented and accessible. The bar for "does this need to be restricted?" is much higher than at most organizations.

Apply this: Audit your team's information access. Is there information that's restricted by default but that most engineers should reasonably have? Postmortems, architecture decisions, product context, and team retrospectives are all candidates for "default visible." Reducing unnecessary access barriers directly improves knowledge sharing.
5
"Iteration" as a documentation norm — good enough now beats perfect later

GitLab explicitly values documentation that's incomplete but published over documentation that's perfect but unpublished. Their engineering values include "boring solutions" and "iteration" — the idea that a minimal, imperfect version shipped today is more valuable than a comprehensive version shipped in six months.

Applied to documentation: a runbook that covers 80% of cases and is published today is more valuable than a runbook that covers 100% of cases and gets written "when there's time." GitLab engineers are expected to publish drafts, mark sections as TODO, and iterate — not wait until they have something polished before sharing it.

The iteration norm in practice
"This section is incomplete — we'll add rollback steps after the next incident teaches us what they should be. Known gap: this runbook doesn't cover the edge case where both primary nodes fail simultaneously. Please add what you learn here when you encounter it."
Apply this: Normalize "draft — please add to this" documentation on your team. A page with known gaps that engineers actively contribute to is more valuable than a polished page that accurately describes how things worked two years ago and hasn't been touched since.
6
The handbook as onboarding — new engineers read before they ask

GitLab's onboarding process is structured around the handbook: new engineers are expected to spend their first weeks reading handbook sections relevant to their role before asking questions. The expectation is explicit: "if it's in the handbook, look it up before asking." This creates a virtuous cycle — because new engineers rely on the handbook, the handbook needs to be good, so engineers keep it current.

The alternative dynamic — where new engineers are expected to ask senior engineers for everything — creates bus factor problems, interruption costs for senior engineers, and institutional knowledge that lives in people's heads rather than in searchable documentation.

Apply this: Track the questions new engineers ask during their first 30 days. Each question that isn't answered by existing documentation is a documentation gap. Use new-hire onboarding as a systematic documentation audit: every question reveals something that should be written down but isn't.

What You Can Apply — Without Going Fully Remote

GitLab's practices are designed for a fully remote, async-first organization. Most teams won't adopt the entire model. But specific practices translate cleanly regardless of work model.

📝
Write the doc before the meeting
For any decision meeting, write a one-page document first. Share it 24 hours before. Meeting time goes to deciding, not briefing. Most meetings become shorter; some become unnecessary.
🔓
Reduce access restrictions by default
Audit which information is restricted that shouldn't be. Postmortems, architecture docs, and team retrospectives are good candidates for "default visible to all engineers."
🔄
Ship incomplete documentation
Normalize publishing drafts with known gaps marked explicitly. A published 80% runbook beats an unpublished perfect one. Mark sections with "TODO" and trust engineers to contribute.
🎓
Use onboarding as a documentation audit
Every question a new engineer asks that isn't answered in docs is a gap. Track onboarding questions for 30 days and convert them into documentation. New hires are your most accurate signal of documentation quality.

📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

GitLab's most transferable practice is handbook-first: the written document is the decision, not a record of the decision. This produces documentation that is always current by definition — because you can't change a process without changing the handbook — and eliminates the "we'll document it later" gap that kills most knowledge bases.

Two GitLab norms that apply regardless of work model: low-context writing (write for someone who wasn't there, not someone who was) and iteration over perfection (publish incomplete documentation now, improve it over time). Both directly address the documentation quality failures — curse of knowledge and waiting-for-perfect — that affect most engineering organizations.

New engineer onboarding is the most accurate signal of documentation quality. Every question a new hire asks that isn't answered in existing documentation is a documented gap. GitLab uses onboarding systematically as a documentation audit — a practice any team can adopt immediately with no additional tooling.
