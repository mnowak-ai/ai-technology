Lesson 4.2 — Pattern Recognition & Insights

How to Interpret AI Insights Without Being Fooled: Actionable, Expected, and False Positives

The video's three-question framework is the right one. This article makes it a practiced skill — walking you through the same questions against five different alerts until the pattern becomes instinct.

The Three Questions That Protect You

When an AI tool surfaces an insight — an alert, a flagged anomaly, a pattern it detected — your job is to evaluate it. The video's three questions give you a structured way to do that. Used together, they prevent two failure modes: acting on something that doesn't matter, and ignoring something that does.

❓
Is this actionable?
Does this insight point toward something you can actually do? If there's no action available in response to this insight, it's informational at best — not worth escalating. Actionability filters out noise that looks like signal.
🗓️
Does this match expectations?
Does context — a deployment, a scheduled job, an external event, normal usage patterns — explain this observation? Expected deviations are informative but not urgent. Unexpected deviations require investigation.
🔍
Could this be a false positive?
Is there a reason the AI might be wrong here? A metric that's behaving normally but whose baseline was calculated from a period that didn't include this type of event? A threshold miscalibration? Checking for false positive risk before acting saves wasted escalation effort.

These three questions don't tell you what to do. They tell you how to think before you decide. The goal is to never act on an AI insight without having consciously applied all three filters.

🤖 AI INSIGHT — DATADOG
Ticket resolution time anomalous — 340% above weekly baseline
help_desk: avg_resolution_time = 14.2 hrs (7-day baseline: 3.3 hrs) Affected category: ALL categories Time window: Last 48 hours Confidence: 94%
1
Is this actionable?
▾
Resolution time 340% above baseline across ALL categories is significant and almost certainly actionable. The "all categories" detail is important — this isn't a single team's bottleneck. Something affecting the entire help desk is driving this. Potential actions: investigate queue depth across all teams, check for a system issue that's preventing closures, check for a staffing issue, review whether any new process requirement is adding steps to closure. This passes the actionability test — there's clearly something worth investigating and potentially fixing.
2
Does this match expectations?
▾
Check the calendar and recent changes. Were the last 48 hours a holiday period? Did a major incident generate an unusual volume of complex tickets? Did the team lose staff to illness or vacation? Was a new mandatory approval step added to the closure process? If any of these are true, the deviation may be expected — unusual but explainable. If none are true, this deviation is genuinely unexpected and requires deeper investigation. The answer changes the urgency and the investigation path.
3
Could this be a false positive?
▾
Two false positive risk checks: First, is the baseline trustworthy? If last week had unusually fast resolution times (below normal), the 7-day baseline would be artificially low — making this week's normal performance look anomalous. Second, was there a data issue? If tickets that were auto-closed or improperly timestamped inflated or deflated resolution times in the baseline period, the comparison is misleading. For a 94% confidence alert at 340% deviation, false positive risk is low — but it's still worth a quick sanity check of the underlying data.
Verdict after applying three questions
This insight is actionable (all-category elevation needs investigation), requires context check (is there a calendar or staffing explanation?), and has low false positive risk (94% confidence, 340% deviation is substantial). The right response: check whether the last 48 hours had a known explanation first. If yes — document and monitor. If no — escalate immediately to help desk management to investigate why all-category resolution time tripled.

📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

The three questions — Is this actionable? Does this match expectations? Could this be a false positive? — are a sequence, not a checklist. Apply them in order: actionability tells you whether to keep reading, expectations check provides context, and false positive assessment catches AI errors before you act on them.

A "matches expectations" answer doesn't mean ignore it — it means classify the urgency correctly. An expected deviation (post-offsite ticket catchup, Monday morning volume, nightly batch job) may still warrant a documented note or capacity check. What changes is whether you escalate to incident response vs. log and monitor.

False positive risk is highest when the baseline was calculated from an atypical period, when the monitored metric is inherently high-variance, or when a new type of event is occurring that the AI hasn't encountered before. These are structural limitations of pattern matching, not system errors — understanding them helps you calibrate how much weight to give high-confidence alerts in novel situations.
