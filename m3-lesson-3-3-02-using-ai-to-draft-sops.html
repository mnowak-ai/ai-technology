Lesson 3.3 — Creating SOPs & Guides

Using AI to Draft SOPs: What Works, What Doesn't, and What Must Be Human-Reviewed

AI can write an SOP in 30 seconds. The problem is that a 30-second AI SOP can cause a 6-hour incident if the right human doesn't review it first. Here's what you need to know.

AI Is Useful for SOPs — With One Big Condition

AI tools like ChatGPT, Claude, GitHub Copilot, and Confluence AI can write the first draft of an SOP in seconds. They know what an SOP looks like. They can generate the structure, populate the sections, and even write plausible-sounding steps. This is genuinely useful.

The condition: the AI does not know your environment, your team, your tools, or your specific configuration. It knows what SOPs look like in general. It does not know what your production cluster is named, which version of Kubernetes you're running, who your on-call escalation is, or what your team actually does when a deployment fails.

That gap between "plausible-sounding" and "accurate for our environment" is exactly where AI-drafted SOPs fail — and in IT, that failure doesn't show up until an incident.

An AI-drafted SOP is a starting point that sounds finished. That's what makes it dangerous if you treat it as finished.

What AI Does Well

Let's be fair. AI tools are genuinely helpful for SOP drafting in specific ways.

✓ What AI Does Well
Generates complete SOP structure and section headers instantly
Writes clear, grammatically correct placeholder text for each section
Suggests standard steps for common procedures (deployments, incident response)
Identifies sections you might have forgotten to include
Rewrites vague or passive steps into clearer, active-voice versions
Formats numbered steps and checklists consistently
✗ What AI Gets Wrong
Environment-specific details (cluster names, namespaces, URLs)
Version-specific commands that differ between your tools and the defaults
Your team's actual escalation paths and contacts
Edge cases and failure modes your team has actually encountered
Steps that were learned the hard way after incidents
Internal tool names, aliases, and scripts unique to your org

The Five Errors AI Makes Most Often in SOPs

These aren't random mistakes — they're patterns. AI makes these same errors predictably, which means you can learn to spot them fast.

1
Generic Environment Details

AI fills in environment details with plausible-sounding placeholders. These look real but aren't. An engineer following the SOP runs a command against a system that doesn't exist — or worse, against the wrong environment.

❌ What AI Writes
kubectl get pods -n production
✅ What It Should Say (for your team)
kubectl get pods -n ai-prod --context prod-us-east-1-cluster

The AI doesn't know your namespace is ai-prod, not production. It doesn't know your context name. Every environment-specific detail must be verified and replaced.

2
Missing Failure Handling

AI writes the happy path. It describes what to do when everything works. It almost never includes what to do when a step fails — which is precisely the information an engineer needs most during an incident.

❌ What AI Writes
5. Run the database migration script.
6. Verify the migration completed successfully.
7. Bring the service back online.
✅ What It Should Include
5. Run: ./run_migration.sh --env prod
If exit code ≠ 0: DO NOT proceed. Run ./rollback_migration.sh immediately. Page #db-oncall.
6. Verify: run ./check_migration_status.sh. Confirm "Migration: COMPLETE" in output.
If status shows PARTIAL or ERROR: stop and page #db-oncall before proceeding.
3
Outdated or Version-Wrong Commands

AI training data includes documentation from many versions of many tools. The command it generates may be correct for an older or newer version than what your team uses. This is especially common with Kubernetes, Terraform, and cloud CLIs, which change frequently.

❌ What AI Might Write (for an older kubectl version)
kubectl run --generator=deployment/apps.v1beta1 ...
✅ Verify Against Your Actual Tool Version
Run: kubectl version --client — then confirm the command matches your version's syntax. Never paste AI-generated commands into production without running them in a non-production environment first.
4
Generic Escalation Paths

AI writes escalation sections that sound correct but refer to generic roles, not real people or real channels. "Escalate to the infrastructure team" is useless at 3am. "Page the infrastructure on-call via PagerDuty policy infra-prod-oncall and post in #infra-alerts" is actionable.

❌ What AI Writes
If the issue cannot be resolved within the expected timeframe, escalate to the appropriate engineering team for further investigation.
✅ What It Should Say
If root cause is not identified within 30 minutes: page ml-platform-oncall in PagerDuty. Post in #ai-oncall with: alert name, what you've checked, current metrics. If error rate exceeds 20% for 10+ minutes: declare P1 in #incident-response.
5
Missing Team-Specific Context

Your team has learned things the hard way. There are steps in your procedures that exist because of specific past incidents. AI doesn't know about the incident in Q3 where skipping the cache drain caused a 4-hour recovery. It won't include the warning. Your team's institutional knowledge doesn't make it into an AI-drafted SOP unless a human adds it.

✅ What You Must Add
⚠️ Important: Always drain the cache before running this step. In October 2023, a deployment without cache drain caused a 4-hour data inconsistency recovery. See incident report INC-2023-0847.
🎯 Apply It
What is the most critical problem with this AI-drafted SOP step?

An AI tool generated this step for an incident response SOP. Read it carefully and identify the highest-risk issue.

Step 4: Roll back the model deployment to the previous version.
Run: ./deploy.sh --model inference-api --version previous --rollback true
Monitor the service health dashboard to confirm the rollback completes successfully.

The Non-Negotiable Human Review Checklist

Before any AI-drafted SOP ships to your team, a human with direct knowledge of the procedure must review these items. Check them all.

🔴 Must Fix Before This SOP Ships
Every environment name, namespace, cluster name, and URL has been verified against your actual infrastructure
Every command has been tested in a non-production environment and confirmed to work with your current tool versions
Escalation paths name specific PagerDuty policies, Slack channels, or people — not generic roles
Every step that can fail has a "what to do if this fails" instruction
Team-specific context — lessons learned from past incidents — has been added where relevant
🟡 Check When Relevant
Version numbers are specified where commands are version-dependent
Success criteria are specific and observable (HTTP 200, specific log output) not vague ("confirm the service is healthy")
The scope accurately reflects which environments, systems, and roles this applies to
🧠 Quick Check
Your team uses AI to draft an SOP for responding to a database failover event. The AI's draft looks professional, covers all seven SOP sections, and the steps seem logical. A junior engineer reviews it and says it looks good. Should the team use it?
📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

AI is great for generating SOP structure and placeholder content quickly. It is not reliable for environment-specific details, commands, escalation paths, or team-specific context — and these are precisely the parts that matter most when an engineer is following the SOP under pressure.

The five errors AI makes most often in SOPs: generic environment details, missing failure handling, outdated or version-wrong commands, generic escalation paths, and missing team-specific context from past incidents. Every AI-drafted SOP should be checked for all five.

"The AI wrote it" is not a defense in a postmortem. The moment a human approves an SOP, that human owns its accuracy. AI-drafted SOPs for critical production procedures must be reviewed by someone who can verify commands in a real environment — not just someone who can check if it reads well.
