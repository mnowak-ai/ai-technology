Lesson 5.3 — Professional Responsibility

Simulation: You Caught Something the AI Missed — Now What?

You're a junior data analyst on a team that's about to deploy an AI-assisted report to a major client. During your final review, you find something that doesn't look right. Walk through every decision, from discovery to resolution.

This simulation follows a single scenario through six consecutive decision points. Each choice shapes what comes next. Read carefully — the decisions build on each other, and the goal is to navigate from discovery to resolution as professionally as possible.

Your role: Junior data analyst, two months into your first tech job. Your team uses an AI tool to generate market analysis reports for enterprise clients. You've been asked to do a final quality check before tomorrow's client presentation.

Step 1 of 6
You find something odd in the report
During your review, you notice the AI generated a statistic: "Market penetration in the healthcare sector reached 73% in Q3 2024, a 19% increase from Q2." You try to find a source for this number. You can't. You're not certain it's wrong — but you can't verify it, and the client is a major healthcare company that will make decisions based on this data.
What do you do?
Stop the review. Spend the next 20 minutes searching for any credible source for this statistic before deciding what to do next.
Keep going — it\'s a specific-looking number, so it\'s probably based on real data. Complete the review and flag it only if you find something else suspicious.
Add "approximately" before the number and keep reviewing — that makes it less definitive without stopping the process.
You've searched — and found nothing
You spent 20 minutes searching. You checked three industry databases, two government health statistics sites, and the AI tool's output history. The 73% figure appears nowhere. It's either from a source you don't have access to — or the AI generated it. You still have 45 minutes before the report needs to be sent for final formatting.
What do you do now?
Tell your manager immediately. Explain what you found, what you searched, and that you couldn\'t verify the statistic. Ask how they want to proceed with 45 minutes to deadline.
Remove the statistic yourself and replace the section with language like "market data pending" — that keeps the report moving without the unverified figure.
Keep searching for another 30 minutes before deciding — you might find the source with more time.
You told your manager. They're annoyed — and asking you to just send it.
Your manager's response: "We've used this AI tool for months, it's never been wrong before. The client is expecting this tomorrow. Just leave the stat in — if they ask, we'll look it up then." You understand the deadline pressure. You also know the stat can't be verified.
What do you do?
Your manager said to leave it in, so you leave it in. They have more experience and authority than you, so defer to their judgment.
Respectfully push back: "I hear you on the deadline. Could we remove the statistic or flag it as unconfirmed? That lets us send on time without a claim we can\'t stand behind if the client asks." Then offer to make the edit yourself.
Send the report but add a footnote: "Some statistics in this report are AI-generated and may require independent verification." That gives the client fair warning.
Your manager accepts the counter-proposal. You remove the stat. Report goes out.
The report was sent with the statistic removed. Your manager says "good catch" and moves on. Three days later, in the client debrief, the client asks why the healthcare penetration data was missing from the report — they were specifically looking for that benchmark. Your manager turns to you.
How do you explain what happened?
Explain clearly: "The AI tool generated a market penetration figure that we couldn\'t verify against any source within our timeline, so we removed it rather than include an unverified claim. We can research the right source and send you a verified figure by [specific date]."
Vaguely explain that the data needed additional verification and you\'ll follow up. Keep it brief to avoid getting into the details.
Say you couldn\'t find the right data source in time and will send it separately. Don\'t mention the AI tool — that might make them question the whole report.
The client is satisfied. Back at the office, you're thinking about the AI tool.
You resolved this specific problem — but the AI tool is still being used for client reports. You don't know if this is a one-off hallucination or a pattern. You're junior. Nobody has asked you to evaluate the tool.
What's the right professional move here?
Document what happened in a brief internal note. Mention it to your manager in your next 1:1 as something worth monitoring: "That stat issue last week made me wonder if we should add a verification step for AI-generated statistics before they go to clients."
Let it go — you caught it this time, the client is satisfied, and raising it again will make you look like you\'re pushing an agenda.
Request a formal audit of all previous reports that used the AI tool — this might be a systemic problem.
Your manager responds to your 1:1 note. They ask you to write up a one-page process recommendation.
Your manager was receptive. They want you to write a brief internal recommendation for how the team should handle AI-generated statistics in client reports going forward. This is your chance to turn a single incident into a team-level improvement.
What does a strong one-page process recommendation include?
Brief description of the incident and why it matters, a specific verification step (all statistics from AI tools must be sourced before client submission), a clear workflow integration (final reviewer checks AI-sourced data against linked external source), and a documentation requirement (note verified sources in the report file).
A recommendation to ban the AI tool from client-facing work until its accuracy can be independently validated.
A disclosure policy requiring all reports to note when AI was used in research, so clients understand the provenance of the data.

You navigated from discovery through escalation, client communication, and process improvement — the full professional arc of catching something the AI missed. The key at every step: act on what you know, communicate clearly, and turn individual incidents into better systems.
📌 What the Simulation Teaches

The Pattern Across All Six Steps

Every step had a "keep going and hope" option. That option was available at every decision point — and wrong at every one. Professional responsibility means stopping when something is wrong, not assuming it will be fine.

Escalating to your manager is not the end of your responsibility. When your manager pushed back, the right response was a professional counter-proposal — not compliance with a directive you believed was wrong, and not going over their head immediately.

Individual incident → team-level process improvement is the full professional arc. Catching the problem was step one. Communicating about it, resolving it honestly with the client, and turning it into a better process — that's the complete response.
