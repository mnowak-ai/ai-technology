Lesson 2.1 · Using AI to Diagnose Problems

The Difference Between Monitoring, Alerting, and Diagnosing

Three words used interchangeably in tech environments — but they refer to three distinct activities with different goals, different tools, and different roles for both AI and the human analyst. Confusing them is one of the most common sources of incomplete incident response.

Three Distinct Activities, Often Conflated

In many technical conversations, "monitoring" is used as a catch-all for the entire activity of keeping an eye on systems and responding when something goes wrong. But this conflation hides three activities that are fundamentally different in purpose, method, and outcome.

Monitoring is continuous observation — watching system metrics, collecting data, maintaining visibility. Alerting is threshold notification — automatically signaling when a metric crosses a defined boundary. Diagnosing is investigative analysis — determining why a problem exists and what would actually fix it.

You can have excellent monitoring and alerting, and still do poor diagnosing. You can have perfect diagnostic skills and be blind to problems because monitoring is inadequate. They're complementary, not interchangeable.

Monitoring shows you the body of water. Alerting tells you when the water level gets too high. Diagnosing figures out why it's rising and where it's coming from. All three are necessary. None substitutes for the others.

The Three Pillars Defined

👁️
Monitoring
"What is the system doing right now?"
Continuous collection of system metrics — CPU usage, memory, disk I/O, network throughput, response times, error rates. Monitoring produces data. It doesn't interpret it or act on it. The goal is visibility: maintaining an accurate, real-time picture of system state.
🔔
Alerting
"Is something outside normal thresholds?"
Automated notification when monitored metrics cross predefined thresholds. Alerting is rules-based: if CPU > 90%, trigger alert. It answers "something crossed a line" but not "why" or "what to do about it." The quality of alerting depends entirely on how well thresholds are configured.
🔬
Diagnosing
"Why is this happening and what would fix it?"
Investigative analysis using monitoring data, log evidence, alert context, and domain knowledge to identify root cause. Diagnosis is what bridges the gap between "an alert fired" and "we know what to do." It requires judgment, not just data. This is where both AI tools and human analysts do their most important work.

The most common failure point in this chain is between alerting and diagnosing. An alert fires, a human acknowledges it, applies a quick fix — and the incident is marked resolved before diagnosis ever happens. Alert acknowledgment is not diagnosis. Fix application is not diagnosis. Diagnosis is the investigative step that determines root cause — and it cannot be skipped if you want permanent resolution.

See All Three in Action

Select a scenario to see how monitoring, alerting, and diagnosing operate as distinct activities — and where AI contributes to each:

👁️ Monitoring — What the system is reporting
The monitoring platform has been collecting App-Server-07 memory metrics every 60 seconds for the past 6 hours. Current reading: 87% memory utilization (34.8GB / 40GB). The trend graph shows a steady, gradual increase from 62% six hours ago.
🤖 AI role: Collects and aggregates metrics continuously, maintains trend data, flags deviation from baseline. No human involvement needed for data collection at this stage.
🔔 Alerting — What threshold was crossed
Alert fires: "App-Server-07 memory utilization exceeded 85% threshold." The alert includes: current value (87%), duration above threshold (14 minutes), trend direction (rising), and alert priority (Warning).
🤖 AI role: Evaluates metric against configured threshold, triggers notification, includes contextual data. Rule-based — fires when condition is met.
👤 Human receives the alert. At this point, all the analyst knows is that a threshold was crossed. They don't know why memory is high, whether it's a problem, or what to do about it. Alerting ends here — diagnosing begins.
🔬 Diagnosing — Finding the root cause
AI diagnostic analysis: "Gradual memory increase matches memory leak pattern (67% confidence). Possible cause: application process with increasing memory allocation over time. Recommend: identify process with highest memory growth rate." The analyst runs the process analysis, identifies a scheduled analytics job running for 10 hours (normally completes in 4). Data team confirms the job is stuck on an oversized dataset.
🤖 AI role: Pattern-matches trend to historical leak signatures, suggests investigation direction, accelerates evidence gathering.
👤 Human role: Evaluates AI suggestion against operational context (is there a scheduled job that could explain this?), confirms actual cause with data team, decides appropriate action (cancel and restart the job vs. wait for it to complete). Applies organizational knowledge AI doesn't have.
👁️ Monitoring — What the system is reporting
API response time monitoring shows customer-facing endpoints averaging 4,200ms response time over the past 8 minutes. Normal baseline is 180ms. Error rate has risen to 8.4%. Database query time metrics show query execution averaging 3,800ms (normal: 40ms).
🤖 AI role: Collects response time, error rate, and database metrics simultaneously across all endpoints. No human needed for data collection.
🔔 Alerting — What threshold was crossed
Two alerts fire simultaneously: "API response time > 2,000ms threshold" and "Database query time > 500ms threshold." The coincident timing of both alerts is itself a signal — two thresholds crossing at the same moment often indicates a shared cause.
🤖 AI role: Fires both alerts, notes coincident timing, flags correlation for diagnostic review. Alert system itself doesn't diagnose — it reports what crossed a line.
🔬 Diagnosing — Finding the root cause
AI analysis: "Simultaneous API and database latency with high correlation (0.97). Pattern consistent with database resource contention. Possible cause: lock contention or resource exhaustion. Confidence: 76%." Analyst checks active database sessions — finds 847 concurrent connections (normal peak: ~200). Traces to a reporting service that launched at the same time as the latency onset, opening connections without closing them properly (connection leak).
👤 Human role: Confirms AI's resource contention hypothesis using active session data. Identifies specific cause (connection leak in reporting service) that AI flagged as "resource exhaustion" but couldn't specify. Restarts the reporting service — connection count drops, performance restores within 2 minutes.
👁️ Monitoring — What the system is reporting
Network throughput monitoring shows a sudden drop from 950Mbps to near zero on the primary uplink at 14:23:07. Packet loss rate: 99.2%. All servers behind the affected uplink show simultaneous loss of external connectivity.
🤖 AI role: Continuous network metric collection immediately captures the drop. The simultaneity across all servers (vs. one server having a problem) is captured in the monitoring data and is the first diagnostic clue.
🔔 Alerting — What threshold was crossed
Critical alert: "Primary uplink throughput at 0% — complete connectivity loss." Secondary alert: "All servers in Zone-A: external connectivity failed." The alerts fire within 4 seconds of the actual event — rapid detection is a core value of well-configured alerting.
🤖 AI role: Near-instantaneous alert generation. The 4-second lag between event and alert is valuable — manual detection could take minutes in a large environment.
🔬 Diagnosing — Finding the root cause
AI analysis: "Complete uplink failure — possible causes: physical cable failure, ISP outage, or switch/router failure. No historical pattern match available (novel event). Manual investigation required." Analyst checks: ISP status page (no reported outage), physical cable (intact), switch logs (switch rebooted at 14:23:05 — 2 seconds before the connectivity loss). Switch reboot log shows power supply failure triggering a safety reboot.
👤 Human role: Low AI confidence ("novel event") triggers full manual investigation. Analyst systematically eliminates possible causes. Finds the switch power supply failure in the switch's own logs — a data source AI platform may not have had access to. Orders replacement power supply, activates secondary switch for failover. This is a case where AI correctly identified it couldn't help much and human investigation was the primary diagnostic method.

AI is most reliable in monitoring and alerting — rule-based activities where accuracy is high and the failure modes are predictable. AI is least reliable in diagnosing — the activity that requires contextual judgment, organizational knowledge, and the ability to recognize when a situation doesn't match any historical pattern. That's precisely why diagnosis is the activity that most requires a skilled human analyst — not to replace AI, but to evaluate and act on what AI surfaces.

📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

Monitoring (what is the system doing?), alerting (has a threshold been crossed?), and diagnosing (why is this happening?) are three distinct activities. Monitoring and alerting tell you that a problem exists and approximately where. Diagnosing determines why it exists and what would actually resolve it permanently. All three are necessary; none substitutes for the others.

AI is highly reliable for monitoring and alerting — rule-based activities where data collection and threshold evaluation are consistent and predictable. AI is moderately reliable for diagnosing — the activity most dependent on contextual judgment and organizational knowledge. This is why diagnosing is the activity that most requires skilled human oversight of AI outputs.

The most common failure in incident response is treating alert acknowledgment as the end of the process rather than the beginning of diagnosis. Excellent monitoring and alerting that isn't followed by thorough diagnosis produces teams that repeatedly respond to the same incidents — because symptoms are being addressed but root causes are not. Lesson 2.1 is fundamentally about building the diagnostic capability that monitoring and alerting make possible but cannot replace.
