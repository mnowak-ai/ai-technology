Lesson 1.2 · How AI Works (Conceptually)

Machine Learning Without the Math: How Models Are Built, Trained, and Deployed in Tech Environments

You don't need to know the math to work effectively with AI tools. But you do need the vocabulary and the mental models — because these concepts shape every capability and limitation you'll encounter in the field.

Why You Need This Vocabulary

Machine learning vocabulary shows up in vendor documentation, incident reports, team discussions, job postings, and product meetings. When a security tool's documentation says it uses "unsupervised clustering," or a monitoring vendor describes their model as "achieving 94% accuracy on test data," you need to know what those phrases actually mean — not just to sound informed, but to make good decisions about when to trust the output.

The vocabulary is not trivia. It's operational knowledge. Understanding the difference between training and inference, or why "94% accuracy" might be misleading for your use case, directly determines whether you use AI tools well or badly.

Two Fundamentally Different Ways AI Learns

All machine learning involves learning patterns from data — but the nature of the learning differs significantly based on what kind of data is available. The most important distinction to understand:

Supervised Learning
Learns from labeled examples

The model is trained on data where each example has a known answer (the "label"). The model learns to predict the label for new examples it hasn't seen. This requires significant labeling effort upfront — someone has to label all that training data.

In practice: A spam filter trained on millions of emails labeled "spam" or "not spam." A code review tool trained on code labeled "vulnerable" or "safe." An anomaly detector trained on server logs labeled "normal" or "incident."

Security: Malware classifier — trained on files labeled as malware or clean
DevOps: Incident predictor — trained on metrics labeled as pre-incident or normal
Dev: Bug detector — trained on code labeled as containing bugs or not
Unsupervised Learning
Discovers structure without labels

The model finds patterns and structure in data without being told what to look for. It clusters similar things together or detects things that don't fit the general pattern. No labeling required — but the model's findings require human interpretation.

In practice: A ticket clustering tool that groups similar support requests without being told what the categories are. An anomaly detector that flags deviations from learned baseline behavior without needing labeled incident examples.

IT Support: Ticket clustering — groups similar issues without predefined categories
Security: Behavior anomaly detection — flags unusual patterns without labeled incidents
DevOps: Log clustering — groups similar log events for faster triage

The critical difference for your work: supervised models tell you what something is (this is spam, this is malware, this is a bug). Unsupervised models tell you something looks different from the rest. Both require human interpretation, but in different ways.

Training vs. Inference — Two Completely Different Activities

The single most important conceptual distinction in machine learning for practical use:

Training vs. Inference — What's Actually Happening
🏋️ Training (happens once)
  • Massive amounts of labeled data are fed to the model
  • The model's internal parameters adjust to reduce prediction errors
  • This process runs for hours, days, or weeks on specialized hardware
  • At the end, parameters are frozen — the model "knows" what it learned
  • Training is expensive; inference is cheap
→
⚡ Inference (happens every time you use it)
  • A new input (your prompt, your code, your log) is run through the frozen model
  • The model generates a prediction based on its learned parameters
  • This happens in milliseconds to seconds
  • The model does NOT update or learn from this interaction
  • Same input can produce slightly different outputs each time
Why this matters for you: Every time you use an AI tool — submitting a prompt, triggering a code review, watching an anomaly alert — you are using inference. The model is not learning from your interaction. It is applying what it learned during training. This means it cannot adapt to your specific environment, your team's conventions, or events that happened after training. That knowledge has to come from you.

📌 Article Summary

What to Carry Forward

Supervised learning requires labeled training examples and tells you what something is. Unsupervised learning discovers patterns without labels and tells you something looks different. Both require human interpretation, in different ways.

Training happens once — the model's parameters are frozen after it. Inference happens every time you use the tool. The model does NOT learn from your interactions. Context that the model lacks has to come from you, explicitly.

"Model accuracy" is not a simple number. It depends on what dataset accuracy was measured on, how the task was defined, whether false positives and false negatives are counted differently, and whether the test data resembles your actual environment.

The ML vocabulary — training, inference, supervised, unsupervised, false positive, false negative, overfitting, model drift — is operational knowledge. It determines whether you evaluate AI tools critically or accept vendor claims at face value.
