Lesson 3.2 · Summarizing Technical Information

How to Summarize a Technical Incident: A Step-by-Step Framework with Real Examples

A raw incident log is not a summary. Turning one into the other is a skill — knowing what to keep, what to cut, how to structure it, and how to write the "so what" that makes it useful to the people who receive it.

Every time a system goes down, a deployment fails, or an AI tool misfires in production, someone has to write the incident summary. Not the raw log — the summary. The document that tells engineering leadership what happened, tells the on-call team what to watch for, and tells the postmortem committee what to change so it doesn't happen again.

Most incident summaries are written the same way the incident was experienced: chronologically, with every detail, in the heat of the moment. The result is a document that is technically accurate and nearly impossible to act on. A good incident summary is not a transcript of what happened. It is a structured argument about what happened and what it means.

The raw incident log is evidence. The incident summary is the case you build from that evidence. They are two different documents with two different jobs.

The Five-Step Framework

These steps apply whether you are summarizing a P1 outage, a failed deployment, a security event, or an AI model producing bad outputs in production. Do not write a single word of the summary until you have completed steps 1 through 3.

1
Before you write
Identify the audience and their decision
Every incident summary serves someone who will do something with it. The on-call engineer reading the summary at 3am needs different information than the VP of Engineering reviewing it Monday morning, or the SRE team writing a postmortem, or the customer success team drafting communications. Before writing a single word, name your reader and the specific action your summary needs to enable.
Ask yourself first
Who reads this? What will they do with it? What decision does this need to inform? If I can't answer these questions, I don't yet know what to summarize.
2
Before you write
Read the full log and annotate — don't write yet
Read the complete incident timeline before writing anything. As you read, mark three categories only: (A) events that changed the trajectory — the trigger, the escalation, the intervention, the resolution; (B) the exact moment the incident began and ended; (C) contributing factors. Everything else — status updates, duplicate alerts, routine acknowledgments — is noise.
Annotation categories
🔴 Trajectory-changing events → must include
🟡 Contributing context → include if relevant to audience
⚪ Status noise / duplicates → omit
3
Before you write
Identify the "so what" before you write the "what"
The "so what" is the technical or business significance of the incident — why this summary needs to exist. Was it a config drift that's probably affecting other services? A silent failure mode in an AI pipeline that nobody has been monitoring? A dependency that's been a latent risk for months? You must know the significance before writing the narrative, or the summary drifts into description instead of insight.
Complete this before writing
"This incident matters because _____, and the reader needs to know this so that they can _____."
4
Writing
Write in four required sections
Every incident summary has four sections regardless of length. Each does one job. Do not combine them or skip them.
The four required sections
1. Incident identification — What, when, scope of impact. Two to three sentences max. No analysis yet.

2. Timeline of key events — What happened, in order, at the detail level your audience needs. Trajectory-changing events only. No noise.

3. Resolution and current status — What fixed it, who did it, when. Current system state.

4. Root cause and recommended action — The "so what." What this incident reveals, what systemic risk it represents, and what specific action is recommended. This section is why the summary exists.
5
After writing
Cut everything that doesn't serve the audience's decision
After a full draft, read it from the perspective of your specific audience making their specific decision. Every sentence that doesn't move that decision gets cut or moved to an appendix. This is where most incident summaries go from exhausting to useful.
The cut test
For each sentence: "If the reader didn't have this, would they make a different decision?" If no — cut it.

❌ Weak Summary
On Tuesday at approximately 2pm, the RecoEngine experienced elevated latency and began returning null results for a significant portion of product searches. The on-call engineer investigated and identified a connection issue with the feature store. A hotfix was deployed and the system was restored within approximately 47 minutes. ❌Written for no one in particular. Useless to every stakeholder.
✅ Strong Summary — For Engineering Leadership & Postmortem Committee
INCIDENT SUMMARY — P1 · RecoEngine Null Response Spike Audience: Engineering Leadership, Postmortem Committee Impact: 34% of product search queries returned null for 47 minutes (14:03–14:50). Revenue impact under assessment by analytics team. WHAT HAPPENED At 13:56, the Feature Store team deployed a schema change that renamed the column `user_session_id` to `session_uuid`. This change was not communicated to downstream teams. At 14:03, RecoEngine — which had a hardcoded reference to the old column name — began returning null results for any query that hit the renamed column. Auto-scaling did not help because the problem was a bad data reference, not a capacity issue. The Feature Store team identified the schema change at 14:22 after being pinged. A hotfix updating the column reference in RecoEngine config was deployed and validated by 14:50. THREE CONTRIBUTING FAILURES 1. No change communication protocol: Feature Store deployments do not currently require notification to downstream model owners before schema changes go to production. 2. Inaccurate status page: Feature Store health dashboard showed "green" during the outage because monitoring only checks service availability, not schema integrity. 3. Hardcoded configuration: RecoEngine referenced the feature column name in static config rather than a schema-aware abstraction — making any column rename a silent breaking change. ROOT CAUSE & RECOMMENDED ACTIONS This is the second time in 90 days that an undisclosed Feature Store schema change has broken a downstream ML model (previous incident: Oct 14). This is a systemic coordination failure, not a one-off deployment error. Recommended for committee action: • Immediate: Mandatory downstream notification before any Feature Store schema change touches production • 30 days: Feature Store schema changes require approval from all registered downstream model owners (RecoEngine, PersonalizationEngine, SearchRanker) • 30 days: Status page monitoring updated to include schema validation checks, not just service availability • 60 days: Audit all ML model configs for hardcoded feature column references; migrate to schema registry pattern
✅Written for a specific audience making a specific decision (systemic changes)
✅Three contributing failures named explicitly — each addressable independently
✅The 90-day pattern is the headline finding — this is the "so what" that justifies systemic action, not a one-off fix
✅Specific recommended actions with owners and timeframes the committee can vote on
🤖 AI Dimension
When AI Summarizes Incidents Involving AI Systems
AI incident summarization tools — whether Jira's AI, Confluence AI, or custom LLM pipelines — are increasingly used to auto-draft postmortem summaries from incident timelines. When the incident itself involved an AI system misbehaving, a specific risk emerges: AI summarization tools tend to soften technical failure details. They describe "a data issue" rather than naming the specific model config that hardcoded a schema reference it shouldn't have. They say "monitoring gaps" rather than "the status page was showing green because no one configured it to check schema integrity." Vague summaries of AI failures protect the status quo. Specific ones enable the systemic fixes that prevent the next incident.

📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

A technical incident summary is a structured argument, not a transcript. The five-step framework: identify audience and decision, read and annotate the log, identify the "so what" before writing the "what," write in four sections (identification, timeline, resolution, root cause/action), then cut everything that doesn't serve the decision.

The root cause and recommended action section is why the summary exists. "Review our processes" is not a recommended action — it's a platitude. Recommended actions must be specific, name an owner, and have a timeframe. A summary without these has not finished its job.

When AI tools contribute to an incident and are also used to summarize it, verify that the specific technical failure is named and the scope of ongoing risk is quantified. AI-generated incident summaries tend to soften technical specifics — vague summaries of AI failures leave the underlying systemic risk unaddressed.
