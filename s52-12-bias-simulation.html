A healthcare AI recommends a pain management protocol for a patient.
🤖 AI Recommendation
Based on the patient's reported pain level of 7/10 and diagnostic imaging results, the recommended protocol is ibuprofen 400mg three times daily for 14 days, followed by a reassessment. Physical therapy referral is not indicated at this stage. Surgical consultation is not warranted given the current presentation.
Research has documented that AI pain assessment tools trained on historical medical data often underestimate pain in Black patients. What's the most important thing to check before implementing this recommendation?
Whether the recommended ibuprofen dosage follows standard clinical guidelines
Whether the AI system has documented accuracy disparities for this patient's demographic group, given research on pain assessment bias in AI tools
Whether physical therapy should have been recommended before medication
A developer asks an AI coding assistant about a security vulnerability they've discovered.
🤖 AI Response
This vulnerability type was patched in CVE-2023-1847 through the March 2023 security update. The NIST National Vulnerability Database rates this class of vulnerability as CVSS 7.8 (High). The standard remediation is to update to version 4.2.1 or later, which includes the security patch. This was the most significant vulnerability of this type reported in 2023.
The developer's company is running version 4.5.0, which is newer than 4.2.1. What concern should the developer flag about this AI response before closing the issue?
The AI\'s training cutoff means its vulnerability information could be outdated — the developer should verify against the current CVE database and current security advisories
The CVSS score of 7.8 seems too high — the developer should question whether that rating is correct
Nothing — the company is already running a newer version than the recommended patch, so the issue is resolved
An AI hiring tool scores two résumés. Both candidates have identical experience and education.
🤖 AI Scores
Candidate A (Emily Chen): Score 87/100 — Strong technical background, leadership experience in student government, demonstrated communication skills through debate team. Recommended for interview.

Candidate B (Jamal Washington): Score 71/100 — Technical background present, leadership activities noted. Communication and soft skills less evident from résumé presentation. Interview at recruiter's discretion.
Both candidates have identical qualifications. The AI scored them 16 points apart. What is the most likely explanation and the right response?
Candidate A\'s activities are more relevant to the role than Candidate B\'s, which explains the score difference
The score gap on identical qualifications is a potential bias signal — likely reflecting name-based or language-style bias in the model. Both candidates should interview, and the finding should be escalated for audit.
Accept the AI score as an objective assessment — the tool was designed to find differences a human recruiter might miss
A content team uses an AI image generator to create stock photos of "doctors and patients" for a hospital website.
🤖 Generated Image Set (described)
The AI generates 12 images. In all 12: the doctor is a white man in his 40s-50s. The patient in 10 of 12 images is white. In 2 images, the patient is Asian. No Black, Latino, or Indigenous patients appear in any image. The doctor is male in all 12 images.
The hospital serves a patient population that is 40% Black, 25% Latino, and 15% white. What type of bias is this, and what should the team do?
Confirmation bias — the model reproduces the demographic patterns it saw most in training (white male doctors, white patients). For this hospital\'s patient population, these images are actively misrepresentative and should be regenerated with explicit diversity guidance.
A hallucination — the AI invented doctors that don\'t exist
Use stock image libraries instead — AI-generated images always have this problem
A loan company's AI system is reviewed after 18 months of operation.
📊 Audit Finding
Overall loan approval rate: 68%. Model accuracy on predicting default: 91%. False positive rate (incorrectly flagging creditworthy applicants as high risk): 4.2% overall. By demographic group: White applicants 2.1% false positive rate. Black applicants 8.7% false positive rate. Latino applicants 7.3% false positive rate. The model was trained on 12 years of the company's historical loan data.
The model has 91% accuracy and a 4.2% overall false positive rate, which the team considers acceptable. What's the critical finding they're overlooking?
The model needs recalibration — 91% accuracy isn\'t high enough for loan decisions
The disaggregated false positive rates reveal a critical disparate impact problem — Black applicants face a false positive rate 4x higher than white applicants, which likely constitutes illegal discrimination under fair lending law. The overall numbers hide this finding.
The model needs more training data — 12 years isn\'t enough for a financial application

📌 Article Summary

The Patterns Across All Five Scenarios

Aggregate metrics consistently hide demographic disparities. Scenario 5's 91% accuracy looked fine until you disaggregated the false positive rates by demographic group. The pattern appears across all five scenarios: the top-line number is not the relevant number.

Historical data carries historical bias. Scenarios 3 and 5 both involve systems trained on historical data that reflects past discrimination — in hiring and in lending. The model doesn't know the history behind the pattern; it just learns to replicate it.

The right response to a bias signal is always escalation, not acceptance. In every scenario where a bias concern was flagged, the correct answer involved flagging the finding and not acting on the biased output. "The AI said so" is never sufficient justification when a bias signal has been identified.
