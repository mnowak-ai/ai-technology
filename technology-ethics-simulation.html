Scenario 1 of 6⬤ Easier

The AI That Remembers Everything

📘 Key Term — Data Privacy: Data privacy means people have the right to control who sees their personal information — like their name, passwords, location, or messages. When an AI tool stores or shares that information without permission, it becomes a security risk.

Meridian Tech has rolled out an AI assistant called HelperBot that employees use to answer questions, draft emails, and look up company files. It's connected to the company's internal network and can read any file it has been given access to.

A new employee named Jordan uses HelperBot to help write a proposal. While using it, Jordan notices the AI's response includes details from a confidential contract — one Jordan was never supposed to see. Jordan didn't ask for it. The AI just pulled it in because it was relevant.

What should Jordan do?

Scenario 2 of 6⬤ Medium

The AI That Opens the Door

📘 Key Term — Network Security: Network security means protecting a company's computers, servers, and data from being accessed by people who shouldn't have access. AI tools that connect to the internet or store data in the cloud can create new openings — called vulnerabilities — that hackers might use to get in.

Meridian Tech's engineering team has integrated a popular third-party AI coding assistant into their development environment. The AI helps developers write code faster by suggesting lines of code and finding bugs.

During a security review, you discover that the AI coding tool sends snippets of the company's source code to an external server to generate its suggestions. The company that makes the tool says the data is "anonymized and deleted after 30 days."

However, Meridian's source code contains proprietary algorithms — the secret logic that makes their products valuable. No one signed off on sharing this externally when the tool was installed. What do you recommend?

Scenario 3 of 6⬤ Medium

The Hiring AI With a Pattern

📘 Key Term — Algorithmic Bias: Algorithmic bias happens when an AI tool makes decisions that unfairly favor or disadvantage certain groups of people. This often happens because the AI was trained on historical data that already contained bias — so it learned to repeat those unfair patterns automatically.

Meridian Tech has been using an AI tool to screen job applications for the past six months. The AI ranks candidates from 1 to 100 based on their resume. Candidates who score above 70 get a human interview. Below 70, they are automatically rejected.

During a quarterly review, you analyze the data and find a pattern:

// AI Screening Results — Last 6 Months
candidates_reviewed: 1,240
avg_score_engineering_roles: 74.2 (men), 58.1 (women)
interviews_granted: 83% male, 17% female
industry_avg_applicants: 52% male, 48% female
// Note: AI was trained on 10 years of past Meridian hiring data

The AI was trained on 10 years of Meridian's past hiring decisions — a period when the company hired very few women for engineering roles. What do you do with this finding?

Scenario 4 of 6⬤ Harder

The AI That Grades on a Curve Nobody Chose

📘 Key Term — Training Data: Training data is the information an AI learns from before it starts doing its job. If the training data has patterns that are unfair or incomplete, the AI will repeat those patterns — even if the people using it don't realize it's happening.

Meridian Tech sells an AI grading tool to school districts. The tool automatically scores student essays from 1 to 10 and provides feedback. It was trained on essays from college-level writing programs.

A teacher named Ms. Okafor contacts Meridian's support line. She has noticed that her students — who attend a school where many students are English language learners — consistently receive lower scores than similar essays she has hand-graded herself. The AI marks their writing as "unclear" or "informal" even when the ideas are strong and well-organized.

Ms. Okafor has documented 47 examples over three months. She is asking whether the AI is fair to her students. How do you respond?

Scenario 5 of 6⬤ Harder

The Finding Nobody Wants to Hear

📘 Key Term — Responsible Disclosure: Responsible disclosure means reporting a problem you've found — like a security flaw or an AI error — to the right people in the right way, so it can be fixed before it causes serious harm. In technology, staying quiet about a known problem is considered unethical and can be illegal.

While doing a routine check of Meridian Tech's customer-facing AI assistant, you discover something alarming: the AI has been giving incorrect answers about government benefit eligibility to users who ask about financial assistance programs.

The AI confidently tells users they "do not qualify" for certain benefits when, in fact, they do. You estimate the tool has been giving wrong answers for at least 8 weeks. The affected users are people applying for housing and food assistance programs.

Your manager tells you: "Don't make this bigger than it is. We'll fix it in the next release cycle in 6 weeks. No need to tell customers — it'll just cause panic."

What do you do?

Scenario 6 of 6 — Final Challenge⬤ Hardest

📘 Key Term — Accountability: Accountability means being willing to take responsibility for your decisions and their consequences — including when things go wrong. In AI, accountability means someone has to own the outcome of what an AI tool does. That person is usually a human, not the AI itself.
