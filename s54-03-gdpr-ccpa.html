Lesson 5.4 — Compliance & Policy Awareness

What Is GDPR, CCPA, and Why Should a Tech Worker Care?

Privacy regulations aren't just for lawyers. They set rules that every tech worker using AI tools must understand — because violations happen at the level of individual decisions, not organizational policies.

Why Regulations Matter at Your Level

Compliance teams handle the organizational side of privacy regulations — the contracts, the audits, the formal risk assessments. But the daily decisions that create compliance risk happen at your level: when you decide what to put in a prompt, which tool to use, how to handle a customer's data in your workflow.

Privacy laws define what individuals have a right to expect about their data. When your AI use violates those expectations, the violation happened in your workflow — not in the organization's legal department.

Regulations are not obstacles. They're the codified answer to the question "what should a reasonable organization do to protect people's data?" Understanding them makes you a better professional — not just a more compliant one.

The Four Regulations Tech Workers Encounter Most

🇪🇺 European Union
GDPR — General Data Protection Regulation
Enacted 2018 · Applies to anyone handling data of EU residents
Who it applies to:
GDPR applies to any organization that processes personal data of people in the EU — regardless of where the organization is located. A US company with even a small number of EU customers is subject to GDPR.
You must have a legal basis for processing personal data (consent, contract, legitimate interest)
Data subjects have the right to access, correct, and delete their data
Data transfers outside the EU require specific safeguards (this is why US AI tools need EU-specific data agreements)
Automated decision-making that significantly affects individuals requires human review capability
Fines for violations can reach €20 million or 4% of global annual revenue — whichever is higher
🤖 AI impact: Sending EU resident data to a US-based AI tool without appropriate data transfer safeguards (like Standard Contractual Clauses) may violate GDPR — even if the AI tool is widely used and reputable.
🇺🇸 California, USA
CCPA / CPRA — California Consumer Privacy Act
CCPA enacted 2020, expanded by CPRA 2023 · Applies to businesses handling California residents' data
Who it applies to:
Businesses that collect personal information from California residents AND meet any of: $25M+ annual revenue, data on 100,000+ consumers/households, or 50%+ revenue from selling personal data.
California residents have the right to know what data is collected about them
Residents can opt out of the sale or sharing of their personal information
Businesses must honor deletion requests within 45 days
Automated decision-making rules: consumers can request human review of decisions made by AI
Sensitive personal information (health, financial, precise location) has additional protections
🤖 AI impact: If your company uses AI to make decisions that affect California residents (hiring, lending, content recommendations), those residents may have rights to opt out and request human review that your workflow must support.
🏥 United States
HIPAA — Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act
Enacted 1996 · Applies to covered entities and their business associates in healthcare
Who it applies to:
Healthcare providers, health plans, healthcare clearinghouses, and any vendor that handles protected health information (PHI) on their behalf — including tech companies that build healthcare software.
Protected health information (PHI) cannot be shared with any third party without a Business Associate Agreement (BAA)
This includes AI tools — an AI company that processes PHI must sign a BAA with your organization
PHI includes more than patient names — it includes diagnosis codes, dates of service, and system architecture that could identify patients
Violations carry fines from $100 to $50,000 per violation, up to $1.9M per year for repeated violations
🤖 AI impact: Any AI tool used to process patient data — including code that touches patient records systems — requires a signed BAA. Free consumer AI tools do not have BAAs and cannot be used with PHI under any circumstances.
📋 Other Regulations
FERPA, COPPA, FINRA, SOX, and More
Industry-specific and population-specific regulations that may apply to your work
Key examples:
FERPA (education records — limits what AI can do with student data), COPPA (children's data — strict age verification requirements), FINRA (financial services communications and recordkeeping), SOX (financial reporting integrity, including AI-assisted reporting).
FERPA: Student education records cannot be shared with AI tools without proper authorization or anonymization
COPPA: AI tools cannot collect data from children under 13 without verifiable parental consent
FINRA: Communications used to generate financial advice — including AI-assisted — may be subject to recordkeeping requirements
SOX: AI used in financial reporting must have audit trails and controls to prevent manipulation
🤖 AI impact: If you build for or work in a regulated sector, ask your compliance team specifically which regulations apply to your work — and what they require for AI tool usage in that context.

Does This Apply to My Work?

Answer these questions to get a quick sense of which regulations are most relevant to your current role:

🔍 Regulation Relevance Check
Which of these apply to your work context?
Does your company have customers, users, or employees in the European Union?
Does your company serve California residents or have 100,000+ users?
Do you work with health-related data, patient records, or healthcare software?
Do you work in financial services, fintech, or on financial reporting systems?
🧠 Quick Check
A developer at a US healthcare startup uses ChatGPT's free tier to help debug code that queries patient appointment records. The code doesn't contain actual patient names — just appointment IDs and dates. Is this a potential HIPAA issue?
📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

GDPR applies wherever EU residents' data flows — geography of the company doesn't matter. CCPA protects California residents at scale. HIPAA governs all health-related data and requires BAAs with any vendor touching patient systems. These laws create obligations at the individual decision level, not just the organizational level.

PHI under HIPAA is broader than patient names. Appointment IDs, dates of service, diagnosis codes, and code that queries patient systems are all within HIPAA's scope. The BAA requirement applies to any vendor that handles PHI — including AI tools used to process or debug healthcare software.

You don't need to know every regulation in depth — you need to know which ones apply to your work context and what they require for AI use. When you're working in a regulated domain and considering using an AI tool, the question to ask is: does using this tool in this context comply with the regulations that govern this data?
