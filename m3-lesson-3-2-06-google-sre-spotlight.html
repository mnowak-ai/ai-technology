Lesson 3.2 — Summarizing Technical Information

Real-World Spotlight: How Google SREs Write Postmortem Summaries

Google's SRE team developed the postmortem culture that most of the industry now follows. Here's what makes their approach to summaries distinctive — and what you can take from it.

🔦
Real-World Spotlight
About Google SRE
Google's Site Reliability Engineering practice — documented in their publicly available "Site Reliability Engineering" book — established the postmortem culture that most modern engineering organizations now use. Their approach to summarization, blamelessness, and root cause analysis has been adopted across the industry. This spotlight draws on Google's publicly released SRE book, postmortem templates, and engineering blog posts.

When most engineering teams do postmortems, they're following practices that trace back to Google's SRE organization. The blameless postmortem, the five-why root cause analysis, the structured timeline — these ideas were formalized and published by Google and have since spread across the industry.

But the part most teams copy poorly is the summary. The format, yes. The philosophy, often not. Here's what Google's SRE approach to postmortem summaries actually emphasizes — and why it produces better outcomes than the typical "fill out the template" approach.

Six Principles from Google's SRE Postmortem Approach

1
The summary must make the "so what" explicit

Google SRE's postmortem format includes an explicit "Impact" section that translates technical events into user and business terms. This isn't optional — it's required, because the summary needs to communicate consequences, not just events.

Most postmortems describe what happened. Google's approach insists that the summary also states what that means — how many users were affected, what user-facing behavior changed, and whether SLOs were violated.

The "so what" in practice
Instead of: "The service returned 5xx errors for 43 minutes." Google SRE approach: "The service returned 5xx errors for 43 minutes, affecting approximately 2.3M requests. Users experienced failed searches with no error message. The 43-minute window violated our 99.9% monthly SLO — this incident alone consumed 62% of our monthly error budget."
Apply this: Every postmortem summary should include the user-facing consequence and the SLO impact — not just the technical event. If you don't know the SLO impact, finding out before you write the summary is part of the job.
2
Root cause summaries use the five-why method — not the first-why

A common postmortem failure is stopping at the proximate cause — the thing that directly triggered the incident — without asking why that condition existed. Google SRE's approach explicitly pushes for the underlying systemic cause.

The five-why method is simple: after identifying the cause, ask "why did that happen?" and repeat until you reach a systemic or process failure — something that can be fixed to prevent the next incident, not just the current one.

Five-why in postmortem summary
Surface cause: The deployment script failed. Why? → The script referenced a config file that had been renamed. Why? → The rename happened in a separate PR that wasn't linked to the deployment PR. Why? → Our PR review process doesn't require cross-referencing config changes. Why? → We've never had a config rename cause a deploy failure before, so no one thought to add this check. Root cause for the summary: "No process exists to flag config file renames as deployment dependencies. This is the systemic gap — fixing the specific script would have prevented this incident; fixing the process prevents the class of incidents."
Apply this: When writing your root cause summary, ask: could this same root cause produce a different incident next month? If yes, you haven't reached the systemic cause yet. Keep asking "why."
3
Action items are required to be "SMART" — not vague

Google SRE's postmortem culture has a strong norm against vague action items. "Improve monitoring" is not an action item. "Add PagerDuty alert for memory utilization > 80% on inference-api pods (owner: infra team, due Nov 20)" is.

The reasoning is practical: vague action items don't get done. They don't have owners who feel accountable. They can't be tracked. They can't be verified as complete. The postmortem becomes a ritual with no operational output. Google SRE treats action items as commitments — and the summary must make those commitments specific enough to be verified.

Vague vs. SMART action items
❌ Vague: "Improve test coverage for the payment service." ✅ SMART: "Add integration test for payment-service webhook retry handler behavior at queue depths >500 — owner: A. Chen, payments team — due Nov 22. Test must reproduce the OOMKill condition from this incident and verify the fix prevents it."
Apply this: Before any action item makes it into your postmortem summary, ask: does it have a specific owner (a person, not a team), a due date, and a clear definition of "done"? If not, it's not ready to be an action item.
4
Detection time is always summarized — not just resolution time

Most postmortem summaries focus on time-to-resolution (TTR). Google SRE's approach treats time-to-detect (TTD) as equally important — and often more so. A 3-hour outage that was detected in 2 minutes is a different story than one detected in 90 minutes. The detection gap is where monitoring, alerting, and on-call processes either succeed or fail.

Summarizing detection time forces the postmortem to address whether the monitoring caught the problem appropriately — or whether the incident was discovered by a customer complaint, a dashboarding engineer, or an escalation from another team.

Detection summary in practice
Time-to-detect: 47 minutes Detection method: Customer support ticket volume (NOT automated monitoring) Why monitoring didn't fire: PagerDuty alert rule had been misconfigured after Nov 8 monitoring update This is a significant finding — 47 minutes of customer impact before internal detection is a monitoring failure, not just a slow response. Action required: fix the alert rule AND audit all recently modified alert rules for similar suppression issues.
Apply this: Add "detection time" and "detection method" to every postmortem summary. If the method was anything other than automated monitoring, that's a finding — and it needs its own action item.
5
Postmortems are shared broadly — and that's the point

Google SRE's postmortem culture includes a strong norm of broad sharing. Postmortems aren't confidential internal documents — they're learning artifacts. The philosophy is that every team that could learn from an incident should have access to its postmortem, because organizational learning is the ultimate goal of the entire process.

This means the postmortem summary needs to be written for readers who weren't in the incident. Context that's obvious to the response team isn't obvious to an engineer in a different part of the organization who encounters the postmortem six months later. Good summaries are self-contained enough to be useful to someone who wasn't there.

Apply this: Before publishing your postmortem, read it as if you're an engineer who wasn't involved in this incident. Is every acronym defined on first use? Is the system architecture clear enough without background knowledge? Would someone unfamiliar with this service understand what happened?
6
The summary section is written last — and written separately

Google SRE's postmortem format treats the summary section as a distinct document within the postmortem — written after all other sections are complete, and written to stand alone. Someone who only reads the summary should have everything they need to understand the incident and its key findings.

This is counter to how most engineers write postmortems — they fill in sections in order, and the "summary" is written first as a placeholder. The result is a summary that doesn't reflect the full analysis. Writing the summary last ensures it synthesizes the completed work, not just the initial framing.

Apply this: In your next postmortem, write the summary section last. Fill in every other section first. Then write a summary that synthesizes all sections — including any findings that emerged during the analysis that weren't visible at the start.

What You Can Apply Immediately

📊
Add SLO impact to every summary
How much error budget did this incident consume? If you don't know, find out before publishing the postmortem.
🔍
Record detection time and method
How long between incident start and internal detection? How was it detected? If it wasn't automated monitoring, that's a finding.
✅
Make every action item SMART
Named person, specific deliverable, due date, definition of done. No team-level owners, no "improve X" action items.
✍️
Write the summary section last
Complete the full analysis first. Then write a summary that synthesizes everything — including findings you didn't expect when you started.

📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

Google SRE's postmortem approach adds three elements most teams skip: the "so what" (user impact and SLO budget consumed), detection time and detection method (not just resolution time), and SMART action items (named owner, due date, definition of done). All three close gaps that produce recurring incidents.

The five-why method distinguishes proximate causes (what triggered the incident) from systemic causes (what made the trigger possible). Postmortem summaries that stop at the proximate cause produce one-time fixes. Summaries that reach the systemic cause produce process improvements that prevent a class of incidents.

Write the summary last. The summary section of a postmortem synthesizes the full analysis — it can't be written accurately until all other sections are complete. A summary written first is a guess. A summary written last is a synthesis.
