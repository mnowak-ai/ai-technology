Lesson 5.2 — Bias & AI Limitations

IBM Watson for Oncology: The Full Story of What Went Wrong and Why It Matters

IBM's Watson was supposed to revolutionize cancer treatment. Instead, it recommended unsafe treatments — and almost no one outside IBM knew why. This is one of the most instructive AI failures in history.

The Promise

In 2011, IBM's Watson AI system beat two of the greatest Jeopardy! champions of all time. The world watched a machine demonstrate what looked like broad, flexible intelligence — and IBM set out to put that power to work on one of medicine's hardest problems: cancer.

The vision was genuinely compelling. Cancer treatment involves thousands of research papers, dozens of evolving drug protocols, and individualized patient factors that are nearly impossible for a single oncologist to synthesize in real time. Watson, IBM argued, could read everything — and recommend the best treatment for each patient. Dozens of hospitals around the world paid millions of dollars to find out if this was true.

It wasn't. And the way it wasn't is a case study every tech professional needs to understand.

The failure of Watson for Oncology wasn't a software bug. It was a fundamental mismatch between what the AI was trained on and what it was being asked to do — a mismatch that nobody told the hospitals buying the system.

The Story, Year by Year

2011
2011 — The Beginning
IBM partners with Memorial Sloan Kettering Cancer Center
Flush from the Jeopardy! victory, IBM begins a partnership with Memorial Sloan Kettering (MSK), one of the world's leading cancer hospitals. The plan: train Watson on MSK's oncology expertise and build a tool that recommends cancer treatments. IBM calls the product "Watson for Oncology." The partnership is announced to significant media fanfare.
⚠️
The Critical Flaw — Discovered Later
Watson is trained on hypothetical cases, not real patients
This is the central failure. Rather than training Watson on real anonymized patient records — which would have required complex privacy compliance and data collection — IBM and MSK's oncologists trained Watson primarily on hypothetical cancer cases. A small group of MSK physicians created training scenarios and supplied the "correct" treatment recommendations. Watson learned from their opinions about imaginary patients, not from outcomes data about real ones.
2015
2015–2017 — The Rollout
Hospitals across the world buy Watson for Oncology
Watson for Oncology is sold to hospitals in the US, Europe, and Asia. IBM's marketing describes it as capable of synthesizing vast amounts of oncology literature to generate evidence-based treatment recommendations. Hospitals in India, South Korea, Thailand, and elsewhere pay licensing fees and integrate Watson into their clinical workflows. Watson is used — with varying degrees of direct influence — in recommendations for real cancer patients.
🚨
2017 — The Internal Document Leak
Internal Slack messages reveal "unsafe and incorrect" recommendations
An internal IBM document — later obtained by investigative journalists — describes Watson for Oncology as generating "multiple examples of unsafe and incorrect treatment recommendations." The document, from 2017, shows IBM's own teams flagging that Watson was suggesting treatments that differed significantly from what oncologists would actually recommend for real patients. In some cases, Watson recommended chemotherapy regimens at doses or combinations that experienced oncologists considered unsafe for the patients described.
2018
2018 — The Investigation
STAT News publishes the full exposé
Investigative journalism outlet STAT News publishes a detailed investigation. It reveals the hypothetical training data problem, the "unsafe and incorrect" recommendations documented internally, and the fact that Watson's recommendations were often inconsistent with the latest clinical guidelines and standard of care. The investigation describes the training process — dominated by the preferences of a small number of individual MSK oncologists — as a fundamental methodological flaw that made Watson's "evidence-based" branding misleading.
📋
2019–2022 — The Retreat
IBM quietly discontinues Watson for Oncology
Multiple hospital systems discontinue Watson for Oncology. IBM eventually sells its Watson Health division in 2022, effectively acknowledging the failure. The total investment — by IBM, its hospital partners, and the patients whose treatment may have been influenced by Watson's recommendations — was never fully accounted for. The tool was never recalled or regulated the way a medical device would be.

The Four Things That Went Wrong

1
Training data that didn't reflect reality
Hypothetical patient cases created by a small group of oncologists are not a representative sample of real cancer patients. Real patients are older, sicker, have more comorbidities (other conditions), and don't fit the clean profiles used in hypothetical cases. Watson learned from idealized examples, then was applied to messy reality — and the gap between the two is where unsafe recommendations were generated.
2
One team's expert opinions encoded as universal truth
Medical practice varies. Treatment guidelines differ across countries, hospitals, and patient populations. Watson was trained on the opinions of one institution's oncologists — MSK — as if those opinions were objective, universal standards. When Watson was deployed in hospitals in India or South Korea, it was recommending treatments based on a narrow American institutional perspective that didn't account for local guidelines, available drugs, or patient demographics.
3
Marketing that dramatically overstated what the system could do
IBM described Watson for Oncology as synthesizing "millions of pages" of medical literature and producing "evidence-based" recommendations. Neither description matched how the system actually worked. The hospitals buying Watson understood they were getting a literature-synthesis engine. They were actually getting an opinion-encoding system trained on a small set of hypothetical cases. This mismatch meant clinicians may have trusted Watson's outputs more than warranted.
4
No regulatory oversight for AI clinical decision support
A new drug or medical device requires clinical trials, FDA approval, and post-market surveillance before it can influence patient care. Watson for Oncology — which was directly influencing cancer treatment decisions at hospitals worldwide — went through none of this. The regulatory framework for AI in clinical settings was not yet developed. Watson was deployed at scale before anyone outside IBM and MSK could independently validate whether it worked.

What This Teaches Every Tech Professional

🏷️
Marketing claims are not technical validation
IBM called Watson "evidence-based." The actual training process was opinion-based. When evaluating any AI tool for professional use, ask what the system was actually trained on — not what the marketing says it can do.
🌍
Context transfer is never automatic
A system trained in one context (MSK oncologists' preferences) will not automatically work in another (Indian hospitals, Thai patient populations, South Korean treatment guidelines). Assumptions baked into training data travel with the model everywhere it goes.
🔬
High-stakes deployment needs real validation
Watson was deployed in clinical settings before anyone had rigorously tested whether it produced better patient outcomes than standard care. In any domain where the stakes are high — health, finance, legal, safety systems — validation against real-world outcomes is not optional.
📢
Flagging problems is a professional obligation
IBM's internal teams flagged "unsafe and incorrect" recommendations years before this became public. The failure to act on those internal flags — or to communicate them to hospital customers — compounded the harm. Raising concerns early is always the right call.

📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

Watson for Oncology failed because it was trained on hypothetical cases created by a small group of oncologists — then marketed and deployed as if it were a broad, evidence-based clinical decision tool. The gap between the training data and the deployment context produced unsafe treatment recommendations.

One institution's expert opinions, encoded into training data, became the model's universal standard — applied to patient populations with different demographics, comorbidities, and local treatment norms. Context doesn't automatically transfer with an AI system.

Marketing language about AI is not technical validation. "Evidence-based," "AI-powered," and "trained on millions of data points" are claims that require scrutiny — what data? How collected? Validated how? By whom? These questions are your professional responsibility to ask.

High-stakes AI deployment without rigorous real-world validation is a pattern — not just a Watson problem. The lesson isn't to avoid AI in high-stakes domains. It's that the higher the stakes, the more rigorous the validation needs to be before anything affects real people.
