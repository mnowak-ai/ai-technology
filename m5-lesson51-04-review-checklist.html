Lesson 5.1 — Security & Privacy Risks

Reviewing AI Outputs Before You Share Them: A Professional Checklist

AI can produce a polished, confident-sounding response in seconds. But polished and confident doesn't mean accurate, appropriate, or safe to share. This article gives you a practical review process — one you can run through in under two minutes — that catches the problems that have gotten tech workers into serious trouble.

Why "It Looked Good to Me" Isn't Enough

Here's the core problem with AI outputs: AI doesn't know when it's wrong. A made-up statistic looks exactly like a real one. A hallucinated court case sounds exactly like a real case. A paragraph that includes a client's personal information reads just like a paragraph that doesn't.

The AI has no embarrassment, no uncertainty signals, and no professional liability. It generates the best-looking answer it can and presents it with complete confidence, regardless of whether it's accurate, appropriate, or safe.

You have all three of those things. Which is why the review step — before you share, forward, submit, or publish anything an AI helped create — is not optional. It's the whole point of keeping a human in the loop.

AI does the drafting. You do the reviewing. That's not a weakness in the system — it's the entire design. The review step is where your professional value lives.

🎯 Section 1: Accuracy & Factual Integrity
✓
Have I verified every specific statistic, number, or percentage the AI included?
AI frequently generates plausible-sounding but fabricated numbers. "73% of companies..." "Research shows that..." — these require a source you can point to. If you can't find the source, remove the number.
✓
Have I confirmed that any named organizations, products, people, or events actually exist and are accurately described?
AI hallucinates company names, product features, executive names, and historical events. Every proper noun in an AI output is worth a 10-second verification.
✓
Is the information current? Could anything have changed since the AI's training cutoff?
AI knowledge has a cutoff date. Laws change, prices change, companies get acquired, products get discontinued. Anything time-sensitive needs to be verified against a current source.
✓
Does the AI's conclusion actually follow from the information it presents?
AI can confidently assert a conclusion that doesn't logically follow from its own supporting points. Read the argument as if you're evaluating someone else's work, not approving your own.
🔐 Section 2: Privacy & Data Safety
✓
Does this output contain any personal information about real people that wasn't supposed to be included?
If you gave the AI data about real people, check whether any names, emails, account numbers, addresses, or identifying details made it into the output — even if you didn't ask for them.
✓
Is any customer, patient, employee, or client data present in this output that shouldn't be shared externally?
This is about what's going out, not what went in. Even if you appropriately used enterprise AI tools with internal data, the output might summarize or expose that data in ways that shouldn't leave the organization.
✓
Is the recipient of this content authorized to see everything in it?
Who you're sending to matters as much as what you're sending. An AI summary that's appropriate to share with your manager may not be appropriate to share with a client, a vendor, or a colleague in a different department.
⚠️ Section 3: Confidentiality & Proprietary Information
✓
Does this output reveal anything about internal systems, architecture, processes, or strategies that is confidential?
AI outputs built from internal information can inadvertently expose that information. A summary document might name internal system names, describe proprietary workflows, or reveal organizational details that shouldn't be in an external-facing document.
✓
If this output were seen by a competitor, journalist, or regulator — would any of it be a problem?
This is the "headline test" applied to outputs. Imagine the worst-case reader. Is there anything in here that, if publicly disclosed, would be embarrassing, damaging, or legally problematic for your organization?
🧠 Section 4: Professional Quality & Appropriateness
✓
Does the tone match the situation — the audience, the stakes, and the professional context?
AI defaults to certain tones that aren't always appropriate. An AI-drafted email to a grieving client might sound too businesslike. A technical response might use jargon inappropriate for the reader. Always read the tone, not just the content.
✓
Would I be comfortable putting my name on this and owning everything it says?
This is the final and most important question. You're not distributing "AI's work" — you're distributing your work that AI helped with. If any part of this output makes you uncomfortable to own, that part needs to be fixed or removed before it goes anywhere.
✓
Have I read this output fully — not just skimmed it — at least once before sharing?
The most embarrassing AI output problems are in the parts people don't read carefully. AI often saves the problematic content for the middle of a long response, not the beginning. Read the whole thing, every time.

The Four Most Common Problems the Checklist Catches

These are the issues that come up most frequently when tech workers review AI outputs carefully. Each one is easy to miss on a quick read — which is exactly why the checklist matters.

🎭
Failure Type 1
Hallucinated Facts That Sound Real
AI generates statistics, citations, and specific facts that don't exist. The writing flows naturally, the number sounds reasonable, the source sounds credible — and none of it is real. This is the most common and most damaging type of AI output failure.
❌ "According to a 2023 Gartner report, 68% of enterprises plan to expand AI investment by Q3." — This specific statistic may not exist. Always find and link the actual source.
🔍
Failure Type 2
Personal Information Surfacing in Output
When you give AI data to work with — customer lists, employee records, support tickets — it sometimes surfaces identifying details in its output even when you didn't ask for them. The names and emails you fed in might appear in a summary meant for a broader audience.
❌ "Top complaint themes from your customer data: issues reported by users in the 90210 zip code, most notably from john.doe@company.com, center around..." — Personal data appearing in a general analysis report.
📅
Failure Type 3
Outdated Information Presented as Current
AI has a training cutoff. It can't know what changed after that date. Leadership teams change, laws get updated, products get discontinued, companies get acquired, prices shift. AI presents all of its knowledge with the same confidence regardless of whether it's current.
❌ "The current CTO of [Company] is [Name]" — presented confidently, but the executive may have changed since the AI's training data was collected. Always verify current roles, prices, laws, and recent events.
🎯
Failure Type 4
Tone That Doesn't Match the Situation
AI writes for a general audience. It doesn't know the specific person you're writing to, the history of your relationship, or the sensitivity of the situation. It can produce something technically correct but completely wrong for the actual human context — too formal, too casual, or missing an empathetic note that the situation required.
❌ An AI-drafted response to a client complaint that says "Thank you for your feedback. We have logged this issue for review." — Technically accurate, but reads as cold and dismissive in a high-stakes relationship.

📌 What to Remember

The Review Step Is Where Your Judgment Lives

The 12-item checklist covers four categories every AI output needs before it leaves your desk: accuracy and factual integrity, privacy and data safety, confidentiality of proprietary information, and professional appropriateness. Every category has caught real incidents in real workplaces.

The four most common output failures — hallucinated facts, personal information surfacing, outdated data presented as current, and wrong tone — share one thing in common: they're easy to miss on a quick read and obvious in hindsight. The checklist forces a slow enough look to catch them.

The ownership question — "Would I be comfortable putting my name on everything this says?" — is the most important question on the checklist. It forces you to engage with the output as your work, not as the AI's work. Because legally, professionally, and ethically, it is your work. The AI helped you create it; you're responsible for what it contains.
