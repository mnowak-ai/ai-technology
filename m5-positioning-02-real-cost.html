Module 5 — AI Ethics, Security & Responsible Use

The Real Cost of Getting AI Wrong: Stories From the Field

It's easy to think AI ethics mistakes are things that happen to other companies — big tech giants making billion-dollar decisions. But the cases in this article show something different: most AI failures start with ordinary workers making ordinary choices, without realizing the consequences. These stories are from the real world. Learn from them.

Why Stories Matter More Than Rules

You could memorize a list of rules about AI use. "Don't share sensitive data. Verify outputs. Follow policy." But rules without context are easy to forget the moment you're under pressure on a deadline.

Stories are different. When you know what actually happened — who was involved, what they did, and what it cost — the risk becomes real. And real risks are the ones you actually remember to avoid.

The four cases below cover the four most common ways AI gets people, companies, and communities into serious trouble: data breaches, algorithmic bias, misplaced trust in AI outputs, and policy violations. Each one has a lesson you can carry into your first tech job.

Every one of these cases started with someone who was just trying to do their job. Not one of them set out to cause harm. That's what makes them so important to understand.

Four Case Files: What Went Wrong and What It Cost

🔐
Case File 1 — Data Security Breach
The Genetic Data Company That Exposed 6.9 Million People
23andMe — 2023
What Happened
23andMe is a company that analyzes your DNA to tell you about your ancestry and health risks. In 2023, hackers broke into thousands of user accounts by using passwords that people had reused from other websites. Once inside those accounts, the attackers used a 23andMe feature called "DNA Relatives" — a tool that shows you genetic matches with other users — to scrape the data of 6.9 million people connected to those accounts. Most of those 6.9 million people had done nothing wrong. Their data was exposed because of other users' weak passwords and the way 23andMe's data-sharing features worked.
How It Unfolded
🔓
Initial breach
Hackers used a technique called "credential stuffing" — trying passwords stolen from other breaches to log into 23andMe accounts. About 14,000 accounts were compromised this way.
🌐
Cascade effect
Those 14,000 accounts had access to the DNA Relatives feature. Through that one feature, attackers reached data from 6.9 million connected profiles — family relationships, ethnicity estimates, location data.
📢
Public disclosure
The stolen data appeared on hacker forums. It was sorted and sold by ethnicity — including specific databases of Ashkenazi Jewish and Chinese users. The breach became national news.
⚖️
Legal consequences
23andMe faced over 30 lawsuits. The company eventually agreed to a $30 million settlement. Multiple executives departed. The company's stock dropped significantly.
What It Cost
$30M
Settlement paid to affected users
6.9M
People whose genetic data was exposed without consent
30+
Lawsuits filed against the company
🎓 The Lesson for Tech Workers
Data sharing features are a security risk, not just a convenience feature. When you build, configure, or use AI tools that connect users' data together, you need to think about what happens if access to one account unlocks data from thousands of others. The access control lesson in Module 5 is directly connected to this case. Who can see what — and how much can spread from a single entry point — is a critical design question.

Case File 2 — Algorithmic Bias
The Healthcare Algorithm That Gave White Patients Priority Over Black Patients
Optum / United Health — 2019
What Happened
Across the United States, hospitals and insurance companies use AI algorithms to decide which patients need extra care management. These programs cost money to run, so the AI was trained to identify patients who were sick enough to benefit but also likely to generate high healthcare costs — with the idea that cost roughly tracks need.
A research study published in the journal Science analyzed one of the most widely used of these algorithms and found a serious problem: it was systematically recommending less care for Black patients compared to white patients with the same level of illness. The algorithm wasn't explicitly looking at race — but it used healthcare spending as a proxy for need, and because Black patients had historically been given less access to care (and therefore spent less), the system assumed they were healthier than they actually were.
Why the Bias Happened
The algorithm used past healthcare cost data to predict who needed care. But that data reflected decades of unequal healthcare access. Black patients had historically received less care, not because they were healthier, but because of systemic barriers. The AI learned from that history and repeated it — automatically, at scale, across thousands of hospitals, every single day.
What It Cost
~200M
People affected by this type of algorithm across the US healthcare system
~50%
Reduction in bias when the algorithm was corrected
$1B+
Market cap hit by Optum as the story circulated among investors and policymakers
🎓 The Lesson for Tech Workers
AI learns from historical data. If that history is unfair — and a lot of history is — the AI will repeat the unfairness at scale, automatically, without anyone intending harm. That's why understanding bias isn't just a feel-good exercise. In tech roles, you may be the person who notices that something feels off. The IBM Watson lesson in Module 5 will give you the framework to recognize it and know what to do.

Case File 3 — Misplaced Trust in AI
The Air Canada Chatbot That Promised a Discount That Didn't Exist — and Lost in Court
Air Canada — 2024
What Happened
Jake Moffatt's grandmother passed away in November 2022. He needed to fly from Vancouver to Toronto for the funeral and checked Air Canada's website before booking. The airline had a "bereavement fare" policy — reduced prices for people traveling due to a family death — but the details were complicated. Moffatt asked Air Canada's AI chatbot about the policy.
The chatbot told him he could book a full-price ticket now and apply for the bereavement discount within 90 days of travel. He did exactly that. When he applied for the discount, Air Canada said the chatbot was wrong — the policy didn't work that way, and he wasn't entitled to any refund. Moffatt took them to small claims court.
Air Canada's legal team argued that the chatbot was "a separate legal entity" responsible for its own statements. The judge rejected that argument entirely and ruled in Moffatt's favor. Air Canada was responsible for what its AI said, period.
How It Unfolded
💬
Customer interaction
Moffatt asked the chatbot a specific policy question and received a confident, specific, incorrect answer. He screenshotted the conversation.
📋
Company refusal
Air Canada refused the refund and told Moffatt the chatbot "cannot provide binding information." He had documentation proving what the chatbot said.
⚖️
Court ruling
The tribunal ruled that companies cannot disown their AI tools. Air Canada had to pay Moffatt's partial refund plus additional damages.
What It Cost
🌍 Global
News coverage in 40+ countries about corporate AI accountability
Legal Precedent
Set the standard: companies own what their AI says, period
Brand Damage
Air Canada became the global example of how not to deploy a customer-facing AI chatbot
🎓 The Lesson for Tech Workers
AI tools that interact with customers, patients, or anyone external represent your organization. When they say something incorrect — and AI tools regularly do — your organization is responsible for the consequences. "The AI said it, not us" is not a legal defense and has now been rejected in court. This is why reviewing AI outputs before they reach the public, and understanding the limits of what AI tools should be authorized to promise or confirm, is critical professional knowledge.

Case File 4 — Policy Violation
The Company That Used AI to Score Employees on "Happiness" — Without Telling Them
Teleperformance — 2022–2023
What Happened
Teleperformance is one of the largest call center companies in the world, employing hundreds of thousands of workers. In 2022, investigative journalism revealed that the company was using AI surveillance tools to monitor workers far more extensively than employees knew — including facial expression analysis during video calls to score workers on their "emotional state," monitoring keystrokes and mouse movements, and using AI to evaluate performance in ways that workers had no visibility into or ability to contest.
Workers in Colombia — where many of the call centers were located — reported that the AI systems were flagging them for "disengagement" or poor performance based on signals they couldn't see or understand, affecting their employment status. The workers had signed contracts giving consent to monitoring, but many said the AI's scope went far beyond what those contracts described. Regulators in Italy eventually blocked some of Teleperformance's data collection practices entirely.
What Made This Particularly Serious
This case combines two problems that come up repeatedly in AI ethics: lack of transparency (workers didn't know how they were being evaluated) and lack of meaningful consent (agreeing to vague monitoring terms doesn't mean you agreed to AI emotional analysis). When AI makes decisions that affect people's livelihoods — hiring, firing, performance reviews — those people have a right to understand how those decisions are made.
What It Cost
Regulatory
Italian regulators blocked data collection operations after investigation
Congressional
US Senate investigation launched into AI workplace surveillance practices
Reputational
Named in multiple international reports on exploitative AI use in workplaces
🎓 The Lesson for Tech Workers
AI tools that monitor, score, or make decisions about people have to operate transparently and within clear, understood boundaries. "We have consent buried in the contract" is not ethical — and it's increasingly not legal either. As a tech professional, you may be the person building or deploying these systems. The transparency and compliance principles in Module 5 exist precisely because of cases like this one. People have a right to know when AI is affecting their lives.

The Four Types of Cost That Always Appear

Across every AI ethics failure, the consequences tend to fall into the same four categories. They don't always all show up, but understanding each type helps you see why the stakes are so high.

💸
Financial Cost
Fines, settlements, lawsuits, and the cost of fixing what went wrong. The 23andMe settlement was $30 million. GDPR violations can cost up to 4% of a company's total global revenue. These numbers get people's attention at the executive level fast.
📰
Reputational Cost
Trust is hard to earn and easy to lose. When a company's AI does something harmful, the story travels everywhere. Customers leave. Investors get nervous. Future employees hesitate. For individuals, an AI ethics mistake can follow your name in search results for years.
⚖️
Legal and Regulatory Cost
Governments are increasingly writing laws that hold companies responsible for what their AI does. Italy blocked Teleperformance. The EU's AI Act creates serious liability for high-risk AI systems. Breaking these rules isn't a slap on the wrist — it can mean losing the ability to operate in certain markets entirely.
👥
Human Cost
This is the one that matters most. Behind every data breach is a real person whose medical history, genetic information, or financial data is now in the hands of strangers. Behind every biased algorithm are real people who didn't get care, jobs, or loans they deserved. AI errors hurt people.

📌 What to Remember From This Article

The Stories Behind the Rules

Data breaches often start small and cascade fast. The 23andMe breach started with 14,000 compromised accounts and reached 6.9 million people through a single connected feature. Understanding how data flows — and where access control can stop a cascade — is a core tech skill.

AI bias is inherited from historical data. The healthcare algorithm didn't set out to discriminate — it learned from decades of unequal care and repeated that inequality automatically, at scale. Bias in AI isn't always intentional; it's often a reflection of an unfair past being carried forward.

Companies are legally responsible for what their AI says. The Air Canada ruling made this official: you cannot blame your AI tool. If your organization deploys an AI system that gives people incorrect information that affects their decisions, the organization — and the people who built or authorized it — is accountable.

Transparency isn't optional when AI affects people's lives. When AI makes decisions about people's employment, health, credit, or access to services, those people have a right to understand how — and that right is increasingly being enforced by law. Designing AI systems that are accountable and explainable isn't just ethical; it's becoming legally required.
