Lesson 5.1 — Security & Privacy Risks

Approved vs. Unapproved AI Tools: Why the Difference Matters More Than You Think

Not all AI tools are created equal — and at work, that difference can be the line between staying safe and creating a serious problem. This article explains what makes a tool enterprise-approved, what "shadow AI" means, and exactly what to do when you're not sure.

The Same Tool. Two Very Different Situations.

Let's say your company uses Microsoft Copilot — the AI assistant built into Microsoft 365. And let's say you also use ChatGPT's free version on your personal browser at work.

To you, they might feel like the same thing. Both answer questions. Both help write code. Both summarize documents. But from a security and legal standpoint, they are completely different products with completely different data rules.

Microsoft Copilot in your company's M365 environment operates under a contract your company negotiated. Your data stays inside your company's Microsoft tenant. It doesn't train the AI model. It's covered by your company's compliance agreements. ChatGPT's free version has none of those guarantees — and using it for work tasks means your company's data is traveling to an external server under a consumer agreement your employer never signed.

An approved tool is one your company has reviewed, vetted, and signed a contract with. An unapproved tool is one your company knows nothing about — and can't protect you from.

What Makes a Tool "Enterprise-Approved"?

Getting a tool onto a company's approved list isn't simple. It usually involves a formal review process. Here's what companies check:

📝
Data Processing Agreement (DPA)
A legal contract that specifies exactly how the AI company can use, store, and delete your organization's data. Without a DPA, your company has no legal guarantee about what happens to your inputs.
🔒
Security Certifications
Certifications like SOC 2 Type II (a security audit standard) confirm that the vendor has real security practices in place. Your IT team verifies these before approving any tool.
⚖️
Compliance Compatibility
If your company handles health data (HIPAA), financial data (SOX), or personal data from Europe (GDPR), the AI tool must support those regulatory requirements. Not all do.
🏗️
Data Isolation
Enterprise versions of tools are configured so that your company's data is isolated — other organizations' data can't mix with yours, and your inputs don't influence model behavior for other users.

What Is "Shadow AI" — and Why Is It a Problem?

Shadow AI (also called shadow IT) refers to AI tools that employees use at work without the knowledge or approval of the IT or security team. It's not necessarily malicious — most people using shadow AI are just trying to be more productive.

But the risks are significant:

DATA RISK
Company data — code, customer information, internal documents — leaves the company's secure environment and enters systems the security team has never reviewed. If those systems are breached, your company's data is exposed too.
LEGAL RISK
In regulated industries, using an unapproved tool to handle protected data can trigger regulatory violations — even if nothing goes wrong. The violation is the act of sharing the data, not just the outcome.
CONTRACT RISK
Most employment contracts and NDAs include clauses about not sharing proprietary information with third parties. An unapproved AI tool is a third party your company never authorized. You could be personally liable.
BLIND SPOT RISK
Your IT team can only protect what they know about. Shadow AI creates gaps in the security picture that make it harder to detect and respond to incidents — because nobody knew there was data in that system to begin with.

What to Do When You're Not Sure

You discover a new AI tool that looks genuinely useful for your work. You're not sure if it's approved. Here's the right sequence of steps:

1
Check your company's approved tools list first. Most companies maintain an internal list of approved software. It might live on an internal wiki, in the IT portal, or in an employee handbook. Start there — the answer might already be available.
2
Ask your manager or IT team before using it for real work. A quick message — "Hey, is [tool name] approved for use with work data?" — takes 30 seconds. Getting called into a meeting about an unauthorized data transfer takes much longer.
3
If you want to try it, use only public or non-sensitive information. While you wait for an approval answer, you can experiment with a tool using information that's already publicly available — not internal documents, client data, or proprietary code.
4
If you think a tool should be added to the approved list, say so. Many companies have a process for employees to request new tools. You're not just protecting yourself by following this process — you're helping your whole team get access to good tools safely.
🧠 Quick Check
A developer starts using a free AI coding assistant they found online because it gives better code suggestions than the company-approved tool. They only use it for internal code — nothing with customer data. Is this okay?
📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

An enterprise-approved tool has been reviewed by your company's security and legal team, signed a data agreement, meets compliance requirements, and keeps your data inside a protected environment. A consumer tool has none of these guarantees.

Shadow AI — using unapproved tools at work — creates data, legal, and contract risks even when nothing goes wrong. The act of sharing proprietary information with an unauthorized third party is itself a violation in many contexts.

When you're unsure about a tool, check the approved list, ask your manager or IT team, and don't use work data until you have a clear answer. That 30-second question protects you, your team, and your company.

Internal code is proprietary — not just customer data. Using an unapproved AI tool with your company's source code violates most employment contracts, regardless of whether any customer information is involved.
