Lesson 3.1 — Clear Technical Communication

Real-World Spotlight: How Atlassian Standardized Technical Communication Across 10,000+ Employees

Atlassian built Jira, Confluence, and Trello — and used them to solve its own hardest communication problem: how does a distributed engineering organization stay aligned when everyone writes differently?

🔦
Real-World Spotlight
About Atlassian
Atlassian is the company behind Jira, Confluence, Trello, and Bitbucket — tools used by more than 300,000 organizations to manage software development and documentation. With over 10,000 employees across distributed offices and teams, Atlassian has spent years developing and publicly sharing its approach to internal communication standards. This spotlight draws on Atlassian's publicly available team playbooks, engineering blog, and documentation culture materials.

There's something interesting about studying Atlassian's communication practices: the company's entire product line exists to solve communication and coordination problems in software teams. They're not just practitioners of technical communication — they've built the tools that millions of engineering teams use to do it. Their own internal practices are worth examining closely.

Atlassian doesn't just build collaboration tools. They eat their own cooking — and what they've learned about what makes technical communication work at scale is some of the most practical guidance available.

The Core Problem: Communication Drift at Scale

Atlassian's engineering organization scaled rapidly through the 2010s — from a few hundred engineers to thousands, spread across Sydney, Amsterdam, Austin, New York, and San Francisco. Each new office, each new team, and each new product line brought its own communication norms: different Jira ticket formats, different Confluence page structures, different definitions of "done," different interpretations of what a "P1" incident meant.

This is the communication drift problem — and it happens to every organization that grows faster than its documentation standards. What worked when everyone was in one room stops working when teams are distributed across time zones and can't rely on informal clarification.

Atlassian's response was to treat communication standards as a product problem — something to be deliberately designed, tested, and maintained — rather than an HR problem or a culture initiative.

Five Lessons from Atlassian's Approach

1
Jira tickets are documentation — and they treated them that way

Most engineering teams treat Jira tickets as task trackers: a title, an assignee, and maybe a description. Atlassian's engineering teams treat Jira tickets as the primary documentation artifact for a unit of work. A well-written Jira ticket at Atlassian includes: a clear problem statement, the specific user or system affected, acceptance criteria that are testable (not vague), links to relevant Confluence documentation, and — critically — an explanation of why this work matters, not just what it is.

This shift — from ticket as task to ticket as documentation — changes what it means to "complete" a ticket. Work isn't done when the code ships. It's done when the ticket captures enough context for any engineer to understand what was built, why, and what "success" looks like.

Apply this today: Before you close your next Jira ticket, ask: could someone who wasn't part of this work understand what was built and why from the ticket alone? If not, add a one-paragraph summary to the description before closing.
2
They built "plays" — reusable communication templates for recurring situations

Atlassian's Team Playbook is one of its most widely shared contributions to the industry. Rather than vague guidance ("communicate clearly"), the playbook offers specific structured templates — called "plays" — for recurring communication situations: how to run a project kickoff, how to write a decision document, how to structure an incident retrospective, how to communicate a team health check.

Each play defines: what situation it's for, what the output should look like, who participates, and how long it should take. The plays don't eliminate creativity — they eliminate the blank-page problem that slows down communication in most organizations. Instead of figuring out how to write a postmortem, teams follow the postmortem play and spend their energy on the content, not the format.

This is the key insight: standardizing the format frees up cognitive capacity for the substance. When engineers don't have to decide how to structure a decision document, they have more mental energy to make the actual decision.

Apply this today: Identify the three most common documents your team writes (incident reports, design docs, deployment notices). Create a simple template for each. Even a five-line structure reduces writing time and improves consistency.
3
Confluence as the source of truth — not email, Slack, or memory

Atlassian made an explicit organizational commitment: decisions, documentation, and institutional knowledge live in Confluence — not in email threads, not in Slack history, not in individual engineers' heads. This sounds obvious, but it requires active enforcement. The natural default in most organizations is to make decisions in meetings or Slack and document them only when someone remembers to.

Atlassian's practice is to document decisions before they're finalized — the decision document is the artifact that gets shared for input, revised based on feedback, and approved when consensus is reached. The Confluence page is not a record of what was decided in a meeting. It is the meeting, in written form.

For distributed engineering teams, this shifts who can participate in decisions. A decision made verbally in a Sydney standup excludes engineers in Amsterdam and Austin. The same decision made in a Confluence document can be reviewed and commented on asynchronously, across time zones, by anyone who needs to weigh in.

Apply this today: For your next technical decision, write a one-page decision doc in your team's wiki before the meeting — not after. Use the meeting to review and refine it, not to make the decision from scratch. See how the quality of the discussion changes when everyone arrives with written context.
4
They defined what "P1" means — and enforced it

One of the most practically valuable things Atlassian did was define its incident severity levels in writing, specifically, and enforce those definitions across teams. In many organizations, "P1" means "the thing the loudest engineer is most upset about right now." That makes prioritization impossible and incident response chaotic.

Atlassian's severity definitions are specific and measurable. A P1 is not "a serious problem" — it's defined by specific criteria: what percentage of users are affected, whether core functionality is completely unavailable, what the revenue impact threshold is, and what the customer-facing impact is. P2 has different thresholds. P3 has different ones again.

This specificity does two things. First, it removes the argument — if an incident meets the P1 definition, it's a P1, regardless of which engineer is escalating it. Second, it makes communication consistent: when an Atlassian engineer in Austin says "we have a P1," an engineer in Amsterdam knows exactly what that means without asking.

Apply this today: Look at your team's incident severity definitions. Are they specific enough to classify an incident without debate? If "P1" means different things to different engineers, that's a communication standard gap worth closing — and it takes one documentation session to fix.
5
They measured communication quality — not just communication volume

Most organizations measure communication by volume: how many tickets were created, how many pages are in the wiki, how many Slack messages were sent. Atlassian developed practices for measuring communication quality — specifically, whether documentation was actually being used to make better decisions faster.

One example: they track how often engineers have to ask clarifying questions after a Jira ticket is assigned. High clarification rates on tickets are a signal that ticket quality is low — engineers are receiving incomplete information and having to interrupt the person who wrote the ticket to get what they need. Reducing clarification rate is a direct measure of documentation improvement.

This approach — treating communication as a system with measurable outputs rather than a behavior to be encouraged — is what distinguishes a mature engineering communication culture from one that has good intentions but inconsistent results.

Apply this today: Count how many clarifying questions you get on the next five tickets or documents you write. That number is your baseline. Then apply the clarity principles from this module and track whether it drops. Clarity improvement has a measurable signal.

What You Can Steal From This — Right Now

You don't need to be at a 10,000-person company to apply these ideas. Here are the most portable lessons regardless of your team size.

📋
Build three templates
Pick the three documents your team writes most often and create a simple template for each. Even a five-bullet structure eliminates the blank-page problem and makes consistency easy.
🎯
Define severity levels in writing
Write down what P1, P2, and P3 mean with specific, measurable criteria. Post it where everyone can see it. Eliminate the "is this a P1?" debate before the next incident.
📝
Document decisions before the meeting
Write a one-page decision doc and share it before any meeting that's supposed to reach a decision. The quality of the conversation will be visibly better.
📊
Track clarification rate
Count how often you get follow-up questions on your documentation. That number — not the length of your documents or the number of pages in your wiki — is the real measure of communication quality.

📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

Atlassian treats communication standards as a product problem — something to design deliberately, not just encourage culturally. Their most practical contributions: treating Jira tickets as documentation, building reusable templates for recurring communication situations, and making Confluence the source of truth rather than meeting minutes or Slack history.

Standardizing the format frees up cognitive capacity for the substance. When engineers have a template for a postmortem, an RFC, or a deployment notice, they spend their energy on the content — not on figuring out how to structure it. This is why templates are not bureaucracy — they're productivity tools.

Communication quality has a measurable signal: how often people need to ask clarifying questions after receiving your documentation. That number — your clarification rate — is a more honest measure of your communication effectiveness than any word count or page count in your wiki.
