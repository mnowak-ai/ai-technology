Lesson 5.4 — Compliance & Policy Awareness

Simulation: Policy Check — Would This Get You Fired?

Read the company AI policy below. Then assess eight real workplace scenarios against it — deciding for each whether the employee is safe, in a gray area, or in direct violation.

Step 1: Read the Policy

This is TechCorp's AI Acceptable Use Policy. Read it carefully — your verdicts should be grounded in what it actually says.

📋
TechCorp — AI Tools Acceptable Use Policy v2.1
Applies to all employees, contractors, and vendors · Effective January 2025
§1 — Approved Tools
1.1 Employees may use AI tools on the IT-approved tools list for work purposes.
1.2 Unapproved tools require written IT and manager approval before use.
1.3 Personal AI accounts must not be used for work tasks involving any company data.
§2 — Data Classification
2.1 Public data (press releases, public docs) — may be used in any approved tool.
2.2 Internal data (internal communications, non-public code, strategy docs) — approved tools with DPA only.
2.3 Confidential data (client data, contracts, PII, financial projections) — requires explicit written Legal + data owner approval before entry into any AI tool.
2.4 Restricted data (patient health data, payment card data, credentials, biometrics) — prohibited in any AI tool, no exceptions.
§3 — Output & Verification
3.1 AI-generated content in client deliverables must be reviewed and verified by a qualified employee before submission.
3.2 Employees are personally responsible for accuracy of work incorporating AI-generated content.
3.3 AI-generated code deployed to production must pass the same review and testing requirements as human-written code.
§4 — Disclosure
4.1 When a client specifically asks whether AI was used, employees must answer accurately and completely.
4.2 Significant AI use in client-facing work should be noted in the project file for audit purposes.
4.3 Employees must not represent AI-generated content as their own original work when directly asked.
§5 — Violations
5.1 Violations of §2.3 or §2.4 are grounds for immediate disciplinary action up to termination.
5.2 Violations of §1.2 without prior written approval are subject to corrective action.

What This Simulation Teaches

Data classification is the controlling variable. Whether an action is safe or a violation almost always depends on data category — public, internal, confidential, restricted — not on how convenient or time-saving the AI use seems. Scenarios 2 and 7 both felt low-risk to the employees involved. Both were violations.

Tool approval and data approval are separate checks. An approved tool doesn't automatically permit all data — §2.2 requires a DPA specifically, §2.3 requires Legal approval regardless of tool status. Scenario 2 used an approved tool with the wrong data category.

Disclosure when directly asked is a separate requirement from appropriate AI use. Scenario 6 involved legitimate AI use — the violation was the lie in response to a direct client question. Transparency obligations (§4.1, §4.3) apply independently of whether the underlying AI use was appropriate.
