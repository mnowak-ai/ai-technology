Lesson 5.2 — Bias & AI Limitations

Facial Recognition and Bias: Why This Technology Fails Some People More Than Others

Facial recognition AI has error rates that vary dramatically by demographic group — and when it's used in high-stakes settings like law enforcement, those errors have life-altering consequences. This is bias you can see in the numbers.

The Numbers From NIST

The National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) is a US government research agency. In 2019, they published one of the most comprehensive studies of facial recognition bias ever conducted — testing 189 different facial recognition algorithms from companies around the world on millions of images.

The findings were stark. False positive rates — cases where the algorithm incorrectly identified someone as a match — were significantly higher for certain demographic groups. The gap wasn't small. For some algorithms, the false positive rate for Black women was up to 100 times higher than for white men. Not 10% worse. Up to 100 times worse.

✅ Lowest Error Rates
~0.1%
White men (middle-aged)
Algorithms performed most accurately on this group — heavily represented in most training datasets
🔴 Highest Error Rates
Up to 34%
Black women, Indigenous women
Up to 100x higher false positive rates in some algorithms — the group least represented in training data

These numbers come from controlled testing. In real-world deployments — lower-quality surveillance cameras, poor lighting, different angles — the gaps are likely even larger. The technology isn't equally reliable for everyone, and it's least reliable for the people who have historically had the least power in encounters with systems that use it.

A technology that fails some people 100 times more often than others isn't a neutral tool. The gap in error rates is itself a form of discrimination — encoded in the algorithm, invisible in the interface.

When Error Rates Meet High Stakes: Three Wrongful Cases

Facial recognition errors are not merely inconvenient when the technology is used in law enforcement. In documented US cases, people have been wrongfully arrested based on facial recognition misidentifications — and the people misidentified have been Black men.

🚨 Wrongful Arrest
Robert Williams — Detroit, Michigan, 2020
Robert Williams, a Black man, was arrested at his home in front of his family after Detroit police used facial recognition to match a shoplifting suspect's image to Williams's driver's license photo. The match was wrong. Williams spent 30 hours in jail before investigators realized they had the wrong person. The ACLU filed a complaint on his behalf. The Detroit police department later suspended the use of facial recognition for arrest warrants.
⚠️ Outcome: Wrongful arrest, 30 hours detained. Charges eventually dropped. First documented US case of wrongful arrest due to facial recognition misidentification.
🚨 Wrongful Arrest
Michael Oliver — New Orleans, Louisiana, 2022
Michael Oliver, a Black man, was charged with robbery based on a facial recognition match. Oliver had been at work — with witnesses — during the time of the alleged robbery. He lost his job due to the arrest, as it violated the terms of his employment. The case was eventually dropped after his defense attorney demonstrated the facial recognition match was incorrect. Oliver sued the city of New Orleans.
⚠️ Outcome: Wrongful arrest, job loss. Civil lawsuit filed. Case highlights how wrongful arrests can cause cascading economic harm even after charges are dropped.

Where Facial Recognition Is Used — And Why Tech Workers Need to Know

This isn't only a law enforcement issue. Facial recognition is increasingly integrated into systems that tech workers build, evaluate, or deploy:

🏢
Building access and security systems
Office buildings and secure facilities use facial recognition for access control. Higher error rates for certain groups means those employees may more frequently be locked out, flagged, or require manual verification.
📱
Device authentication
Face ID and similar features have shown accuracy disparities across skin tones and lighting conditions. For consumer products, this means some users get a worse experience — and less secure authentication — than others.
🏪
Retail loss prevention
Some retailers use facial recognition to flag suspected shoplifters. Higher false positive rates for darker-skinned individuals means those customers are incorrectly flagged more often — creating both discrimination and legal liability.
🛂
Border control and identity verification
Government agencies use facial recognition in passport control, visa processing, and border screening. Accuracy disparities in these contexts can create delays, additional scrutiny, or errors in identity verification for certain travelers.

📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

NIST testing of 189 facial recognition algorithms found false positive rates up to 100 times higher for Black women than for white men. This isn't a fringe finding — it's the documented state of the technology from rigorous government testing.

Accuracy disparities cause real harm when the technology is used in high-stakes settings. Multiple people have been wrongfully arrested in the US based on facial recognition misidentifications — all Black men, in all documented cases.

Overall accuracy is the wrong metric for evaluating facial recognition. The question that matters is whether the system performs equally across the demographic groups it will be used on — and in most documented cases, the answer is no.
