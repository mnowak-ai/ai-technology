Trust or Verify? A Framework for Deciding When to Act on AI Recommendations and When to Check First

The skill isn't knowing AI is fallible — it's knowing when a specific output in a specific context needs verification before action. This article gives you a practical framework for making that call without becoming so skeptical you lose the productivity gains.

The Two Failure Modes This Framework Prevents

Most guidance about AI verification falls into one of two useless extremes: "always trust AI outputs" (dangerous) or "always verify AI outputs" (unworkable). Neither reflects how experienced practitioners actually use these tools.

The practical skill is calibrated verification — knowing which outputs in which contexts need scrutiny, which can be acted on with light review, and which should be overridden regardless of AI confidence. This calibration develops through experience, but it can be accelerated by understanding the factors that should drive it.

📌 Article Summary

What to Carry Forward

The practical skill is calibrated verification — applying scrutiny proportionally to risk, not uniformly to all outputs. Maximum skepticism eliminates productivity gains; zero skepticism creates professional risk.

Four factors drive verification effort: consequence of being wrong (stakes), reversibility of the action, how novel the output is relative to training patterns, and your organizational knowledge of the specific context.

Confidence scores are an input to the decision, not the decision itself. High confidence in a high-consequence, irreversible action still requires verification. Low confidence in a low-stakes, easily reversible action may be acceptable to act on.
