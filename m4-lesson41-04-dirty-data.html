Lesson 4.1 — Data Literacy for Tech Roles

Dirty Data: What Incomplete Records, Duplicates, and Conflicting Formats Actually Cost a Tech Team

The video showed three data challenges on one slide. This article shows you what each one actually looks like in a real dataset, what it costs when it goes undetected, and what you can do about it — including what happens when AI gets fed dirty data.

What "Dirty Data" Actually Means

Dirty data is any data that has quality problems that make it unreliable for analysis, reporting, or AI processing. It's not exotic or rare — every real tech environment has dirty data. The question isn't whether your organization has it. The question is whether it gets caught before it causes problems.

The three most common types are incomplete data (missing values in fields that should have them), duplicate records (the same event or entity appearing multiple times), and conflicting formats (the same kind of information stored differently across records or systems). Each one looks different, causes different problems, and requires different remediation.

Most data quality problems don't announce themselves loudly. They hide inside datasets that look superficially normal. A report is generated, a metric is calculated, an AI makes a routing decision — and the dirty data underneath produces a wrong result that no one notices until something breaks or a decision turns out to be wrong.

AI doesn't compensate for dirty data. It amplifies it — applying patterns learned from bad records to millions of new decisions, faster than any human could. Clean data is not a technical nicety. It's a safety requirement.

🕳️
Incomplete Data
Records with missing values in fields that should be populated. The data exists — an event happened, a ticket was created — but one or more fields weren't filled in, leaving gaps that affect analysis.

TKT-8820 has no category. TKT-8821 and TKT-8823 are missing category, priority, assignee, and resolution time — they were likely created but never properly filled out.

What this costs in practice
AI routing
Tickets missing a category field can't be routed by category. The AI either skips them, assigns them to a default queue, or makes a guess based on the description alone — often incorrectly.
SLA reporting
If resolution time is null, those tickets are excluded from average resolution time calculations — artificially improving the metric. If they're included as zero, they artificially lower it. Either way, the metric is wrong.
AI training
If this dataset is used to train an AI classifier, records missing the category label are useless or actively harmful — the AI can't learn what kind of ticket it is if that field isn't filled in.
Workload analysis
Tickets without an assigned agent don't show up in anyone's workload report. Managers think some tickets are assigned when they're not — tickets sit in limbo.
✅ How incomplete data gets caught and fixed
• Required fields: Configure the ticketing system to require category and priority before a ticket can be saved — prevents most gaps at creation time
• Validation rules: Run weekly checks that flag any ticket older than 24 hours with null required fields — creates a human review queue for cleanup
• AI-assisted backfill: For legacy incomplete records, AI can infer likely categories from the ticket description — but human review of AI-inferred values is required before treating them as authoritative
• Monitor the data, not just the metrics: When a metric improves unexpectedly, check whether the improvement is real or whether incomplete records are being excluded from the calculation
👯
Duplicate Records
The same event, entity, or transaction appears multiple times in the dataset. Duplicates inflate counts, skew metrics, cause AI systems to over-learn certain patterns, and result in double-actions in automated workflows.

SN-88291 appears twice under the same owner — probably imported twice. SN-77104 appears twice but with different owners — a data entry error from when the device was reassigned. SN-91002 exists as both active and decommissioned.

What this costs in practice
Security audits
A decommissioned device that still appears as "active" in the inventory passes security checks it should fail. If the physical device was resold or lost, its credentials may still appear valid to automated access control systems.
Automated workflows
An automated offboarding workflow that deactivates all assets assigned to a departing employee will trigger twice for any duplicated device — potentially sending two deactivation requests, two notifications, two access revocations.
Budget and procurement
If the asset count is inflated by duplicates, the team appears to have more equipment than it does — leading to incorrect refresh cycle planning and potential procurement shortfalls.
AI training
Duplicate records make some patterns appear more common than they are. An AI trained on ticket data with duplicates will over-learn those ticket types, classifying more future tickets into them than warranted.
✅ How duplicates get caught and fixed
• Unique constraints: Configure the system to enforce that serial numbers must be unique — prevents two records with the same SN from existing simultaneously
• Deduplication scripts: Automated scripts that regularly compare records against each other and flag probable duplicates for human review
• AI-assisted deduplication: For large datasets, AI tools can identify probable duplicates based on fuzzy matching — catching records where names are spelled differently or serial numbers have typos
• Change process discipline: Many duplicates are created during data migrations or system integrations — requiring validation checks at the point of import prevents them from entering the system
🔀
Conflicting Formats
The same type of information stored in different formats across records. Dates as "11/14/2024" in some records and "2024-11-14" in others. Priorities as "1", "High", "P1", and "CRITICAL" all meaning the same thing. To a computer, these are entirely different values.

The date field has at least 5 different formats — some ambiguous (is 11/13 November 13 or the 11th of an unlabeled month?). Priority uses "HIGH", "P1", "1", "CRITICAL", and "urgent" for what may all be the same level. Resolution time is in hours, minutes, hours+minutes, and seconds across different records.

What this costs in practice
Sorting and filtering
Filtering for "HIGH" priority tickets misses "P1", "CRITICAL", and "urgent" — which may all mean the same thing. A query for high-priority tickets returned in under 4 hours would give a completely wrong answer if formats are mixed.
Date ambiguity
"13/11/2024" is unambiguous. "11/13/2024" could be November 13 (US format) or the 11th of the 13th month (impossible, so probably November 11 in DD/MM format). When dates are ambiguous, trend analysis across time is unreliable.
AI classification training
If priority labels are inconsistent, the AI learns that "P1", "CRITICAL", "urgent", and "1" are different categories — and distributes future tickets across all of them rather than routing everything with those labels as high-priority.
System integrations
When data is exported from one system to another, format conflicts cause parsing failures. A receiving system expecting dates in YYYY-MM-DD format will error out on "Nov 12, 2024" — potentially dropping those records entirely from the integration.
✅ How format conflicts get caught and fixed
• Standardized input validation: Require date pickers (not free text) for date fields, dropdown menus for priority levels — prevents format variation at entry time
• Data normalization scripts: Automated scripts that transform all values in a field to a single standard format — converting "P1", "1", "CRITICAL", "urgent" all to "HIGH" based on a defined mapping
• Schema documentation: Define and document the accepted format for every field — so that when data is imported from external systems or migrated from old platforms, the format requirements are explicit
• Integration testing: Before connecting two systems, run sample data through the integration pipeline and verify that formats are correctly parsed on both ends — format conflicts discovered in testing are far cheaper than those discovered in production

How One Dirty Record Cascades Through a System

The danger of dirty data isn't just one wrong record. It's what happens as that record flows through a pipeline. Click through this sequence to see how a single incomplete ticket corrupts multiple downstream outputs.

⚡ Cascade Simulation
One missing field. Five downstream problems.
A new ticket (TKT-9001) is created without a category field. Click "Next" to watch the impact propagate through the pipeline.
1
TKT-9001 created — Category field left blank
A user submits a ticket. They're in a hurry and skip the category dropdown. The system doesn't require it, so the ticket saves successfully with category = null.
2
AI routing receives the ticket
The automated routing system checks the category field to determine which team to assign the ticket to. Category = null. The routing rule doesn't have a team for "null." The ticket goes to a default catch-all queue — general IT support — even though it's a high-priority security issue.
3
SLA clock starts — but nobody's watching
The general IT queue has a 24-hour SLA. The security team's SLA for this type of issue is 2 hours. Because the ticket was miscategorized, it's being held to the wrong standard. 2 hours pass. The real deadline expires. No alert fires.
4
Weekly metrics report generated
The automated weekly report pulls all tickets. TKT-9001 is excluded from category-based breakdowns because its category is null. It's included in total volume count. The security category shows fewer tickets than actually occurred — making the security team's workload appear lighter than it is. Management sees no reason to add security support capacity.
5
Monthly AI model retraining
The ticket classification AI is retrained monthly on new ticket data. TKT-9001 has no category label, so it's excluded from training. But similar tickets in the future will also lack category context — the AI never learns that this description pattern maps to the security category. The problem compounds with each training cycle.

Your Data Quality Validation Checklist

These are the practical habits that tech workers use to catch dirty data before it causes problems. Check each one off as you review it — these are the behaviors that distinguish workers who catch problems early from those who discover them after a report went to the wrong person.

✓
Before distributing any report, spot-check 5 records in the underlying data
Find the source data the report was built from and manually look at a few records. Does the data match what the report is claiming? Are there null values in key fields? Do the totals add up?
✓
Question unexpected metric improvements before celebrating them
When a metric improves suddenly, ask why before accepting it at face value. Did something genuinely get better, or did data quality change (incomplete records being excluded, duplicate resolutions being counted, a category definition shift)?
✓
When validating AI outputs, look for patterns in where it's wrong
AI doesn't usually get random errors — it gets systematic ones. If you notice the AI consistently miscategorizes a certain ticket type, or always misroutes tickets from a certain team, there's likely a data quality issue in how those records were labeled in the training data.
✓
Flag data quality issues — don't silently work around them
When you notice a format inconsistency, a pattern of missing fields, or probable duplicates, report it. A workaround you apply quietly (manually re-categorizing tickets before running the report) doesn't fix the underlying problem and doesn't scale when you're not there.
✓
After any system migration or integration, run a data validation check before going live
System migrations are the most common source of dirty data. Records imported from one system often lose field mappings, introduce format conflicts, or create duplicates. Validate a sample before declaring the migration complete.
🧠 Quick Check
A help desk team deploys an AI tool to predict which tickets will breach SLA so they can prioritize them. After two weeks, the team notices the AI rarely predicts SLA breaches for the "Integration Issues" ticket category, even though that category has the worst historical SLA performance. You investigate the training data and find that Integration Issues tickets historically had the resolution_time field left blank at a much higher rate than other categories. What most likely explains the AI's poor performance on this category?
📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

The three most common data quality problems are incomplete data (missing values in required fields), duplicate records (the same entity or event appearing multiple times), and conflicting formats (the same kind of information stored in different formats across records). Each causes different downstream problems and requires different remediation.

Dirty data doesn't just produce one wrong output — it cascades. A single missing category field can cause wrong routing, missed SLA alerts, incorrect metrics, misleading reports, and corrupted AI training data. The further downstream a problem travels before it's caught, the more expensive it is to fix.

AI amplifies data quality problems rather than compensating for them. An AI trained on data with systematic missing values, duplicates, or format conflicts will learn wrong patterns and apply them at scale — faster than any human could. Catching data quality issues before they reach AI systems is one of the most important responsibilities in any tech role that works with AI tools.
