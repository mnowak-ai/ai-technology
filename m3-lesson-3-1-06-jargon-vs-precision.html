Lesson 3.1 — Clear Technical Communication

Jargon vs. Precision: When Technical Terminology Helps and When It Hurts

"The p99 latency exceeded the SLO threshold" is more precise than "the system was slow." But the same sentence sent to the wrong audience creates confusion and risk. Here's how to know the difference.

The Same Word Can Be Precision or Jargon

Technical terminology isn't inherently good or bad. The same term that gives an SRE a precise, unambiguous handle on a problem becomes exclusionary noise when it crosses an audience boundary — reaching someone who doesn't share the specialized vocabulary.

"p99 latency" is precision when you're writing for an SRE team. It's jargon when you're writing a customer status update. The difference isn't the word — it's whether the reader shares the vocabulary required to decode it without looking anything up.

Jargon is not wrong terminology. It's the right terminology used with the wrong audience — or used to signal expertise rather than to communicate it.

When Technical Terminology Is Precision (Use It)

Technical terms earn their place when they do one or more of the following things that plain language cannot do as well.

✅ Use it when
It's more specific than plain language
The term captures a precise meaning that a plain-language description would either lengthen considerably or make ambiguous.
"p99 latency exceeded 4s" vs. "the slowest 1% of requests took more than 4 seconds" — both work, but the first is 5 words and unambiguous to the audience.
✅ Use it when
The audience shares the vocabulary
All readers of this document will recognize this term without looking it up. Using it saves time and signals shared context.
Writing "the pod went into CrashLoopBackOff" in a Kubernetes runbook for SREs who work with Kubernetes daily — this is their native vocabulary.
✅ Use it when
Precision is operationally required
The distinction between similar-sounding terms matters for the action the reader needs to take. Using a vaguer term would lose that operational distinction.
"TCP timeout" vs. "connection timeout" vs. "read timeout" — these require different fixes. Collapsing them into "connection problem" loses the information needed to debug.
✅ Use it when
The document will be searched by experts
Technical terms in documentation and runbooks are search anchors. Engineers search for "OOMKilled" not "the pod ran out of memory." Using the precise term makes the document findable.
Postmortems and runbooks should use the exact error message, flag name, or technical term so that engineers searching under incident pressure can find the right document.

When Technical Terminology Is Jargon (Translate It)

❌ Translate it when
It crosses an audience boundary
The document will be read by someone who doesn't share the specialist vocabulary — executives, other departments, customers, or junior engineers new to the domain.
An executive summary that says "the SLO breach triggered a P1 and the SRE team initiated a rollback after identifying the root cause in the DORA metrics dashboard" has lost its audience by the third word.
❌ Translate it when
It signals expertise instead of communicating
The term isn't chosen for precision — it's chosen to sound technical. A plainer term would communicate just as precisely but wouldn't advertise knowledge.
"We need to leverage our MLOps infrastructure to operationalize the model artifact via our CD pipeline" — most of these words are adding noise, not precision.
❌ Translate it when
The acronym is undefined in context
Abbreviations and acronyms that aren't defined on first use force readers to stop and look things up — or to keep reading while misunderstanding the document.
A postmortem that refers to "the ETL job failure" in the first line, without ever defining ETL, loses any reader who isn't already familiar with the pipeline.
❌ Translate it when
The term has different meanings across teams
Some words mean different things to different engineering disciplines. Using them without definition assumes a specific meaning that may not be shared.
"Pipeline" means an ML training pipeline to an ML engineer, a CI/CD pipeline to a platform engineer, and a data pipeline to a data engineer. In a cross-team doc, define which one.

The Audience Boundary Problem

The riskiest moment for technical jargon is when a document crosses from one professional community to another. This happens constantly in IT: engineering teams communicate with product, legal, security, executive, and customer-facing teams. Each crossing requires vocabulary recalibration.

Example: Communicating an ML Model Issue
Cross-team boundary
❌ Jargon crossing the boundary
The fraud detection model's precision on OOD inputs has degraded significantly since the last retrain. The drift in the feature distribution is causing the classifier to produce high-confidence false positives on transactions from new merchant categories. We need to schedule an emergency retrain with updated SMOTE-balanced data.
✅ Translated for the product team
Our fraud detection model is incorrectly flagging legitimate transactions as fraudulent — specifically purchases from merchants in categories we added recently (grocery delivery, EV charging stations). This is happening because the model wasn't trained on these new merchant types. We're seeing about 3x more false fraud flags than normal in these categories. We need to retrain the model with current transaction data — this will take 4–5 days. In the meantime, we recommend manual review for flagged transactions in those two categories.
What changed — and what didn't
The technical facts are identical. What changed: "OOD inputs" → "new merchant categories"; "feature distribution drift" → "the model wasn't trained on these new types"; "SMOTE-balanced retrain" → "retrain the model with current data." The plain version also added what the product team actually needs to act on: the scale of the problem (3x more flags), the timeline (4–5 days), and an interim mitigation. Technical precision for a technical audience — operational clarity for everyone else.
Example: Security Advisory to Non-Technical Staff
Cross-team boundary
❌ Jargon crossing the boundary
A zero-day RCE vulnerability has been identified in the version of OpenSSL we're running. CVSS score 9.8. Patch is available but requires downtime. Unpatched systems are vulnerable to arbitrary code execution by unauthenticated remote attackers. IT will be deploying the patch during the maintenance window.
✅ Translated for non-technical staff
We've discovered a serious security flaw in software running on our servers. If not fixed, an outside attacker could potentially access or damage our systems without needing a password. We're applying a fix this weekend during scheduled maintenance. During the maintenance window (Saturday 2–4am), some internal tools will be briefly unavailable. No action is required from you — IT is handling the fix. We'll send a confirmation once the patch is applied.
What the non-technical staff actually needs
"RCE vulnerability," "CVSS 9.8," and "unauthenticated remote attackers" are precise and meaningful to a security engineer. They are noise to a marketing manager. What the marketing manager needs: is this serious? (yes), do I need to do anything? (no), will anything I use be affected? (briefly, here's when). The translated version answers all three questions without requiring any security literacy.

📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

Technical terminology is precision when the audience shares the vocabulary. It becomes jargon — and a risk — the moment it crosses an audience boundary. The same term can be required for one reader and exclusionary for another. Know which side of the boundary your document is on.

The riskiest jargon-boundary crossings in IT happen when engineering teams communicate with legal, executive, customer-facing, or cross-functional teams. These are moments when operational decisions depend on non-engineers understanding technical facts accurately — and jargon creates a gap between what was written and what was understood.

When in doubt, use technical terms in technical documents and define them on first use in cross-audience documents. The practical test: could someone in this audience act on this document correctly without looking anything up? If not, either define the terms or replace them.
