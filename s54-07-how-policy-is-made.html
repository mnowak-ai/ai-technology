Lesson 5.4 — Compliance & Policy Awareness

How Organizations Create AI Policy: What Tech Workers Should Know About Where the Rules Come From

Most employees encounter policy as a finished document — a set of rules that seem to have appeared from nowhere. Understanding how policies are actually created changes your relationship with them: from rules imposed on you to shared protections you can participate in shaping.

Policy Doesn't Come From Nowhere

When you encounter an AI acceptable use policy at work, it feels like a set of rules handed down from above. But every word in that policy was written by a person — usually several people from different departments — who were responding to a specific set of risks, incidents, regulatory pressures, or observed problems.

Understanding that policies are human-made responses to real-world concerns makes them feel less arbitrary — and makes it easier to apply them with judgment rather than just technically complying with their letter.

The rule "don't put client data in an AI prompt" didn't come from someone's imagination. It came from a world where someone did put client data in an AI prompt, something went wrong, and an organization decided to formalize what "reasonable care" means.

How an AI Policy Gets Made: The Five-Stage Process

⚡
A risk or incident creates urgency
Most AI policies start with a triggering event — an employee using an unapproved tool in a way that created legal exposure, a data breach, a regulatory inquiry, a documented case from another company that made leadership nervous, or an industry incident that made the news. The Samsung ChatGPT leak prompted AI policies at dozens of companies within weeks. A real event creates the pressure to formalize guidance that may have previously been informal.
🔍
Legal and compliance assess the exposure
Once a risk is identified, legal and compliance teams map out the exposure — what laws apply, what existing policies say (or don't say), what the worst-case scenarios are, and what other organizations have done. This stage often involves reviewing industry best practices, regulatory guidance, and peer company policies.
🛠️
IT and security define what's technically possible
Policy writers need to know what can actually be enforced technically. IT assesses which tools are in use, which can be monitored, what data classification systems exist, and what controls are technically feasible. A policy that's impossible to audit is harder to enforce and easier to violate unknowingly.
✍️
The policy is drafted and reviewed
Drafting involves multiple stakeholders — often Legal, IT, HR, Compliance, and sometimes business unit leads who represent the people the policy will affect. This stage is where employee feedback — formal or informal — can genuinely influence what the final policy says. The best policies are written with input from the people who will have to follow them.
🔄
Policy is published, trained on, and updated
A policy that nobody knows about isn't a policy. Publication is followed by employee notification and usually training. And because AI is a fast-moving field, good AI policies include a review cycle — a scheduled date when the policy will be reconsidered in light of new technology, new incidents, and new regulatory guidance.

Who Actually Writes AI Policy

⚖️
Legal Team
Maps regulatory requirements, drafts legally protective language, reviews for liability exposure, and ensures the policy is defensible if challenged.
Contributes: The legal floor — what must be covered to avoid liability
🔒
IT / Security
Defines what's technically enforceable, identifies which tools are approved and why, and sets data classification requirements.
Contributes: The technical reality — what can and can't be monitored or controlled
📋
Compliance
Ensures the policy aligns with industry regulations, frameworks, and audit requirements. In regulated industries, compliance often drives the strictest requirements.
Contributes: Regulatory alignment — what external standards require
👥
Business Unit Leaders
Represent the practical needs of the teams that will use AI tools. They push back on requirements that are unworkable and provide real-world context for what employees actually need to do their jobs.
Contributes: Workability — whether the policy is actually followable

Notice who's often missing from this list: the individual contributors — developers, analysts, writers — who will be using the AI tools day-to-day. This is why direct feedback from employees who encounter policy gaps is valuable. Policy writers often don't know what they don't know about how AI tools are actually being used.

Your Role in Shaping Policy — Even as a Junior Employee

💬
Report gaps when you find them
If you're using an AI tool for a legitimate work task and there's no policy guidance about it, that gap is worth reporting. Your observation that "we have no policy about using AI for X" is useful information for the policy writers who may not know this use case exists.
📝
Participate in policy feedback processes
When your organization circulates a draft policy for comment — or runs a training session that invites feedback — engage with it. Your on-the-ground perspective about what's workable and what creates friction is exactly what policy writers need.
🚩
Flag policies that create perverse incentives
Sometimes a policy, well-intentioned, creates an incentive to use a worse but technically compliant option instead of a better but less clearly permitted one. That feedback — "this policy is making us use X when Y would be safer" — is valuable and should be surfaced through appropriate channels.
🧠 Quick Check
A company's AI policy was written 18 months ago and hasn't been updated. The policy doesn't mention large language model APIs — only "AI-powered applications." A developer wants to use an LLM API in a new internal tool. What's the most professionally responsible interpretation?
📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

AI policies are written by humans responding to real risks — incidents, regulatory pressure, and observed problems. Understanding this makes it easier to apply policy with judgment: to ask "what risk was this designed to prevent?" rather than "can I technically work around this?"
