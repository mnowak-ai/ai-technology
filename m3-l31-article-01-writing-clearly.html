Writing for Healthcare Professionals: How to Be Clear Without Being Condescending

Healthcare documentation isn't about sounding authoritative — it's about getting the right information to the right person in a form they can act on. These are two very different goals, and only one of them keeps patients safe.

There's a particular kind of bad writing that healthcare generates at industrial scale: documentation that is technically complete, organizationally correct, and completely useless. The kind that buries the critical finding in paragraph four. The kind where the recommended action is somewhere in the passive voice around sentence six. The kind that a nurse picking up a new patient at shift change has to read three times to figure out what she's supposed to do next.

This isn't a literacy problem or a time problem. It's a clarity problem — and it has a specific cause: writing that treats documentation as record-keeping rather than communication. When the goal is "put it in the chart," the writing looks different than when the goal is "give the next clinician exactly what she needs to take over care safely."

In AI-era healthcare, this distinction is more important than ever. AI documentation tools generate text that is technically complete. What they don't automatically generate is text that is organized for the reader who will act on it. That editing job belongs to you.

Clarity is not simplification. A well-written progress note for a patient with cardiorenal syndrome can be dense with clinical detail — and still be crystal clear about what happened, what was done, and what needs to happen next.

Six Principles of Clear Healthcare Writing

🎯
1. Lead with the action or finding
The most important information goes first. In a handoff note, that's the thing the receiving clinician must act on. In an incident report, that's what happened. In a patient message, that's what the patient needs to do today.
⏱️
2. Make time explicit
"Recently" is not a time. "Yesterday" is not a time. "At 14:30 on 12/4" is a time. "Three days before admission" is a time. Clinical documentation without explicit times creates ambiguity that can harm patients during transitions of care.
👤
3. Assign every action to a person
"The patient was notified" — by whom? "Medications were administered" — by which nurse, when, via which route? Active voice with a named subject is not just cleaner writing. It is accountable documentation.
✂️
4. One sentence, one idea
Clinical sentences that contain three subordinate clauses require the reader to hold multiple pieces of information in working memory simultaneously. Under time pressure, this is how things get missed. Write one idea per sentence.
🔍
5. Specific over general
"The patient appeared uncomfortable" is a general observation. "The patient rated pain 8/10 and was diaphoretic, tachycardic at 108 bpm" is a specific clinical picture. Specificity is what makes documentation useful for the next provider — not the one who wrote it.
🚫
6. Cut the throat-clearing
"This document has been prepared in order to provide information regarding..." Delete everything before the first sentence that contains actual information. In healthcare documentation, the opening sentence should contain the most important clinical fact — not an announcement that documentation exists.

Before and After: The Same Information, Done Right

These rewrites don't add or remove clinical content — they reorganize and rewrite it according to the six principles above. The information is identical. The usefulness is not.

Rewrite Example 1
ICU Handoff Note
❌ Before
Patient is a 67-year-old male with a history of CHF and CKD who was admitted earlier this week and has been experiencing some difficulties with his fluid balance. The team has been monitoring his creatinine which has been trending in a concerning direction. There have been some adjustments to his diuretic regimen. His family has been informed of his condition. At some point during the shift there were some changes to his vitals that the team was made aware of.
✓ After
Urgent: Creatinine rising — hold furosemide pending renal consult.

Mr. Torres, 67M, CHF + CKD Stage 3, admitted 12/2 for acute decompensated heart failure.

Current concern: Cr trending up (1.4 → 1.9 → 2.3 over 48h). Urology consulted at 16:00 today — Dr. Kim to call back by 20:00. Furosemide 40mg IV held as of 18:30 per Dr. Park's order.

Vitals at 18:00: BP 142/88, HR 94, O2 96% on 2L. Hemodynamically stable.

Family (daughter Maria, 917-555-0182) briefed at 17:00 on creatinine concern. She will call tonight — please update her.
What changed and why it matters
The action item (hold furosemide) now leads, not buries — the covering physician knows what she must not do before she reads anything else
Creatinine trend shown as actual values with timeframe — not "trending in a concerning direction"
Every action has a named person (Dr. Kim, Dr. Park) and a time (16:00, 18:30, 20:00)
Phone numbers provided — "call your doctor" when the patient doesn't have the number in front of them is not an instruction. The number is the instruction.
Rewrite Example 2
AI-Generated Note — Corrected for Clarity
❌ AI Generated
The patient presented to the clinic today for a follow-up visit. During the visit, the patient's current medications were reviewed and it was determined that some adjustments may be necessary. The patient was counseled regarding the importance of medication adherence. The patient's blood pressure was measured and found to be elevated. The physician discussed the patient's condition and the potential need for additional intervention. A follow-up appointment was scheduled.
✓ Corrected
Follow-up visit 12/6. BP 158/96 (elevated; goal <130/80).

Plan change: Lisinopril increased from 10mg to 20mg daily. Patient instructed to monitor BP at home daily and call if readings exceed 170 systolic.

Adherence: Patient reports missing doses 2–3x/week due to forgetting. Discussed pill organizer and phone reminders. Patient agreed to try. [Note: AI draft corrected — original stated "counseled regarding importance of adherence" without documenting the specific barrier or intervention discussed. RN Smith, 12/6 at 14:45]
The AI correction note
The corrected version adds something the original lacked: a bracketed note explaining what the AI got wrong and what the human reviewer added. This is best practice when correcting AI-generated notes. It protects the next reader who may not know the note was AI-generated, and it creates a record of the human reviewer's clinical judgment.

📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

Clarity in healthcare writing is about removing obstacles between the reader and the action they need to take — not dumbing information down. The reader is often busy, distracted, and acting on your documentation under time pressure. Every word that delays the key information is a risk.

The six principles (lead with the action, make time explicit, assign every action to a person, one sentence one idea, specific over general, cut throat-clearing) apply across every documentation type — from incident reports to AI-generated notes to patient-facing discharge instructions.

When correcting AI-generated documentation, document the correction inline and briefly explain what the AI got wrong. This protects future readers who may not know the note was AI-generated, and creates a record of the human reviewer's clinical judgment.

Clarity is not the same as simplicity. A clear progress note for a cardiorenal syndrome patient contains more clinical information than a vague one — it is organized differently, not stripped of complexity. The goal is organized precision, not reduction.
