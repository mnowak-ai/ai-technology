Lesson 5.2 — Bias & AI Limitations

Biased by Design: How Training Data Shapes What AI Believes

AI models don't form opinions on their own. They learn from data — and data is a record of the past. Understanding what that means for today's systems is one of the most important things a tech professional can know.

The Simplest Way to Understand This

Imagine you wanted to teach a child what a "typical family" looks like. You give them 10,000 photographs of families from American TV shows between 1950 and 1980. They study every image carefully. Then you ask them to draw a "typical family."

The child draws a white family with two parents, a house, a car, and a dog. They're not wrong — that's what 90% of the images showed. But the dataset was not a picture of reality. It was a picture of what TV producers in that era chose to show. And the child learned from that picture, not from the actual world.

This is exactly what happens with AI. The model learns from whatever data it's given — and that data was created by people, at a specific time in history, with all of the gaps, assumptions, and blind spots that come with that. The model doesn't know what was left out. It only knows what it saw.

Data is not a mirror of reality. It's a mirror of what was recorded, by whom, about whom, and when. AI learns from that recording — not from reality itself.

Three Problems With Training Data — Every One of Them Creates Bias

1
The data is incomplete
No dataset covers everything equally. Some people, languages, regions, and situations are heavily represented — and others barely appear at all. The AI performs best on what it saw most and worst on what it saw least. This isn't a flaw in the AI's logic. It's a direct consequence of what was included in the training set.
📌 Real consequence: AI speech recognition systems were shown to have higher error rates for women and people with accents — because the original voice training datasets included far more recordings of American men speaking in standard accents.
2
The data reflects past assumptions
Data from the past carries the assumptions, values, and norms of the time it was created. In many domains, those past assumptions included formal or informal discrimination — who got loans, who got hired, who was diagnosed with certain conditions, who was seen as creditworthy. When AI learns from this data, it inherits those assumptions and applies them to new decisions as if they were neutral facts.
📌 Real consequence: Recidivism prediction tools (AI used to predict the likelihood of someone reoffending after prison) were found to be significantly more likely to flag Black defendants as high risk than white defendants with similar criminal histories — because the tools were trained on historical criminal justice data that itself reflected racial disparities in policing and prosecution.
3
Some groups are underrepresented
Even when no one intends to exclude anyone, certain groups end up with less representation in training data — because they had less access to the internet, less participation in the systems that generated the data, or were systematically overlooked by the people who built those systems. Less representation means less accurate performance for those users — and in critical applications, that can mean real harm.
📌 Real consequence: Early facial recognition systems had error rates close to zero for white male faces — and error rates as high as 34% for darker-skinned women's faces. The difference wasn't in the algorithm's design. It was in who was in the training data.

Where This Shows Up in the Real World Today

Training data bias isn't theoretical. It produces documented, measurable harm in the systems that tech workers build and use right now:

🏥 Healthcare
Diagnostic AI trained on incomplete patient populations
Algorithms trained primarily on data from white patients have shown lower accuracy when applied to patients of other backgrounds. Skin condition detection AI, trained mostly on lighter skin tones, performs significantly worse on darker skin — affecting diagnosis quality for millions of patients.
💼 Hiring
Résumé screening tools that learned from biased history
Amazon's AI hiring tool (see Article 5.2.5) is the most documented case — but it's not isolated. Multiple studies have found that AI screening tools trained on historical hiring data tend to replicate the demographic patterns of who was historically hired, regardless of actual qualifications.
⚖️ Criminal Justice
Risk assessment tools with racially disparate outcomes
COMPAS and similar risk-scoring tools — used in some US courts to inform bail and sentencing — have been shown by researchers to produce significantly different error rates by race. The tools weren't programmed to be biased; they learned from a criminal justice system that already had documented disparities.
🌐 Language & Translation
NLP models that underperform for non-Western languages
Large language models are trained overwhelmingly on English-language internet content. Languages with smaller digital footprints — including most African languages, indigenous languages, and many regional Asian languages — are dramatically underrepresented, leading to significantly worse AI performance for those speakers.

A Brief History: How We Got Here

The data bias problem didn't happen because of bad intentions. It happened because of the conditions under which data was collected — and understanding the history helps explain why these patterns are so persistent:

1950s–
1980s
The foundation data is created
The records that would eventually become AI training data — medical studies, employment records, financial data, census data, criminal records — are collected during this era. Access to record-keeping systems, clinical trials, financial services, and public institutions was not equal. Women were often excluded from medical research studies as recently as the 1980s. Minority communities faced documented discrimination in lending, housing, and criminal justice.
⚠️ Historical impact: The gaps, disparities, and inequities of this era get encoded into the data that will later be used to train AI systems
1990s–
2000s
The internet creates a new kind of dataset
The rise of the internet creates massive new data sources — text, images, forum posts, news articles, and more. This becomes a primary training source for AI models. But internet access in these early decades was not evenly distributed. Users were disproportionately English-speaking, Western, educated, and male. The resulting dataset reflects those demographics heavily.
📅 Modern consequence: Large language models trained heavily on this internet data tend to reflect Western, English-speaking assumptions as a default
2010s
AI systems go live with the old data inside them
Commercial AI systems begin making real decisions that affect real people — in hiring, lending, criminal justice, healthcare, and more. The bias in the training data becomes visible as researchers and journalists begin documenting systematic disparities in how these systems perform for different groups.
⚠️ Turning point: Studies like the MIT Media Lab's "Gender Shades" research (2018) publicly demonstrate large performance gaps in commercial facial recognition across gender and skin tone
Now
The field knows the problem — and is working on it, slowly
AI teams at major companies and research institutions are actively working on methods to detect, measure, and reduce bias in training data and model outputs. But progress is uneven. Many commercial systems still carry the biases of their original training data. And new systems trained on AI-generated content risk compounding the problem — inheriting the biases of previous models.
📌 Your role: As a tech professional, understanding this history is what lets you ask the right questions when you're building, evaluating, or deploying AI systems

A startup builds a credit scoring AI trained on 15 years of loan approval data from a regional bank. The tool is technically accurate — it predicts default rates well overall. But researchers find it consistently assigns lower scores to applicants from certain zip codes. What most likely explains this?
📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

Training data is not a neutral record of reality. It reflects who collected it, who was included in it, and what assumptions were embedded in the systems that generated it. AI faithfully learns from that record — including the parts that were unfair.

The three structural problems: incomplete coverage (some groups are barely represented), past assumptions baked in (historical discrimination encoded in historical data), and systematic underrepresentation of marginalized communities in the systems that generated the data.

Accuracy overall does not mean fairness for everyone. A model can have high accuracy on average while performing significantly worse for underrepresented groups — which is exactly when the harm is hardest to detect.

Tech workers who understand this history are equipped to ask the right questions: Who was in the training data? How was it collected? Does this model perform equally well across demographic groups? Asking these questions before deployment is what separates responsible AI use from a problem waiting to happen.
