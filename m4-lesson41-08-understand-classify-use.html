Lesson 4.1 — Data Literacy for Tech Roles

The Understand, Classify, Use Framework: A Practical Workflow for Handling Data in Any Tech Role

The video closed with this three-step model. It deserves its own treatment — walked through a full scenario so you can use it as a mental checklist, not just recall it as a concept.

Why a Framework Matters Here

Data literacy is a set of skills — but skills need a decision structure to become habits. The Understand, Classify, Use framework gives you a sequence: three questions to ask about any data you encounter before you do anything with it.

The framework is most useful when you're encountering data you didn't set up yourself — a new dataset, a data source you're inheriting from a colleague, data flowing into an AI system you're validating, or raw data you need to feed into a report or automation workflow. Skipping any of the three steps creates predictable, avoidable failures.

Step 1
Understand
Know what the data actually is before you do anything with it. Where did it come from? What does each field mean? How was it collected? Is it current?
What is the source of this data?
What time period does it cover?
What does each field represent?
Are there known quality issues?
Who collected it, and how?
Step 2
Classify
Determine what type of data it is and what constraints apply to it. Is it structured, unstructured, or semi-structured? Is any of it private or sensitive?
Structured, unstructured, or semi-structured?
Does it contain PII or sensitive information?
What access controls apply?
What's the appropriate use context?
Can it be shared? With whom?
Step 3
Use
Apply the data to the task at hand with full awareness of its strengths, its limitations, and the boundaries of appropriate use.
Is the data good enough for this use?
What limitations should I communicate?
What validation is needed before acting?
What shouldn't be done with this data?
Who needs to review before this is shared?

Walked Through: An IT Support Analyst's Real Scenario

📋 Full Scenario Walkthrough
Applying UCU to a real data task
Click each phase to walk through how an IT support analyst applies the framework when asked to feed ticket data into a new AI routing tool.
📌 The situation
Your manager asks you to export the last 12 months of support tickets from the ticketing system and upload them to a new AI routing tool the team is piloting. The tool will use this data to train its routing model. You have the export ready — a CSV file with 14,000 rows. Your manager says "just upload it and we'll see how it goes."
🔍
Step 1 — Understand
What is this data actually?
▾

Before uploading anything, you open the CSV and actually look at it. You find:

• Tickets span 14 months, not 12 — the export included tickets from before the current ticketing system was deployed, which had a different category taxonomy

• The category field has 23 unique values, but 6 of them appear to be legacy categories that were retired 8 months ago and shouldn't appear in new tickets

• About 11% of records have a blank category field — these are tickets that were auto-imported from email and never manually categorized

• The "resolved_by" field contains both employee names and employee IDs inconsistently — a format conflict from a system migration 6 months ago

What "Understand" surfaces
The data isn't what you thought it was. The 12-month export includes legacy data with a different schema, has significant completeness issues, and has a format conflict in a key field. Uploading this without addressing these issues would train the AI on wrong patterns.
🏷️
Step 2 — Classify
What type of data is this, and what are the constraints?
▾

You classify the data along the dimensions that matter for this use:

Structure: Primarily structured (rows and columns with defined fields), with some unstructured elements (the free-text "description" and "notes" fields). The AI tool will use both.

Sensitivity: The dataset contains full names of employees who submitted tickets (the "submitter_name" field), which is PII. It also contains ticket descriptions that may include personal information — a user writing "I can't access my payroll portal" reveals something about their employment status. The ticket data is internal and should be treated as confidential.

Appropriate use: Using this data to train an internal routing AI is a reasonable use — but you need to verify whether the AI vendor's data handling terms permit you to share this data with them, and whether the data needs to be anonymized before it leaves the internal system.

What "Classify" surfaces
This data contains PII (employee names) and is confidential. Before uploading it to any third-party tool, someone needs to check the data sharing terms and potentially anonymize the submitter field. This isn't something to skip because the manager said "just upload it."
⚡
Step 3 — Use
What do you do now, knowing what you know?
▾

Armed with what you learned in Understand and Classify, you don't just upload the file. Instead:

Data quality fixes: You trim the export to the 8 months since the category migration (eliminating the legacy taxonomy problem), filter out the 11% of records with blank categories (they'll train wrong patterns), and standardize the resolved_by field to use only employee IDs.

Privacy check: You raise the PII question with your manager before proceeding. Turns out the AI tool vendor has a data processing agreement that covers this, but the submitter's full name shouldn't be included — only their employee ID. You remove the name field from the export.

Informed use: You upload the cleaned, 8-month dataset (about 9,200 records instead of 14,000) and document the decisions you made and why. When the AI routing tool is evaluated, the team understands what data it trained on and can interpret its performance accordingly — including its likely weakness on ticket types that were underrepresented in the 8-month window.

What "Use" looks like with the framework applied
The "just upload it" instruction produced a much better outcome because UCU was applied first. The AI tool trains on cleaner data, a privacy issue was caught before it became a problem, and the team has documented context for interpreting the model's performance. None of this required special technical skills — just the habit of asking the framework's questions before acting.

📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

Understand means knowing what the data actually is before touching it — its source, time period, field meanings, collection method, and known quality issues. Skipping this step causes workers to build analyses and train AI models on data that doesn't represent what they think it does.

Classify means determining the data type and the constraints that apply to it — structured vs. unstructured, whether it contains PII or sensitive information, who can access it, and what uses are appropriate. This step is especially critical when data will be shared with third-party tools or used to train AI systems.

Use means applying the data with full awareness of its strengths and limitations — validating before acting, communicating limitations to the audience, and not exceeding the appropriate boundaries the Classify step established. When results are surprising (better or worse than expected), Use requires pausing to validate rather than acting immediately.
