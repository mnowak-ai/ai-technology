Lesson 4.2 — Pattern Recognition & Insights

What Is Anomaly Detection? How AI Flags the Unusual — and Why Humans Still Have to Investigate

The Datadog case study in the video was excellent but compressed. This article unpacks the full mechanism: what anomaly detection actually does, what "unusual" means statistically, why false positives happen, and what the human's job is after an AI alert fires.

The Core Idea — Without the Math

Anomaly detection is the process of identifying data points or patterns that don't fit expected behavior. In a tech context, it answers the question: is this data point unusual enough to warrant investigation?

The challenge is defining "unusual" in a way that's useful. Too sensitive, and the system fires alerts for normal variation — drowning your team in false positives. Too lenient, and real problems slip through undetected. Getting this calibration right is an ongoing human responsibility, not a one-time configuration.

AI tools like Datadog's Watchdog perform anomaly detection continuously across thousands of metrics simultaneously — a scale no human team could match manually. But the AI produces alerts. It doesn't produce conclusions. Every alert still requires a human to investigate, contextualize, and decide what to do.

AI anomaly detection is a smoke detector, not a fire department. It tells you something triggered. It doesn't tell you whether there's a real fire, where it is, or what to do about it. That's the investigation.

How AI Anomaly Detection Actually Works — Step by Step

1
Learn the baseline
The system observes a metric over time and builds a statistical model of what "normal" looks like. It learns not just a single average, but a dynamic range that accounts for time-of-day patterns (higher traffic at 9am than 3am), day-of-week patterns (Mondays are busier), and seasonal trends. This learning period typically takes 1–2 weeks of data.
Example: CPU usage on web-prod-01 is normally 30–45% on weekday mornings and 15–25% on weekends. The model learns both ranges and applies the appropriate one based on when a data point arrives.
2
Define the expected band
The model draws an upper and lower boundary around the learned baseline — called a confidence band or expected range. Values inside this band are considered normal. The width of the band is configurable: a narrow band catches more anomalies (more alerts, more false positives) and a wide band catches fewer (fewer alerts, potentially misses real problems). This sensitivity setting is a business judgment call that the AI cannot make.
Example: The expected band for weekday morning CPU is 25–55% — wide enough to accommodate normal variation without triggering alerts, narrow enough to catch a genuine spike to 85%.
3
Flag values outside the band
When a metric falls outside the expected band, the system generates an anomaly alert. Most systems require a metric to be anomalous for a sustained period (e.g., 5 minutes) before alerting — to avoid firing on transient single-sample spikes that immediately resolve. The duration threshold is configurable and affects how quickly the system responds vs. how many false positives it generates.
Example: CPU reaches 82% at 10:47am and stays there. At 10:52am (5 minutes sustained), Watchdog fires: "Anomaly detected: CPU usage above expected range on web-prod-01."
4
Correlate with other metrics
Advanced anomaly detection systems don't just flag individual metrics — they look for correlated anomalies across multiple metrics. A CPU spike accompanied by a latency spike and an error rate increase is a much stronger signal than a CPU spike alone. This multi-metric correlation reduces false positives significantly because coincidental single-metric variations are common; coincidental simultaneous anomalies across multiple correlated metrics are rare.
Example: CPU anomaly on web-prod-01 at the same time as: memory anomaly on web-prod-01, latency anomaly on the API gateway, and error rate anomaly on the database layer. The system fires a high-severity correlated incident rather than four separate low-severity alerts.
5
Human investigates
The alert reaches a human. This is where anomaly detection ends and incident response begins. The human's job: verify the alert is real (not a false positive), determine the cause, assess the impact, and decide the appropriate response. The AI has done its job — flagging the unusual. Everything that comes next is human work.
Example: The on-call engineer receives the correlated incident alert. They open the Datadog dashboard, review the correlated metrics, check recent deployments in the change log, identify a deployment from 40 minutes ago that modified the database query layer, and escalate to the dev team.

What "Unusual" Actually Means — And Why False Positives Happen

Anomaly detection flags statistical outliers — data points that fall outside the expected range. But statistical outliers and real problems are not the same thing. A data point can be statistically unusual for completely benign reasons, and a real problem can produce data points that look statistically normal until it's too late.

True Positive: The anomaly detection correctly flagged a real problem. Response time spiked well above the expected range after a faulty deployment, stayed elevated, and was correctly identified as an incident. Human investigation confirmed a database query regression. This is anomaly detection working as designed.
False Positive: The system flagged something outside the expected range — but investigation revealed it was a legitimate marketing email campaign driving a traffic surge. The spike was real but not a problem. False positives happen when something unusual (but benign) occurs outside the model's training distribution. Cost: wasted investigation time. This is why human judgment is required before escalating every alert.
False Negative (Missed Anomaly): A gradual memory leak slowly degrades performance over 10 days — each day's value is only slightly above the previous day, staying within the expected band until the server crashes. The anomaly detection didn't fire because no single data point was anomalous. Humans reviewing trend data would have caught this — gradual drift is hard for threshold-based systems to catch. This is the blind spot of baseline comparison.

Understanding all three outcomes is essential for working with anomaly detection in practice. True positives validate the system. False positives require investigation and recalibration. False negatives — the missed problems — are the hardest to catch because you don't know what you don't know. Humans who periodically review trend data, not just respond to alerts, catch the false negatives that anomaly detection misses.

Database query latency jumping 340% while error rate spikes strongly suggests the errors are caused by database timeouts — the API is waiting for the database, hitting a timeout, and returning 500 errors to users. This is a database problem, not an API problem. The API is the victim; the database is the cause.

Why Human Investigation Is Always Required

🧠
AI doesn't know why
An anomaly detection system can flag that error rate is 9× above expected. It cannot tell you that the cause is a database query regression from a deployment 8 minutes ago. Root cause analysis requires knowledge of the system's architecture and change history — context the AI doesn't have.
📅
AI doesn't know what changed
The AI's model is built from historical patterns. It doesn't know about the deployment that just happened, the configuration change that went out this morning, or the new feature that launched yesterday. Correlating anomaly timing with change events is the human's essential contribution to every investigation.
⚖️
AI doesn't know the business impact
An error rate of 2.8% is statistically anomalous. Whether it requires immediate action, a P1 incident declaration, and customer communication — or whether it can wait 30 minutes — depends on who is affected, what the SLA says, and what the business consequences are. That's a judgment call.
🔍
AI doesn't validate its own results
Every alert needs a quick false-positive check before escalation. Is there a known event that explains this? Did a scheduled job run at this time? Was there a deployment? Is there a day-of-week pattern the model might not have learned fully? Automatic escalation of every alert without this check generates unnecessary incidents and wastes everyone's time.

📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

Anomaly detection works in four stages: learn the baseline (what normal looks like across time-of-day and day-of-week patterns), define the expected band, flag values outside it (usually requiring a sustained duration, not just a single data point), and correlate with other metrics to reduce false positives. Multi-metric correlation dramatically improves signal quality.

Three outcomes exist: true positives (real problems correctly flagged), false positives (benign events that look anomalous — cost: wasted investigation time), and false negatives (gradual problems that never trigger an alert because no single data point is anomalous — only caught by humans reviewing trends). All three require understanding to work effectively with these systems.

Human investigation is always required after an alert because the AI doesn't know why an anomaly occurred, what changed recently, what the business impact is, or whether the alert is a false positive. The AI produces the flag. The human produces the conclusion. Alert sensitivity is a business judgment that determines how many false positives the team can handle before developing alert fatigue.
