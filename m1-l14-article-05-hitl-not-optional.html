Why "We Have a Human Review Step" Isn't Enough

Every organization deploying AI workflows claims to have human oversight. In practice, the quality of that oversight varies enormously. A human who clicks "Approve" on 200 AI recommendations per shift without reading them is not a meaningful oversight mechanism. A human who verifies AI-flagged security anomalies against actual network logs before escalating is.

The meaningful question isn't whether a human is present in the workflow — it's whether the human has the time, information, and authority to exercise genuine judgment, and whether they actually do so.

What to Carry Forward

Meaningful human oversight requires three things: adequate time to review, access to the information needed to make a judgment, and a workflow that actually enables — rather than just requires — that judgment. A button labeled "Approve" without supporting context is not oversight.

Good human checkpoint design focuses human attention where it matters most: on anomalies, edge cases, and high-consequence decisions — not on routine pattern-matching that AI handles well. Exception-based review is often more effective than uniform review of everything.

Being the person who asks "should we act on this?" — and who has the judgment to evaluate the answer — is a professional value proposition that becomes more important as more decisions get automated. The checkbox approver becomes redundant; the genuine checkpoint becomes indispensable.
