Lesson 3.1 — Clear Technical Communication

AI-Generated Text in Technical Documentation: What You Must Review Before It Ships

Copilot drafted it. Confluence AI summarized it. Jira AI wrote the ticket. Now you're about to approve it. Here's what you need to check — and what happens when you don't.

The New Technical Writing Workflow

AI writing tools are now standard in most engineering environments. GitHub Copilot suggests code comments and PR descriptions. Confluence AI summarizes meeting notes into action items. Jira AI drafts tickets from brief descriptions. Custom LLMs generate runbooks from incident data.

These tools save time. They also make a specific set of mistakes — and those mistakes have a habit of shipping into production documentation where they cause real problems months later.

The issue isn't that AI writes poorly. AI documentation tools write fluently, confidently, and incorrectly — in ways that are easy to miss when you're reading quickly. They sound like good documentation. They often aren't.

An AI can write a runbook that looks perfect and will fail spectacularly the first time an engineer tries to follow it under pressure. Your job is to be the person who catches that before it ships.

The Six Most Common AI Documentation Errors

These patterns appear consistently across AI-generated technical documentation. Learn to recognize them before you approve anything.

1
Environment-Specific Details That Are Wrong or Generic

AI tools generate documentation based on patterns across many codebases and organizations. They don't know your specific environment — your cluster names, your internal endpoints, your version numbers, your team's naming conventions.

⚠ AI-generated (looks correct — probably isn't)
To restart the inference service, run: kubectl rollout restart deployment/inference-service -n production Verify the pods are healthy: kubectl get pods -n production -l app=inference-service
🔴
Your namespace might be ml-prod not production. Your deployment name might be inference-api not inference-service. Your team might not use the app= label selector. Each of these will cause the command to silently do nothing — or worse, affect the wrong resource.
✓ What to verify
Run the command in a staging environment before publishing. Confirm the namespace, deployment name, and label selectors against your actual cluster configuration. Replace any generic values with your specific environment's values.
2
Missing Rollback and Recovery Steps

AI-generated procedures almost always describe the happy path — what to do when everything works. They consistently underrepresent what to do when a step fails, what rollback looks like, and who to escalate to.

⚠ AI-generated (incomplete)
Deployment steps: 1. Run the deploy script: ./deploy.sh --env prod --version v2.4.1 2. Monitor the deployment in Datadog 3. Confirm successful deployment by checking the status dashboard
🔴
What happens if the deploy script fails at step 1? What does a "successful" deployment look like in Datadog — what metric, what threshold? What's the rollback command? Who do you call if the dashboard shows degraded? An engineer following this at 2am has nowhere to go if anything goes wrong.
✓ What to add
For every AI-generated procedure, ask: "What does the engineer do if this step fails?" Add error handling, rollback commands, escalation paths, and specific success criteria for each step.
3
Ambiguity Resolved Into False Certainty

This is the most dangerous AI documentation error. When the source material contains uncertainty ("we believe this might be caused by..."), AI tools often remove that uncertainty in the summary ("the root cause is..."). This is called ambiguity deletion — and it actively misleads future readers.

⚠ AI summary of incident notes
Root cause: memory leak in the caching module. Issue resolved after pod restart. Monitoring shows stable. Incident closed.
🔴
The actual engineering notes said: "We believe the memory leak in the caching module was the proximate cause, but we haven't ruled out a race condition in the job scheduler. Should monitor for 48 hours before closing." The AI summary deleted the uncertainty — and the instruction to keep the incident open.
✓ What to verify
Always compare AI summaries against source notes when the document involves incident conclusions, root cause determinations, or resolution status. Any uncertainty in the original must be preserved in the summary.
4
Version-Dependent Steps That Are Outdated

AI tools are trained on data with a cutoff date. Kubernetes commands, Terraform syntax, API endpoints, and tool flags change across versions. An AI-generated runbook may reference a flag that was deprecated, a command that changed syntax, or an API that no longer exists in the version your team uses.

⚠ Version-specific risk
Scale the deployment: kubectl scale --replicas=5 deployment/inference-api Check resource usage: kubectl top nodes --sort-by=memory
🔴
The --sort-by flag for kubectl top has changed behavior across versions. Helm commands, Terraform resource names, and cloud provider CLI flags are especially prone to version drift. Commands that look right but reference deprecated syntax will fail silently or produce unexpected results.
✓ What to verify
Run every command in the runbook against your actual environment and tool versions before publishing. Add version requirements at the top of any document with tool-specific commands ("Requires: kubectl v1.28+, Terraform 1.6+").
5
Generic Procedures That Don't Match Your Team's Workflow

AI-generated SOPs and runbooks describe what a typical team would do — not what your team actually does. Your team may use a different escalation path, a different approval process, different monitoring tools, or a different deployment pipeline. Generic procedures that look correct will cause engineers to follow the wrong process during incidents.

⚠ Generic AI escalation path
If the issue cannot be resolved within 30 minutes, escalate to the senior on-call engineer. If unresolved after 60 minutes, notify the engineering manager and consider engaging vendor support.
🔴
Your team may have a specific PagerDuty escalation policy, a specific Slack channel for P1 escalations, a specific person or rotation for senior on-call, and a specific process for engaging AWS/GCP support. "Senior on-call engineer" could mean three different people depending on the day and time.
✓ What to verify
Replace every generic role, channel, or tool reference with the specific person, PagerDuty policy, Slack channel, or process your team actually uses. If it's not specific enough for an engineer to act on during an incident without looking anything up, it's not specific enough.
6
Missing Context About Why the Procedure Exists

AI tools describe what to do. They rarely explain why. Documentation that lacks "why" creates engineers who follow procedures correctly in normal situations and improvise incorrectly in edge cases — because they don't understand the intent behind the steps.

⚠ "What" without "why"
Step 4: Before restarting the inference service, always drain the request queue by running: ./drain_queue.sh --service inference-api --wait 60
🔴
What happens if an engineer skips this step because they're under pressure and the 60-second wait seems unnecessary? Without knowing that skipping queue drain causes in-flight ML scoring requests to be dropped mid-computation — corrupting the output cache — they have no way to judge whether it's safe to skip under time pressure.
✓ What to add
For any step that an engineer might be tempted to skip or modify under pressure, add a one-sentence "why." "Wait 60 seconds for queue drain — skipping this step drops in-flight requests and corrupts the output cache, requiring a full cache rebuild (adds 20–40 minutes to recovery)."

The AI Tools You're Already Using — and Their Specific Blind Spots

⚡ GitHub Copilot (PR descriptions, code comments)
Accurately describing what code does
Missing "why" context, rollback steps, and version-specific caveats. Often omits breaking change notices.
📄 Confluence AI / Notion AI (meeting notes, summaries)
Extracting action items and summarizing decisions
Resolving ambiguity in discussions into false certainty. Missing "TBD" or "undecided" items. Misattributing who owns which action item.
🎫 Jira AI (ticket drafts, acceptance criteria)
Structuring ticket descriptions from brief prompts
Generic acceptance criteria that don't match your system's actual behavior. Missing reproduction steps. Understating severity.
🤖 Custom LLM / RAG systems (runbooks, SOPs)
Synthesizing information from multiple source documents
Mixing information from different versions of the same procedure. Environment-specific details from different teams. High confidence in outdated source documents.

The Accountability Transfer

Here's the career-relevant part. When you approve AI-generated documentation, accountability for its accuracy transfers to you. This is not a matter of policy — it's how incident postmortems, compliance audits, and legal proceedings work in practice.

🤖
Step 1
AI generates the document
The AI does not understand your environment. It has no accountability. It cannot be blamed. It will not be in the postmortem.
👤
Step 2
You review and approve
At this moment, ownership transfers. The document is no longer "AI-generated" — it is your documentation. Your name is on the approval. Your judgment is what made it authoritative.
📋
Step 3
An engineer follows it
They trust the document because you approved it. They follow the generic namespace. They skip the step that has no "why." They close the incident because the AI summary said it was resolved.
🔴
Step 4
The postmortem happens
The question is not "did the AI make a mistake?" The question is "who approved this documentation and was it reviewed adequately?" That's you. "The AI wrote it" is not a defensible answer in a compliance audit or a legal proceeding.

Your Pre-Approval Review Checklist

Before approving any AI-generated technical document, run through these checks. The high-priority items are the ones most likely to cause operational failures.

🔴 High Priority — Always Check
Every command has been tested in the actual environment — namespace, deployment names, and flags match what's in production
Every uncertainty in source material is preserved — "we believe," "suspected," and "TBD" must not become definitive conclusions
Rollback and failure-handling steps are included for every action that can fail
Escalation paths name specific people, channels, or PagerDuty policies — not generic roles
🟡 Important — Check When Relevant
Tool version requirements are specified, and commands match the version currently in use by your team
Steps that engineers might skip under pressure include a brief explanation of why skipping would cause problems
Action items and owners are attributed to specific people, not generic team names
Success criteria are specific and observable — not "confirm the service is healthy" but "confirm HTTP 200 rate >99.5% for 5 consecutive minutes"
🧠 Quick Check
A Confluence AI summary of a postmortem meeting states: "The team decided to implement automatic rollbacks for all future ML model deployments." After reviewing the meeting recording, you find the actual discussion was: "We discussed auto-rollback but decided it was too risky without better monitoring in place. We'll revisit after the monitoring improvements are done." What type of AI documentation error is this, and what's the risk?
📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

AI documentation tools make six predictable mistakes: wrong environment details, missing failure handling, ambiguity deletion, outdated version-specific commands, generic workflows that don't match your team, and missing "why" context. Learn to recognize these patterns and you'll catch them before they ship.

Approving AI-generated documentation is a professional act with professional consequences. "The AI wrote it" is not a defense in a postmortem or an audit. The moment you approve, the document is yours — and so are its errors.

The most dangerous AI documentation error is ambiguity deletion — when the AI turns a team's uncertainty into a confident conclusion. Always compare AI summaries against source notes when they involve incident resolutions, root cause determinations, or team decisions. If the original had a question mark, the summary needs one too.
