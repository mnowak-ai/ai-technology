Lesson 3.2 — Summarizing Technical Information

Why Technical Summarization Is a Skill, Not a Shortcut

Summarizing isn't cutting things out until the document is shorter. It's deciding what a specific reader needs to act correctly — and having the judgment to know what's safe to leave out.

The Mistake Most Engineers Make

When most engineers are asked to "summarize" something, they do one of two things: they copy the most important-sounding sentences from the original, or they describe everything in slightly fewer words. Neither is a summary.

A real technical summary is a judgment call. It answers the question: what does this specific reader need to know, right now, to act correctly? Everything that serves that answer stays. Everything else — no matter how technically interesting — goes.

That judgment is hard. It requires understanding what the reader knows, what they're going to do next, and what would happen if they acted on incomplete or wrong information. It's why summarization is a skill that engineers develop over careers, not a task you can offload to a junior team member or an AI tool without review.

A summary that includes everything important but buries it in everything else has failed. A summary that cuts the right things has done the hardest part of technical writing.

What Summarization Actually Involves

Good technical summarization is three separate activities happening at once, each requiring a different kind of judgment.

🔍
Comprehension — understanding the source material fully
You cannot summarize what you don't fully understand. Engineers who skim incident logs and summarize the parts they recognize produce summaries with dangerous gaps. Before you cut anything, you need to understand everything — including the parts that seem minor. Minor details are sometimes the most important ones.
⚖️
Judgment — deciding what the reader needs vs. what's interesting
The hardest part. Technical material is often interesting in ways that aren't relevant to the reader's decision. An SRE postmortem might contain fascinating detail about a race condition that contributed 2% to the outage duration. That detail matters to the engineer who'll fix it. It does not matter to the exec deciding whether to increase infrastructure budget. Judgment means knowing the difference — and being willing to cut things that are technically interesting but operationally irrelevant for this audience.
✍️
Translation — expressing technical content at the right level
A summary for a non-technical executive isn't just shorter than the full incident report. It's written in different language, with different framing, emphasizing different things. Translation doesn't mean dumbing down — it means finding the framing that allows the reader to act correctly. "A memory leak in the payment service caused pod crashes" and "a software defect caused our payment system to fail under high traffic" describe the same thing at different translation levels. Which is right depends entirely on who's reading.

The Five Questions Every Technical Summary Must Answer

Regardless of the document type — incident summary, model performance report, deployment postmortem, security advisory — a complete technical summary answers these five questions. Any summary missing one of them has a gap that could cause the reader to act incorrectly.

1
What happened?
A specific, factual description of the event — not vague ("the system experienced issues") but concrete ("the payment API returned HTTP 503 for 34% of requests between 02:06 and 08:20 UTC").
2
Who is affected, and how?
Which users, systems, or teams experienced impact — and what that impact was in terms they care about. Engineers care about error rates. Executives care about revenue and customer count. Customers care about whether their transaction went through.
3
What is the current status?
Is this resolved, still ongoing, or partially mitigated? This is the most time-sensitive question in any incident summary — and the one most often buried at the end. Put it first.
4
What caused it?
The root cause, stated at the right level of detail for the audience. Engineers need the technical root cause. Executives need to understand the category of failure (process gap, monitoring failure, software defect) well enough to make resource decisions.
5
What happens next?
The specific actions being taken, by whom, and by when. "We're working on it" is not an answer to this question. "The ML team will add a null check to the normalize() function by Nov 22 (owner: J. Park)" is.

Why AI Tools Can't Replace Summarization Judgment

AI summarization tools — Confluence AI, Notion AI, custom LLM pipelines — are genuinely useful for a first pass. They can extract key sentences, organize timelines, and draft structure. But they make systematic errors in exactly the places where summarization judgment matters most.

❌ What AI tools get wrong
They don't know which reader you're writing for — they optimize for apparent completeness, not audience-specific relevance. They resolve uncertainty into confidence ("root cause confirmed" when your notes say "we believe"). They cut the caveats and edge cases that often contain the most operationally important information. They produce summaries that sound complete but have dropped the qualifications that would have changed someone's decision.
✅ What human judgment provides
Knowing that this executive doesn't need the technical root cause — they need to know whether to delay the product launch. Knowing that "we believe" needs to stay in the summary because the investigation is still open. Knowing that the 2% edge case is actually the thing that will cause the next incident if it's not documented. AI can draft. Human judgment decides what's safe to cut.

📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

Technical summarization is three things at once: comprehension (fully understanding the source), judgment (knowing what this reader needs to act correctly), and translation (expressing it at the right level). Most summarization failures happen at the judgment stage — cutting things that mattered, or keeping things that diluted what was important.

Every technical summary must answer five questions: what happened, who is affected and how, what is the current status, what caused it, and what happens next. A summary missing any of these leaves a gap that could cause the reader to act incorrectly — or fail to act when they should.

AI summarization tools help with structure and first drafts. They fail at the judgment layer — they don't know which reader you're writing for, they delete uncertainty from the source material, and they optimize for sounding complete rather than being operationally accurate. Human review is not optional.
