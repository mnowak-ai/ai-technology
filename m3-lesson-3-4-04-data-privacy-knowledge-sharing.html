Lesson 3.4 — Knowledge Sharing & Collaboration

Data Privacy and Knowledge Sharing: What You Cannot Put in a Wiki, Slack, or Shared Drive

Knowledge sharing platforms are not automatically compliant storage. Putting the wrong information in the wrong place is a career-ending mistake — and it happens more often than most engineers realize.

The False Assumption Most Engineers Make

When engineers think "knowledge sharing," they think about solving the documentation problem — getting information out of people's heads and into a place where the team can find it. That's the right instinct. But it comes with a critical assumption that often goes unexamined: that sharing platforms like wikis, Slack, and shared drives are appropriate places for any work-related information.

They're not. Knowledge sharing platforms are not compliance-controlled environments by default — and storing certain types of information in them creates real legal exposure, regulatory violations, and security vulnerabilities. The engineer who pastes customer PII into a Slack thread to debug an issue isn't being careless about knowledge sharing. They're potentially creating a data breach.

The question isn't whether information should be shared. It's whether this platform is the right place to share it — and whether the people who can access it should be able to.

Five Categories That Must Never Go in General Wikis, Slack, or Shared Drives

🚫
Customer PII and Personal Data

Any information that identifies or could identify a specific customer — names, email addresses, phone numbers, IP addresses tied to identity, transaction histories — is regulated under GDPR, CCPA, HIPAA (for health data), and other frameworks depending on your industry and user geography.

Common ways this ends up in the wrong place
Pasting a customer's email or order ID into Slack to debug a support ticket
Exporting a sample of user data to a shared drive for ML model testing
Documenting a bug with real user data as the example in a Jira ticket or wiki page
Including customer-identifiable information in a postmortem timeline
Why this matters: Most wiki and Slack tools are not GDPR/CCPA Article 28 compliant data processors. Storing customer PII in them means that data is now in a system without appropriate access controls, retention policies, or deletion capabilities — all required by law.
🚫
Internal Security Findings, Vulnerabilities, and Penetration Test Results

Security vulnerability details — open CVEs in your systems, pen test findings, details of known attack vectors — must be handled through controlled disclosure processes with restricted access. Documenting them in a general team wiki or discussing them in broad Slack channels creates a roadmap for anyone who gains unauthorized access to your systems.

Examples of what cannot go in general channels
A Confluence page documenting all open security vulnerabilities with severity ratings
A Slack thread discussing the specific endpoint affected by a newly discovered CVE before patching
A shared drive folder with the full penetration test report (including exploit details)
A wiki page describing your authentication bypass edge case "for awareness"
Why this matters: If your Confluence or Slack is ever compromised, all documented vulnerabilities become immediately exploitable by the attacker. Security findings belong in dedicated, access-controlled security management tools — not general knowledge bases.
🚫
Unreleased Product Details, Revenue Figures, and M&A Information

Specific unannounced product features, revenue projections, strategic acquisition plans, and competitor analysis involving non-public information are material non-public information (MNPI). For publicly traded companies, improper disclosure of MNPI is a securities violation. For all companies, it's a breach of confidentiality obligations to employees, investors, and partners.

Where this commonly goes wrong
A product roadmap wiki page with unannounced feature launch dates and pricing
Slack threads discussing Q3 revenue before the earnings call
A shared drive folder with due diligence materials from an ongoing acquisition
Why this matters: Most engineering wikis and Slack workspaces don't have the access controls required for confidential business information. Information shared broadly "for alignment" often reaches people — including contractors, integration partners, or former employees with retained access — who shouldn't have it.
🚫
AI Training Data Containing Personal Information

This is a newer and rapidly evolving area of risk. When teams document model training data, share sample datasets for debugging ML issues, or store feature engineering examples with real user data, they're creating data governance problems that most organizations haven't yet caught up with.

Common ML team mistakes
Attaching a CSV of real user transactions to a Jira ticket for a model debugging session
Storing sample training data in a shared Confluence space to illustrate a feature engineering decision
Using real customer data in wiki examples when synthetic data would work equally well
Why this matters: Data minimization principles under GDPR and CCPA require that personal data be stored only where necessary and with appropriate controls. General wikis and shared drives don't qualify — and ML teams that store real user data in these places are creating compliance exposure that could survive long after the data seems "irrelevant."
🚫
Credentials, API Keys, and Secrets — Even "Temporary" Ones

This one is well-known but still happens constantly. Credentials shared in Slack "just for now," API keys pasted into wiki pages for a quick demo, passwords in a shared Google Sheet for a new tool. Even after the credential is rotated, the original may be preserved in Slack history, wiki edit history, or shared drive version history indefinitely.

The specific mistakes that get teams in trouble
"Temporary" API keys in Slack threads that are never rotated
Database connection strings in Confluence pages for "convenience during onboarding"
Shared service account passwords in a team Google Sheet
Why this matters: Credentials shared in collaboration tools are effectively public to anyone with access to those tools — current and former employees, integration partners, anyone whose account is compromised. Use secrets management tools (Vault, AWS Secrets Manager, 1Password Teams) exclusively. No exceptions.

The Safe Alternative Pattern

For each scenario where a privacy violation is tempting, there's a compliant alternative that accomplishes the same knowledge sharing goal.

❌ Don't
Paste a real customer's email address into Slack to debug why their account isn't working.
✅ Do instead
Use the customer's internal ID (which is not PII) in Slack. Pull up their account in your internal admin tool where access is logged and controlled.
❌ Don't
Attach a CSV of real user transactions to a Jira ticket so the ML team can reproduce a model bug.
✅ Do instead
Use a synthetic dataset that reproduces the same feature distribution without real user data. Most ML bugs can be reproduced with properly structured fake data.
❌ Don't
Document a known authentication vulnerability on a general Confluence page to "raise awareness."
✅ Do instead
File a security ticket in your restricted security tracking system. Reference the ticket number in general channels without details.

📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

Five categories that must never go in general wikis, Slack, or shared drives: customer PII and personal data, security vulnerabilities and pen test findings, unreleased product details and financial information, AI training data containing personal information, and credentials of any kind. Each has a compliant alternative that accomplishes the same knowledge sharing goal.

The most common mistake is using real data "just to debug" or "just temporarily." There's no such thing as temporary data in a collaboration tool — Slack retention policies, wiki edit histories, and shared drive version histories preserve content indefinitely. The safer default is synthetic data for all debugging and documentation examples.

Knowledge sharing is a positive organizational practice — but it requires knowing which information belongs where. When in doubt, ask: does this platform have the access controls, retention policies, and compliance certifications required for this type of data? If you don't know, assume the answer is no and ask your security or legal team before sharing.
