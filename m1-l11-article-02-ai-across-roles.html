Lesson 1.1 · What Is AI in Tech Work?

AI Across Tech Roles: What It Actually Looks Like for a Developer vs. a Security Analyst vs. an IT Ops Engineer

AI doesn't look the same in every tech role. A security analyst's experience with AI is fundamentally different from a developer's — different tools, different risks, different judgment calls. This article makes it concrete by role.

Why "AI in Tech" Isn't One Thing

The phrase "AI is changing tech work" is true — but it's so broad it doesn't help anyone know what to actually do differently. A software developer using GitHub Copilot is having a completely different experience than a security analyst using an AI-powered SIEM, or a DevOps engineer using predictive monitoring.

The tools are different, the failure modes are different, and the judgment calls are different. What stays the same across every role is the underlying dynamic: AI handles pattern recognition at scale, and humans own interpretation, verification, and decisions with real consequences.

What Stays the Same Across Every Role

Despite very different tools and use cases, five dynamics appear consistently across every tech role that works with AI:

🔍
AI surfaces candidates — humans make decisions
Whether it's a code suggestion, a security alert, or a deployment recommendation, AI surfaces what's most likely to be relevant. The human decides what to do about it. This division of labor is consistent across every role.
⚡
Volume increases, but so does the need for judgment
AI allows every role to process more — more code, more alerts, more logs, more tickets — faster. But this means judgment calls happen faster too. The increased volume doesn't reduce the importance of each decision; it often increases it.
🧪
Verification becomes a core skill
In every role, the ability to critically evaluate AI outputs — without assuming they're correct and without dismissing them reflexively — becomes more valuable than it was before AI tools existed. The question is never "did AI say it?" but "is it actually right?"
📉
The cost of context loss is higher
AI doesn't have your organizational context — your team's conventions, your company's risk tolerance, your system's history. Workers who provide that context get dramatically better results. Workers who don't get confidently wrong outputs that match general patterns but miss specific realities.
🏛️
Accountability doesn't transfer to the tool
In every role, the human who acted on an AI recommendation owns the outcome. A developer who shipped AI-generated code owns the bug. A security analyst who dismissed an AI alert owns the missed incident. The tool is a collaborator, not a decision-maker.

AI makes you faster at the parts of your job that involve processing information. It doesn't make you better at the parts that require judgment — it just makes judgment more important, more frequent, and harder to avoid.

📌 Article Summary

What to Carry Forward

AI looks different in every tech role — the tools, failure modes, and judgment calls are role-specific. Understanding what AI does in your specific role is more useful than understanding AI abstractly.

Across every role: AI surfaces candidates, humans make decisions. AI increases volume, but judgment becomes more important — not less. Accountability never transfers to the tool.

The skill gap AI creates is not knowledge of AI internals — it's the ability to verify AI outputs critically, provide context that improves output quality, and maintain accountability for decisions that AI informed but didn't make.
