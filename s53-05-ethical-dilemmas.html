Lesson 5.3 — Professional Responsibility

What Would You Do? Ethical Dilemmas in AI-Assisted Tech Work

Ethical judgment isn't built from rules — it's built from practice. These six realistic workplace dilemmas are the kinds of situations you'll actually face. Work through each one, then read the analysis.

The scenarios in this article don't have single "correct" answers in the way a math problem does. They have better and worse responses — and understanding why certain choices are better is what builds the professional judgment you need. Read each scenario, choose the response that feels most right, then read the analysis carefully.

Ethical judgment is a muscle. It gets stronger every time you practice it — and weaker every time you outsource it to the AI, to your manager, or to the assumption that someone else will handle it.

🎮 Ethical Dilemma
Scenario 1 of 6
Your manager asks you to speed up a code review by using an AI assistant. The AI flags no security issues. You find one yourself — a minor one. You're under pressure to submit.
The deadline is tight. Your manager has made it clear the project needs to move. The AI reviewed the code and gave it a clean pass. You found a minor cross-site scripting vulnerability that requires a fix. Fixing it will take about an hour and push the deadline.
⚖️ The tension
Deadline pressure vs. professional responsibility. The AI said it was fine. You found something it missed. The fix is real but takes time.
Fix the vulnerability, then tell your manager it will push the deadline slightly and why
Ship the code — the AI gave it a clean pass, and you don\'t want to second-guess the tool
Document the vulnerability as a future ticket, ship now, and fix it in the next sprint
🎮 Ethical Dilemma
Scenario 2 of 6
You're asked to build an AI feature that your company wants to describe as "unbiased" in marketing materials. You've read enough to know that no AI system is truly unbiased.
Marketing has drafted copy describing the feature as "completely unbiased AI-powered decisions." Legal has signed off. Your manager is excited. You're the engineer who built it. You know it's been tested for the most obvious bias types but not comprehensively audited across all demographics.
⚖️ The tension
Your expertise vs. organizational momentum. The claim isn't technically accurate, but everyone above you has already approved it.
Raise the concern with your manager: the "unbiased" claim isn\'t technically accurate. Propose alternative language like "designed to minimize bias" or "bias-tested."
Say nothing — marketing and legal have already approved it, so it\'s not your call
Document your concern in writing to protect yourself, then let it go
🎮 Ethical Dilemma
Scenario 3 of 6
A client asks you to use AI to generate performance review text for their employees. The AI produces reviews that are positive for some employees and subtly negative for others in ways that seem to correlate with demographics.
You've been hired to automate the review process. The client is excited. The AI writes fluent, professional-sounding reviews. But as you review the outputs, you notice the tone and the specific language choices seem different for different demographic groups in ways that could affect promotions and raises.
⚖️ The tension
Your professional obligation to the client vs. the potential harm to employees who are the clients' workers — and your potential liability as the person who delivered the tool.
Stop delivery, document the pattern you\'re seeing, and raise it with your manager and the client before going further
Deliver it — it\'s the client\'s responsibility to use the tool appropriately, not yours
Add a disclaimer to the deliverable about AI limitations and let the client decide
🎮 Ethical Dilemma
Scenario 4 of 6
You used AI heavily to write a technical article you're publishing under your name on the company blog. Your manager asks "how long did this take you?" You answer honestly. They seem surprised and ask, "did you write it all yourself?"
The article is technically accurate — you verified everything in it. The thinking and direction are yours. The drafting was substantially AI-assisted. Your company doesn't have a policy on this. Your manager's question feels like it has professional implications.
⚖️ The tension
Transparency vs. the (perhaps unfounded) fear that disclosure will be seen as a failure of professional effort.
Be honest: you directed the content and verified everything, but AI assisted substantially with drafting. Explain your process and the value you added.
Say yes — the article is accurate, the ideas are yours, and how you produced it is your business
Change the subject — bring up the article\'s content and metrics to redirect the conversation
🎮 Ethical Dilemma
Scenario 5 of 6
An AI tool your company uses starts recommending the same product to users who have already purchased it — clearly a system error. It's not your code, and you're the only one who notices. Fixing it isn't your job.
You're working on a different part of the platform when you see, in system logs, that the recommendation engine is repeatedly suggesting an item to users who bought it days ago. It's not causing financial harm, but it's creating a bad user experience at scale. The team responsible is in a different department.
⚖️ The tension
Scope of responsibility vs. the fact that you're the one who noticed. Is it your job to flag something outside your domain?
Send a message to the responsible team with what you observed. It\'s not your code to fix, but you can be the one who flags it.
Ignore it — it\'s not your system and not your responsibility to monitor it
Mention it to your manager at your next check-in and let them decide whether to escalate
🎮 Ethical Dilemma
Scenario 6 of 6
Your team is considering deploying an AI feature in a healthcare context. The AI performs well on average but you've read that similar tools often have higher error rates for certain patient populations. You don't have demographic data to test this yet.
The feature would help triage patient inquiries. Leadership is excited and wants to move quickly. You raise the possibility of demographic accuracy disparities, but the team's position is: "We don't have evidence it's biased — let's launch and monitor." You're junior on this team.
⚖️ The tension
The precautionary principle vs. organizational pressure to move fast. The absence of evidence of bias is not the same as evidence of absence of bias — especially in a high-stakes healthcare context.
Document the concern formally and escalate to a senior leader or the medical ethics team, asking that demographic testing be completed before launch
Accept the team\'s position — without evidence of bias, there\'s no basis to delay the launch
You\'ve raised it once and been overruled — at this point, consider whether you can stay on a project with practices you disagree with

The Framework Behind Every Scenario

Every dilemma above can be analyzed using the same four questions. Memorize these — they're the framework for ethical judgment in AI-assisted work:

🔍 The Four-Question Ethical Framework
Ask these before every difficult call
1
Who could be harmed, and how? Identify the people who could experience negative consequences from this decision — not just your organization, but the users, patients, or community members affected by the AI's outputs.
2
Am I the right person to handle this, or does it need more authority or expertise? Know your scope. Some concerns need to go up. Some need a specialist. Trying to handle something alone when it requires escalation is itself an ethical failure.
3
Would I be comfortable if this decision were fully transparent — to my manager, to the affected users, to a regulator? The "transparency test" surfaces the gap between what feels expedient and what's professionally defensible.
4
What's the cost of being wrong in each direction? Weigh the cost of a false alarm (unnecessary delay, a difficult conversation) against the cost of missing a real problem (harm to people, legal exposure, professional consequence). In high-stakes domains, asymmetry matters: the cost of missing is usually far higher than the cost of checking.
📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

Ethical dilemmas in AI-assisted work rarely announce themselves as ethical dilemmas. They look like deadline pressure, scope questions, manager disagreements, and inconvenient findings. The skill is recognizing when a routine-looking situation has ethical weight and responding to that weight, not just the surface pressure.

Silence is a choice with consequences. In every scenario above, saying nothing or going along without comment was an available option — and in every case, it was the professionally and ethically weaker choice. Raising concerns, even when they're inconvenient, is part of the job.

The four-question framework helps in every situation: Who could be harmed? Am I the right person to handle this? Would I be comfortable if this were fully transparent? What's the cost of being wrong in each direction? These questions don't always produce obvious answers — but they reliably produce better ones.

Being junior doesn't remove ethical responsibility. You have knowledge, access, and observations that others don't. Your professional obligation is to surface what you know through the right channels — not to defer indefinitely because you're not the most senior person in the room.
