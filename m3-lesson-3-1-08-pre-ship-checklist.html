Lesson 3.1 — Clear Technical Communication

A Tech Professional's Checklist for Reviewing Any Document Before It Ships

One checklist. Every document type. Built from everything in this lesson — clarity, audience, tone, jargon, plain language, and AI review. Pin this somewhere useful.

Everything in Lesson 3.1 — how to be clear, how to write for multiple audiences, how to review AI-generated text, plain language principles, tone, jargon — comes down to a handful of questions you need to ask before any technical document leaves your hands.

This is that checklist. It's interactive. Work through it for your next document. The high-priority items are the ones most likely to cause real problems if missed.

Clarity
5 items
Every action step uses a specific command, name, or valueHigh
No vague verbs like "check," "fix," or "address" without specifying what to check, what to fix, or what to address.
Systems, services, and endpoints are named specificallyHigh
Not "the service" or "the system" — the actual service name, namespace, endpoint, or environment.
Success and failure are defined, not assumedHigh
If the reader needs to judge whether something worked, tell them what "worked" looks like — specifically.
The most important information comes firstMed
Critical warnings, resolution status, and required actions appear at the top — not buried after context paragraphs.
Sentences are under 25 words where possibleMed
Especially in runbooks and incident comms — long compound sentences cause misreads under pressure.
👥
Audience
4 items
I know exactly who will read this — and what they need to doHigh
Every reader has a job. This document gives them what they need to do that job — no more, no less.
Technical terminology is appropriate for this audienceHigh
Terms that engineers need are present. Terms that would confuse a non-technical reader are translated or defined.
If this crosses a team boundary, jargon is translatedMed
Executive summaries, legal notifications, customer updates, and cross-team communications use audience-appropriate language.
Acronyms are defined on first useMed
Even "obvious" abbreviations like SLO, SRE, ETL, or RLHF — define them once if there's any chance of a mixed audience.
✅
Accuracy
4 items
Every command or procedure has been tested in the actual environmentHigh
Especially for runbooks — namespace names, deployment names, flags, and expected outputs match reality, not documentation templates.
Uncertainty is preserved — not resolved into false confidenceHigh
If the source material says "we believe" or "suspected," the document must say so too. AI summaries frequently delete this qualifier.
Version-specific details match the tools currently in useMed
CLI flags, API endpoints, and configuration syntax for the actual version your team runs — not the version in the template.
Facts and figures are sourced — not estimated or approximated as if exactMed
If a number is an estimate, label it as one. "~$52,000" is honest. "$52,000" in an exec summary implies a precision you may not have.
📋
Completeness
4 items
Escalation paths name specific people, channels, or policiesHigh
Not "escalate to the team" — the specific PagerDuty policy, Slack channel, or person on call.
Failure handling is included for every step that can go wrongHigh
Runbooks and SOPs especially — what does the reader do if step 3 fails? AI-generated docs almost always omit this.
Steps that could be skipped under pressure include a "why" noteMed
If skipping a step causes downstream problems, say so briefly. One sentence prevents a lot of 3am improvisations.
Action items have named owners and due datesMed
In postmortems and decision docs — "the team will investigate" is not an action item. A name and a date are.
🤖
AI-Generated Content (skip if no AI was used)
3 items
Every environment-specific detail has been verified against the actual systemHigh
Namespaces, service names, command flags, API endpoints — AI tools generate plausible-sounding generic values. Verify every one.
AI summaries have been compared to source material for ambiguity deletionHigh
Check that any uncertainty in the source ("we believe," "suspected," "not yet confirmed") is preserved in the AI summary.
I accept full accountability for this document's accuracyHigh
"The AI wrote it" is not a defense. By approving this document, you own its contents — including any errors the AI introduced.

Quick-Reference: Which Items Matter Most for Each Document Type

Not every document needs every item. Here's which sections to focus on for the most common IT documents.

🔴 Runbooks & SOPs
All Clarity items — especially specific commands and success criteria
All Completeness items — failure handling and escalation are critical
Accuracy: every command tested in the actual environment
📊 Postmortems & Incident Reports
Accuracy: uncertainty preserved, facts sourced
Completeness: action items with owners and due dates
AI check: ambiguity deletion is the #1 risk in AI-summarized postmortems
📧 Customer & Executive Comms
All Audience items — jargon boundary is especially critical
Clarity: most important information first
Accuracy: no false confidence, estimates labeled as such
🎫 Jira Tickets & PRs
Clarity: specific systems, specific acceptance criteria
Completeness: why this work matters, not just what it is
AI check if Jira AI or Copilot wrote the description

📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

The 20-item pre-ship checklist covers the five dimensions that determine whether a technical document is safe to publish: clarity, audience fit, accuracy, completeness, and AI-specific review. Not every item applies to every document — focus on the high-priority items for your document type.

The highest-risk gaps — the ones most likely to cause incidents, compliance failures, or missed incidents — are: vague action steps, missing failure handling, ambiguity deletion in AI summaries, and jargon crossing audience boundaries. These five appear repeatedly in postmortems and audit findings.

A checklist is only as useful as the habit of using it. The goal is not to run through 20 items for every Slack message — it's to internalize these questions so they become automatic for any document that matters: runbooks, postmortems, incident comms, and anything going to a new audience.
