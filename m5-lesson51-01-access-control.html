Lesson 5.1 — Security & Privacy Risks

Access Control 101: Why Not Everyone Should Have the Same AI Permissions

One of the most powerful security tools in any tech environment isn't a piece of software — it's a decision about who is allowed to do what. Access control is the practice of making sure the right people can reach the right information, and nothing more. When AI tools get added to the mix, this becomes even more critical.

Start Here: The Building Analogy

Imagine you work in a 20-story office building. Everyone in the building has a keycard to get through the front door. But does everyone have access to every floor? Of course not. The executive suite on the top floor requires a different clearance. The server room in the basement has its own lock. The accounting department's file room isn't open to marketing.

This is access control — and it works exactly the same way in tech. Different people need access to different things. Giving everyone access to everything isn't generous — it's a security disaster waiting to happen.

The Four Principles Behind Every Good Access Control System

Access control isn't arbitrary. It's built on a set of principles that security professionals have developed over decades. Understanding these principles helps you make sense of why your company has the policies it does — and helps you recognize when something seems off.

🔒
Least Privilege
Every person and every system should have the minimum access needed to do their job — and nothing more. This is probably the most important principle in all of security. If you only need to read a file, you shouldn't be able to delete it. If you only need one AI tool for your job, you shouldn't have access to five.
A customer service rep needs to look up order history. They should be able to read it — not edit, not delete, not export to an external AI tool.
👥
Role-Based Access Control (RBAC)
Access is granted based on what your job requires, not who you are personally. All developers get developer-level access. All managers get manager-level access. This makes it easier to manage, audit, and revoke access consistently — and prevents the "special favors" problem where one person quietly accumulates more access than they should have.
When someone gets promoted from analyst to manager, their access profile changes automatically — they gain some things and lose others based on the new role's actual needs.
🎯
Need-to-Know
Even within the same role, access to specific sensitive data should only be granted when there's a specific, documented reason to need it. Curiosity doesn't count. "It might be useful someday" doesn't count. A legitimate business task with a clear connection to that data counts.
A developer building a new payment feature may need temporary access to anonymized payment data. They request it, document why, it gets approved for the project timeline, then it expires.
📋
Audit Trails
Every time someone accesses data, uses an AI tool, or makes a change to a system, that action is logged. This isn't about spying on employees — it's about being able to trace what happened when something goes wrong. Good audit trails mean you can find the source of a problem, fix it, and prove what occurred.
When 23andMe was breached, audit logs were essential for figuring out which accounts had been compromised, what data was accessed, and who the affected users were.

What Happens When Access Control Fails

Here's a real-world scenario — not a famous breach, but the kind of thing that happens quietly inside companies every week. This is how insufficient access control leads to an AI security incident.

⚠️ Scenario: How a Small Permission Gap Becomes a Big Problem
The junior analyst who had access she didn't need
1
A junior marketing analyst is hired. IT gives her the standard "marketing team" access profile. Nobody notices that the previous person in her role had been granted temporary access to the customer database six months ago for a one-time project — and that access was never revoked.
2
She's working on a competitive analysis report and decides to use an AI tool to help summarize trends. She pulls a full customer export — which she can access, because the permissions are still there — and pastes it into the AI tool to help identify patterns.
3
The AI tool she used wasn't on the company's approved list. It stores all inputs for model improvement. 50,000 customer records — names, emails, purchase histories — are now in an external AI system's training pipeline. She had no idea this was happening.
4
Three months later, there's a data incident at the AI tool provider. The company's legal team discovers the customer data exposure. The company has to notify 50,000 customers, hire a breach response firm, and face potential regulatory fines. Nobody was trying to cause harm — the harm happened because of two failures: lingering permissions and no approved-tools enforcement.
✅ How access control would have prevented this
If the previous analyst's temporary database access had been automatically revoked after the project ended (least privilege + time-limited access), the junior analyst would never have been able to pull the customer export in the first place. The entire incident chain would have stopped at step 2. Access control isn't just about locking things down — it's about making sure permissions expire when the reason for them does.

What This Means for You as a Tech Worker

You might be thinking: "I'm not the person who sets permissions — that's IT's job." That's partly true. But access control is everyone's responsibility in three important ways.

First: Don't use access you don't actually need. Just because you can access something doesn't mean you should. If you find yourself with access to data or tools that seem beyond your role, flag it to your manager rather than quietly using it. That lingering access might be a mistake — and if something goes wrong, you'll be in the logs.

Second: Never share your credentials. Your access is yours. If a coworker asks to use your login to access something they don't have access to, the answer is no — always. Credential sharing bypasses every access control system the company has built and makes audit trails meaningless.

Third: Report access that seems wrong. If you're given access to something that doesn't seem right for your role, or if you notice a colleague has access they probably shouldn't, saying something is part of your job. Access control failures are most often caught by employees who notice something feels off — not by automated systems.

📌 What to Remember

Access Control Is How Security Becomes Real

The least privilege principle — give people only the access they actually need — is the single most powerful tool for preventing AI security incidents. Most breaches involve data people could access but shouldn't have been able to.

Role-based access control means your permissions follow your job, not your personality. When your role changes, your access should change too — and old permissions that no longer apply should be revoked, not left sitting around waiting to cause problems.

Never share credentials, never use access you don't actually need for your job, and always flag when something seems off. Access control only works when humans respect it — the systems are only as strong as the people using them.
