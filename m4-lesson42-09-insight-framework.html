The Strong Insight Analysis Framework: Accurate, Context-Aware, Actionable, Verified

The video closed lesson 4.2 with this four-part model. It deserves its own article — walked through a complete real scenario so you can internalize each filter as a practiced skill, not just a concept on a slide.

Why You Need a Framework for Evaluating Insights

AI tools produce insights constantly — pattern findings, anomaly alerts, correlation surfacing, trend predictions. Some of these are gold. Some are misleading. Some are technically accurate but irrelevant. Some are accurate and relevant but not actionable. Without a consistent evaluation framework, you end up either acting on everything (exhausting, error-prone) or dismissing too much (missing real signals).

The four-filter framework — Accurate, Context-Aware, Actionable, Verified — gives you a sequence of questions that separates strong insights from weak ones before you act on them. Each filter eliminates a specific category of bad insight. Together, they define what a finding needs to pass before it earns a decision.

A weak insight acted on confidently causes more damage than no insight at all. The framework's purpose isn't to be skeptical — it's to make sure the insights you act on are worth acting on.

The Four Filters — What Each One Does

01
Accurate
The data underlying the insight is correct, complete, and free of the quality problems (missing values, duplicates, format conflicts) that could distort the finding. An insight built on dirty data is wrong even if the analysis is correct.
Test questions:
Where did this data come from and when was it collected?
Are there known data quality issues with this source?
Does the number seem plausible? Does it match what I know from the raw data?
Could dirty data (auto-closures, format conflicts, missing fields) be distorting this finding?
02
Context-Aware
The insight accounts for the business and operational context that might explain the pattern. A number that looks anomalous without context often makes complete sense once you know what was happening in the business at that time.
Test questions:
Is there a known event (deployment, campaign, holiday, new policy) that explains this?
Is this pattern seasonal or cyclical rather than a new anomaly?
Am I comparing against the right baseline for this period?
Would someone with full business context find this surprising?
03
Actionable
The insight points toward a specific decision or action that can be taken. An insight that correctly describes a real problem but offers no path forward has limited value. Strong insights connect a finding to a concrete next step.
Test questions:
What would someone do differently because of this insight?
Does it point to a specific cause that can be investigated or addressed?
Is this within the team's control to change?
If the finding is "interesting but no clear action," what additional analysis would make it actionable?
04
Verified
The insight has been checked against a second source, a corroborating signal, or a sanity-check before significant action is taken. Single-source findings that haven't been corroborated can lead to expensive mistakes.
Test questions:
Does this finding show up in a second data source or from a different angle?
Does it match what people in the field are observing?
What would change if this finding turned out to be wrong?
Is the potential cost of acting on a false insight proportionate to the verification effort?

Evaluating an AI-surfaced engagement insight

🤖 AI Insight Surfaced
"Mobile app daily active users declined 31% week-over-week, from 48,200 to 33,300. The decline is concentrated in users who accessed the app via the iOS platform. Android DAU remained flat."
Filter 1: Is this accurate?
What you check
You open the analytics platform and look at the raw event counts behind the DAU number. You check: Are iOS sessions being counted correctly? Did any SDK updates deploy this week that might affect tracking? Is the 33,300 number consistent with the raw event logs?
What you find
The raw event data confirms: iOS sessions dropped from ~48K to ~33K. The analytics SDK on iOS was not updated this week. The Android session count is stable. The numbers in the insight match the raw data. No obvious data collection issue.
✅
PASS: The data appears accurate. The 31% iOS-specific decline is real in the tracking data and not attributable to a measurement failure.
Filter 2: Is this context-aware?
What you check
You review what happened this week that might explain an iOS-specific drop. You check: Was there an iOS app update this week? Did Apple release a new iOS version? Was there any news about the app or a permission change? You also check whether the same week last year had similar patterns.
What you find
An iOS app update (v4.2.1) released Tuesday — two days before the DAU decline started Thursday. The update included a change to push notification permissions. Apple's App Store review confirms the update was downloaded by 78% of active iOS users within 48 hours of release. No similar drop occurred in the same week last year. The timing of the decline correlates with the update deployment.
🟡
PARTIAL PASS: The iOS app update is the most plausible contextual explanation. The context has narrowed the likely cause significantly — but hasn't confirmed it definitively yet.
Filter 3: Is this actionable?
What you check
Given the likely cause (iOS update v4.2.1), is there a specific action? You review what the update changed. The notification permission change in v4.2.1 changed the default permission request from "always allow" to "ask each time." You check: how many users likely saw the new permission dialog and declined? Did declining the permission affect their session behavior?
What you find
Post-update analytics show: 41% of users who upgraded declined the new notification permission. Users who declined notifications have a 67% lower 7-day return rate than users who accepted. The notification permission change is almost certainly driving the DAU decline — users who don't receive re-engagement notifications are not returning. Actionable: revert or modify the notification permission default, or add an in-app prompt explaining the value of notifications to users who declined.
✅
PASS: Highly actionable. The finding points directly to a specific cause (notification permission change) with a specific remediation (prompt declined users, consider reverting the default, A/B test notification permission UX).
Filter 4: Is this verified?
What you check
Before recommending a product change (reverting the notification permission default or adding an in-app prompt), you corroborate the finding. You check: do users who declined notifications also show up in support tickets or reviews? Can you cross-reference with user interviews or customer success reports? Is there a cohort of users who upgraded early vs. late where you can see the effect?
What you find
Customer success team confirms 3 enterprise accounts flagged that "mobile usage seemed lower this week" — independently corroborating the DAU decline without being prompted. App Store reviews this week include 7 new reviews mentioning "I keep forgetting to check the app" — consistent with users not receiving push notifications. The finding is corroborated by two independent sources (customer reports and App Store reviews), both consistent with the notification hypothesis.
✅
PASS: Corroborated by independent sources. This insight passes all four filters — it's accurate, context-aware, actionable, and verified. It's ready to drive a product decision.

📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

The four filters — Accurate, Context-Aware, Actionable, Verified — are applied in sequence. Accuracy catches data quality problems before analysis. Context-awareness catches patterns that are real but explainable by known events. Actionability separates interesting findings from findings that can drive decisions. Verification prevents acting on uncorroborated single-source findings.

The actionability filter is the most commonly skipped — and the most costly to miss. A finding that describes a correlation ("users who do X perform better") is often misread as a prescription ("make everyone do X"). Actionable insights must identify a specific mechanism that can be changed, not just a pattern that correlates with good outcomes.

Scale verification effort to decision reversibility. A low-stakes, easily-reversed decision can tolerate weaker verification. A high-stakes, expensive, or hard-to-undo decision deserves independent corroboration before action. The framework isn't meant to make you slower — it's meant to make sure the insights you act on are worth acting on.
