Lesson 3.1 — Clear Technical Communication

The Plain Language Standard in Tech Writing: What It Is and How to Apply It

Plain language isn't for consumer products or non-technical readers. It's the standard that saves lives in runbooks, prevents misconfigurations in API docs, and makes on-call engineers faster under pressure.

What Plain Language Actually Means

Most engineers hear "plain language" and think: simpler words for non-technical readers. That's not what it means.

Plain language is a writing standard developed for any documentation where the cost of misreading is high. It was developed for legal documents, medical instructions, and government regulations — places where a misread sentence has serious consequences. It applies equally to runbooks, API documentation, incident reports, and deployment guides.

Plain language doesn't mean removing technical content. It means organizing and writing technical content so that the right reader can find what they need, understand it correctly, and act on it — on the first read.

A runbook written in dense, passive, bureaucratic prose doesn't just slow engineers down. It creates the conditions for critical misreads at the exact moment when misreads are most dangerous.

The Five Principles of Plain Language in Tech Writing

These principles come from the U.S. Plain Language Guidelines and decades of research on how readers process technical information. They apply directly to every kind of IT documentation.

1
Use active voice — put the actor first

Passive voice hides who is doing what. In technical documentation, that ambiguity creates risk. If a runbook step says "the service should be restarted," the reader doesn't know if the system restarts it automatically or if they need to do it manually.

❌ Passive
The deployment should be verified once the pipeline has completed. Error logs should be checked and any issues should be escalated to the infrastructure team.
✅ Active
After the pipeline completes, you verify the deployment. Check the error logs. If you find any issues, escalate to the infrastructure team in #infra-oncall.

Active voice also shortens sentences naturally. The passive version above is 29 words. The active version is 26 — and dramatically clearer about who does what.

2
Keep sentences short — under 20 words when possible

Under pressure — on-call, during incidents, under time constraints — reading comprehension drops measurably. The cognitive load of a long sentence compounds. Engineers read the first half, start acting, and miss the caveat in the second half.

❌ Too long (52 words)
In order to ensure that the rollback procedure is executed correctly and that all dependent services are notified of the version change prior to the rollback completing, you should first drain all in-flight requests from the queue before initiating the rollback command, as failure to do so may result in partial writes to the database that are difficult to reverse.
✅ Split into short sentences
Before rolling back, drain all in-flight requests from the queue. Run: ./drain_queue.sh --wait 60

Why this matters: skipping queue drain causes partial database writes. Recovering from partial writes takes 2–4 hours.

Once drain completes, run the rollback command.

The rewrite is slightly longer in word count — but faster to read, easier to follow step by step, and safer to act on under pressure. Short sentences are not simpler. They're safer.

3
Use everyday words — except when technical terms are required

This principle is widely misunderstood. It doesn't mean replace "kubernetes" with "the container system." Technical terminology is precise and required. The principle applies to the connecting language around technical terms.

❌ Bureaucratic connective language
In order to facilitate the remediation of the aforementioned latency degradation, it is necessary to effectuate a modification to the cache TTL configuration parameter.
✅ Plain connective language
To fix the latency issue, change the cache TTL configuration.

"Facilitate the remediation" = fix. "Aforementioned" = the. "Effectuate a modification" = change. "Configuration parameter" = configuration (or just the specific parameter name). The technical content is identical. The bureaucratic words add length, not precision.

4
Put the most important information first

In journalism, this is called the inverted pyramid. In technical writing, it's how you make sure that engineers who are skimming under pressure see the critical information — not the background context that comes after it.

❌ Important info buried
Background: The payment service has a retry limit designed to prevent infinite loops in failed transaction handling. This limit was set during the v2.1 architecture redesign. If this limit is reached, the service will stop processing payments. This limit is 50 retries.
✅ Critical info first
⚠ If the payment service reaches 50 retries, it stops processing payments. This is a hard limit set in v2.1 to prevent infinite loops. When this happens, see §4.3 for recovery steps.

An engineer reading the first version during an incident might stop at "the retry limit was set during v2.1" — that context isn't urgent. In the second version, the operational risk is the first thing they read.

5
Use lists and structure — but only when they help

Bullet points and numbered lists make sequential steps and parallel items much faster to scan. They're the right choice for step-by-step procedures, checklists, and option comparisons. They're the wrong choice for explanations, context, and reasoning — which become harder to read when fragmented into bullets.

✅ Lists work well for
• Sequential procedures (do this, then this)
• Checklists before/after an action
• Options to choose between
• Parallel items (all the same type of thing)
❌ Lists break down for
• Explaining why something works
• Describing context or background
• Walking through a nuanced judgment call
• Anything where the relationship between ideas matters

Over-bulleting is a common problem in AI-generated documentation. AI tools default to bulleted lists for everything. When an engineer needs to understand a nuanced failure mode, a wall of disconnected bullets is harder to follow than two clear paragraphs.

Where Plain Language Failures Are Most Dangerous

Plain language matters in all technical writing. It's critical in documents that get read under pressure.

🚨 High-Pressure Reading Scenario
3am — P1 Incident — On-Call Engineer
An SRE is woken at 3am by a PagerDuty alert. The payment service is down. They open the runbook. They have minutes before the on-call escalation chain triggers. Here are three runbook sentences — each with a plain language failure that causes a critical misread under these conditions.
Failure 1 — Passive voice creates ambiguity
"Once the deployment has been verified as stable, the previous version should be decommissioned."
Who decommissions it — the engineer? An automated process? A specific team? At 3am with adrenaline high, engineers will act on the most plausible interpretation. If they're wrong, they've decommissioned the wrong version.
Failure 2 — Critical caveat buried at the end of a long sentence
"Run the drain script to flush pending queue items and ensure that all in-flight requests have been completed before proceeding to the rollback step, noting that skipping this step in high-traffic periods may cause data corruption."
Under pressure, engineers start reading the command and begin executing before they finish the sentence. The data corruption warning — at the end of a 44-word sentence — gets missed.
Failure 3 — Bureaucratic language slows down time-critical reading
"In the event that the aforementioned escalation procedure has been initiated and the issue remains unresolved after the requisite time period has elapsed, it is necessary to engage the vendor support team."
This sentence takes an extra 2–3 seconds to parse at 3am. Across a 2-hour incident with 40 runbook steps, that adds up — and cognitive load compounds. Plain version: "If escalation hasn't resolved the issue after 30 minutes, contact vendor support."

Active Voice Transformations — See the Pattern

Passive → Active: IT Writing Examples
❌ The model should be retrained when accuracy drops below threshold.
→
✅ Retrain the model when accuracy drops below 85%. Owner: ML team.
❌ Errors were detected in the pipeline logs and an investigation was initiated.
→
✅ We detected errors in the pipeline logs and started investigating.
❌ The deployment should not be approved until testing has been completed by QA.
→
✅ Don't approve the deployment until QA completes testing.
❌ It is recommended that the cache be cleared prior to running the migration script.
→
✅ Clear the cache before running the migration script.

A Note on Plain Language and AI-Generated Documentation

AI writing tools consistently violate plain language principles in specific ways. Knowing what to look for helps you review AI-generated docs faster.

AI tools default to passive voice because passive constructions appear frequently in technical writing in their training data. They produce long, multi-clause sentences because this is a common pattern in formal writing. They use bureaucratic connective language ("in order to," "it is necessary to," "in the event that") because it sounds authoritative.

When reviewing AI-generated technical documentation, these are the first things to fix: convert passive to active, split long sentences, and replace bureaucratic phrases with direct ones. These three changes alone will make most AI-generated documentation significantly safer to follow under pressure.

📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

Plain language is not about simplifying for non-technical readers. It's about writing technical content so that the right reader can find what they need, understand it correctly, and act on it — on the first read, under pressure. Runbooks, SOPs, and incident reports written in plain language save time and prevent misreads when they matter most.

The five plain language principles for IT writing: use active voice, keep sentences under 20 words, use technical terms but replace bureaucratic connective language, put critical information first, and use lists for sequential and parallel content — not for explanations and reasoning.

AI tools consistently violate plain language: they default to passive voice, long sentences, and bureaucratic connecting phrases. When reviewing AI-generated technical documentation, these are the first things to fix — and fixing them is faster than rewriting from scratch.
