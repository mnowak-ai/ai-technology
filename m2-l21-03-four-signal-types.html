Lesson 2.1 · Using AI to Diagnose Problems

Errors, Logs, Alerts, and Abnormal Behavior: A Field Guide to the Four Types of System Signals

Every technical diagnosis starts with signals — the data a system produces that tells you something is wrong, developing, or simply happening. This article goes deep on all four signal types: what each one is, what it looks like in the real world, and exactly how a professional responds to it.

Why Signal Recognition Is the First Diagnostic Skill

Before any diagnosis can begin — before AI can analyze anything, before a human can investigate anything — a signal must be recognized. Effective diagnosis begins not with the answer but with accurately understanding what kind of question you're being asked.

The four signal types in this article — errors, logs, alerts, and abnormal behavior — each tell you something different about what a system is experiencing. They require different reading skills, trigger different responses, and contribute different types of evidence to a root cause investigation. Confusing them — or treating them all the same — is one of the most common sources of diagnostic errors.

A system doesn't have problems. It has signals. Your job is to read those signals accurately enough to understand what problem they're pointing to.

The Four Signal Types — An In-Depth Field Guide

Select each signal type to get the full professional treatment — definition, real examples, what it means for diagnosis, and exactly how a skilled tech professional responds:

🔴 Errors — Failure Messages
The system is telling you something specific broke, right now, explicitly
An error is a direct failure notification generated when a specific operation cannot complete. Unlike logs (which record everything) or alerts (which fire when thresholds are crossed), errors are the system's way of saying "I tried to do something and I failed — here is exactly what failed." They are the most explicit signal type and usually the easiest to notice — but they are frequently symptoms of deeper problems rather than root causes themselves.
🖥️ Real Examples of Error Messages
ERROR: Connection timed out to DB-Main [Retry 1/3]
ERROR: Disk full — write operation failed (path: /var/log)
ERROR: SSL certificate expired — secure connection refused
CRITICAL: Database service unresponsive — all connections failed
ERROR: HTTP 503 — Service Unavailable (upstream timeout)
What errors tell you
Exactly what operation failed. When it failed. In some cases, how many times it has retried. The severity (ERROR vs CRITICAL). The component that generated the failure. This is the most concrete signal type — it points directly at a specific failure event.
What errors don't tell you
Why the operation failed — what underlying condition caused it. Whether this is a new problem or a recurring one. What produced the condition that caused the failure. Errors describe outcomes, not causes. A connection timeout tells you the database didn't respond — not why it didn't.
⚙️ How a professional responds to errors Don't treat the error as the root cause — treat it as the starting point of an investigation. Ask: what condition would produce this specific error? Then look backward in the logs to find what was happening before the error appeared. The error message tells you where the failure occurred; logs tell you what led there.
📋 Logs — Event Records
The system's running historical record — your primary evidence trail for any diagnosis
Logs are continuous, timestamped records of every significant event in a system — not just failures. They capture successful operations, warnings, state changes, configuration modifications, and failures all in chronological order. Unlike errors (which only record failures) logs capture the full picture of what was happening before, during, and after a problem. They are the primary evidence source for understanding causation rather than just observing effect.
🖥️ A Log Sequence Showing an Incident Develop
[10:28:01]
INFO Deploy — Version 2.1.3 deployment started
[10:28:44]
INFO DB-Main — Schema migration applied
[10:29:45]
WARN DB-Main — Slow query: 1,240ms (threshold: 200ms)
[10:30:15]
WARN API-Gateway — High latency: 2,100ms
[10:30:31]
ERROR DB-Main — Connection timed out [Retry 1/3]
[10:30:40]
CRITICAL DB-Main — Service unresponsive
The diagnostic power of logs
Logs reveal causation through sequence. In the example above, reading the log forward reveals that the schema migration preceded the slow queries — a crucial diagnostic clue. The CRITICAL event alone tells you nothing about why the database became unresponsive. The full log sequence tells you everything.
The challenge of logs at scale
Modern systems generate hundreds of thousands of log entries per hour. Finding the relevant sequence in that volume is what AI diagnostic tools are built to accelerate — they can scan millions of log lines and surface the relevant sequence faster than any human analyst. But identifying what's relevant still requires human judgment.
⚙️ How a professional reads logs Always read logs forward in time — oldest relevant entry first. Don't start at the error and read backward alone; start before the first warning sign and read forward to understand the full development of the problem. The causal chain lives in the sequence, and reading it in the right direction is how you find it.
🔔 Alerts — Proactive Notifications
Pre-configured warnings that fire when metrics cross defined thresholds — the early warning system
Alerts are automated notifications triggered when a monitored metric crosses a predefined threshold — CPU usage exceeds 90%, disk space falls below 15%, error rate exceeds 5% of requests, response time exceeds 2 seconds. Unlike errors (which are generated by the failing component itself), alerts are generated by external monitoring systems watching the metrics. Well-designed alerts catch problems before they become outages. Poorly designed alerts create so much noise that the important ones get ignored.
🖥️ Alert Dashboard — Multiple Simultaneous Alerts
🔴 CRITICAL — Database: High CPU Usage (>95%) | Status: New | Triggered: 10:45:22
🔴 CRITICAL — API Gateway: 5xx Error Rate Spike (>15%) | Status: New | Triggered: 10:45:29
🟡 WARNING — Storage: Volume /data reaching 85% capacity | Status: Acknowledged
🟢 INFO — Scheduled backup completed successfully | Status: Resolved
Reading alert patterns
Multiple alerts firing simultaneously are often more diagnostic than any single alert. When a database CPU alert and an API error rate alert fire within seconds of each other, the co-occurrence suggests a single shared cause rather than two independent problems. Alert timing and co-occurrence are key diagnostic signals.
Alert fatigue — a real risk
When alerting thresholds are set too low, teams receive hundreds of alerts per day. At that volume, critical alerts get ignored. Alert fatigue is a documented patient safety issue in healthcare and a documented reliability risk in IT. Good alert design means fewer, higher-quality signals — not more.
⚙️ How a professional responds to alerts Don't treat each alert in isolation. When multiple alerts fire, look for common causes before investigating each independently. Check the alert timeline: did they all fire at once, or did one appear first? The first alert in a cluster is often closest to the source. Then go to logs to understand what produced the alert condition.
📊 Abnormal Behavior — Performance Deviations
Metrics that are off from normal — no error has fired, no alert has triggered, but something is wrong
Abnormal behavior is the hardest signal type to catch because it doesn't announce itself. No error fires. No alert triggers. But a metric — response time, memory usage, request throughput, CPU load — is different from what it normally is. Detecting abnormal behavior requires knowing what normal looks like, which is why this signal type is where AI monitoring tools provide the most dramatic advantage over manual human monitoring: AI can track normal baseline behavior for thousands of metrics simultaneously and flag deviations too subtle for a human to notice.
🖥️ Key Metrics Showing Abnormal but Pre-Alert Behavior
DB Server CPU Load: 67% (normal baseline: ~35% | no alert threshold crossed yet)
API Gateway Memory: 22GB/32GB (climbing 2GB/hour | alert fires at 28GB)
Avg Response Time: 840ms (normal: 120ms | no alert threshold crossed yet)
Network Throughput: 12Mbps (normal: 85Mbps | significant drop, no alert set)
Why this signal type matters
Abnormal behavior often appears before errors or alerts. A system that is gradually consuming more memory will show abnormal behavior for hours before the memory alert fires and hours before OOM errors begin. Catching it early — before the threshold breach — is the difference between proactive prevention and reactive firefighting.
Where AI has the clearest advantage
Identifying abnormal behavior requires knowing what normal is — across hundreds of metrics, over different times of day, accounting for seasonal traffic patterns. AI can model this baseline continuously and flag deviations too subtle for human eyes scanning a dashboard. This is anomaly detection, and it's one of AI's most powerful diagnostic capabilities.
⚙️ How a professional responds to abnormal behavior Don't wait for an alert to confirm that something is wrong. If a metric is deviating from its normal range — even without crossing an alert threshold — investigate while you still have time before the situation escalates. The question to ask: is this deviation trending in a dangerous direction? If memory is climbing 2GB/hour and the alert fires at 28GB, you have roughly 3 hours before the alert fires. Use them.

📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

The four signal types appear in a characteristic order during real incidents: abnormal behavior first (often hours before failure), then alerts (when thresholds cross), then logs become the primary diagnostic evidence, and errors appear last (when specific operations fail). Understanding this sequence helps you know which signal type to look for at which stage of an investigation.

Errors are the most visible signal but often the least useful for root cause analysis — they tell you what broke, not why. Logs are the most useful for root cause analysis — they reveal causation through sequence. Abnormal behavior offers the most opportunity for prevention — it appears before problems become crises. Alert co-occurrence patterns are often more diagnostic than individual alerts.

AI diagnostic tools have different strengths across signal types: anomaly detection for abnormal behavior, pattern correlation for alert clusters, log sequence analysis for causation evidence, and error classification for historical pattern matching. Knowing which AI capability applies to which signal type helps you direct your investigation and evaluate AI suggestions more effectively.
