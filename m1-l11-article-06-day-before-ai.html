Lesson 1.1 · What Is AI in Tech Work?

A Day in Tech Before AI Gets Involved: What the Work Actually Looks Like

To understand what AI is actually changing in tech work, you need to understand what tech work looked like before AI got involved — the real steps, the time costs, and the points of friction that defined the job.

Why the Before Matters

It's easy to talk about AI in terms of what it adds. But the more useful frame is understanding what it changes — and to understand what it changes, you need a clear picture of what was there before.

Every AI tool in tech work was built to address a specific friction point in existing workflows. GitHub Copilot exists because writing boilerplate code is slow and tedious. AI-powered SIEMs exist because correlating security events across log volumes no human team can manually review was a real operational gap. Predictive monitoring tools exist because reactive incident response was costing organizations hours of downtime that pattern recognition could prevent.

The Patterns Across Every Role

Looking across these pre-AI workflows, three patterns appear in every role — and they're exactly the patterns that AI tools were built to address:

🔁
High-volume pattern recognition was done manually
Reading logs, reviewing code, searching documentation, scanning alerts — every role involved a significant portion of time spent searching for patterns in large volumes of information. This is exactly the task that scales poorly for humans and scales well for AI.
🕐
Context switching was constant and expensive
The pre-AI day involved moving between many different tools, windows, and contexts — editor to browser to documentation to Slack and back again. Each switch had a cost in lost focus and reorientation. AI tools that surface information in context (in the editor, in the alert dashboard, in the ticket) reduce this tax.
🧠
The work requiring the most judgment got the least time
In nearly every role, the tasks that most benefited from careful human judgment — architectural decisions, security analysis, incident postmortems, process design — were the tasks that got compressed when the high-volume, pattern-recognition tasks consumed the day. AI assistance that handles volume frees time for judgment.

The pre-AI day wasn't slow because the people were slow. It was slow because humans doing pattern recognition at machine scale is the wrong tool for the job. AI doesn't make people better — it removes a category of work that humans were never the right tool for.

📌 Article Summary

What to Carry Forward

Pre-AI tech work was defined by three friction points: high-volume pattern recognition done manually, constant expensive context switching, and judgment work compressed by volume work. AI tools were built specifically to address these friction points.

Understanding what the work looked like before AI is how you evaluate whether an AI tool is actually solving the right problem — and whether it's solving it at the right point in your workflow.

AI doesn't change what the high-judgment work requires — architecture decisions, security analysis, incident investigation, and process design still demand exactly what they always did. It changes where your time goes, which is only valuable if you redirect that time toward the work that needs it most.
