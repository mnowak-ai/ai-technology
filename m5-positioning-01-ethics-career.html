Module 5 — AI Ethics, Security & Responsible Use

Why AI Ethics Isn't Just Philosophy — It's Your Career on the Line

Before we dive into any lessons, there's something you need to understand first. AI ethics isn't a boring topic for people in lab coats. It's the thing that gets tech workers fired, sued, and talked about in the news — and it's also the thing that separates the professionals who get trusted with big projects from those who don't.

Let's Be Honest About What This Module Is Really About

When most people hear the word "ethics," they think about philosophy class — lots of questions with no clear answers, things that don't affect real life. This module is nothing like that.

Module 5 is about protecting yourself, your team, and the people your work affects when you use AI tools. That's a practical skill. And right now, it's one of the most important skills in the entire tech industry.

Here's why this matters right now, in 2025: AI tools are everywhere at work. They help write code, analyze data, send emails, screen job applications, flag security issues, and make decisions that affect real people's lives. And the workers who use those tools? They're responsible for what those tools do.

AI doesn't have a boss. You do. When the AI does something wrong, the person who used it — and the company that deployed it — has to answer for it.

Three Real Stories. Three Ruined Reputations.

Let's look at what actually happens when tech professionals get AI ethics wrong. These aren't made-up worst-case scenarios. These are real things that happened to real people and companies.

🔴 Data Security Failure
The Samsung Engineer Who Leaked Secret Code
In 2023, a software engineer at Samsung copied confidential source code — code that Samsung had spent years and millions of dollars developing — and pasted it into ChatGPT to get help debugging it. The engineer wasn't trying to steal anything. They just wanted to fix a bug faster. But ChatGPT is an external tool, and Samsung's proprietary code was now outside the company. Samsung had to ban all AI tools internally while they figured out the damage.
⚠️ Consequence: Internal AI ban, massive legal review, reputational damage, and a very uncomfortable conversation for the engineer involved. The code was already out — there was no taking it back.
🟠 Bias in Automated Decisions
The Amazon Hiring Algorithm That Discriminated Against Women
Amazon built an AI tool to screen job applications so that recruiters didn't have to read every resume. The AI learned from 10 years of past hiring data. The problem? Amazon had hired mostly men for a decade, so the AI learned to prefer male applicants. It started automatically downgrading resumes that included words like "women's" — as in "women's chess club" or "women's coding group." Amazon eventually scrapped the tool entirely.
⚠️ Consequence: Public embarrassment, congressional scrutiny about AI bias, and a hard lesson that AI learns from whatever data it's given — even when that data reflects old unfair patterns.
🔵 Overreliance on AI Output
The Lawyers Who Filed Fake Court Cases
Two lawyers used ChatGPT to help research legal cases for a real federal lawsuit. ChatGPT listed real-sounding court cases with real-sounding judge names and real-sounding rulings. The lawyers submitted those cases to the court. None of them were real. ChatGPT had completely made them up — a phenomenon called "hallucination." When the judge checked, not a single case existed. The lawyers faced sanctions, fines, and disciplinary hearings.
⚠️ Consequence: Public humiliation, professional sanctions, fines, and a nationwide reminder that AI outputs must always be verified before anyone acts on them.

Notice what all three of these stories have in common: nobody was trying to do something wrong. They were trying to work faster, make better decisions, or solve a problem. But they didn't understand how to use AI responsibly — and real people paid real prices for that.

The Myths That Get People Into Trouble

Most AI ethics mistakes happen because people believe things that sound reasonable but are actually wrong. Let's clear those up right now.

❌ The Myth
"If the AI did it, it's not my fault. I just used the tool."
✅ The Reality
You are responsible for every action you take with an AI tool — just like you'd be responsible for any work you submit, whether you wrote it yourself or got help. "The AI told me to" is not a defense in court, in HR, or with your manager.
❌ The Myth
"If it's already on the internet, it can't be private. So sharing it with an AI is fine."
✅ The Reality
Many things are private even if they're not technically secret — customer data, employee info, internal plans, source code. Inputting those into an AI tool can violate privacy laws, company policy, and your employment contract, even if the information "seems harmless."
❌ The Myth
"AI ethics is for the people who build AI, not the people who use it."
✅ The Reality
The people who use AI tools daily have enormous influence over what harm gets done. Builders set the guardrails; users decide what gets fed in, what gets shared out, and whether outputs get checked before they affect real people.
❌ The Myth
"This is a small company. Nobody is watching what I do with AI."
✅ The Reality
AI audit logs, data logs, and system records exist at most companies whether anyone is actively watching or not. And when something goes wrong — a breach, a complaint, a lawsuit — those logs get reviewed. Size doesn't protect you.

📌 What to Remember From This Article

The Foundation of Everything in Module 5

AI ethics isn't philosophy — it's professional protection. The workers who understand how to use AI responsibly are the ones who get trusted with bigger projects, advance faster, and don't end up as a cautionary tale in a company-wide email about what not to do.

Using an AI tool doesn't remove your responsibility for what it does. Whether it's a security breach, a biased output, or a made-up fact that goes to a client — the person who deployed the tool is accountable. "The AI did it" has never held up as a defense.

Most AI ethics failures aren't caused by malicious intent — they're caused by good people who didn't know what they were doing was risky. The three real-world cases in this article (Samsung, Amazon, the lawyers) all started with someone just trying to do their job more efficiently.

The habit that protects you most is simple: pause before you act. Check the policy. Verify the output. Flag the concern. Those three reflexes — built through the lessons in this module — are what separate a professional who uses AI well from one who creates problems they didn't mean to create.
