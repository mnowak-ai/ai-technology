Lesson 3.3 — Creating SOPs & Guides

SOP vs. Policy vs. Runbook vs. Playbook: Four Documents Tech Teams Confuse Constantly

Engineers use these four terms interchangeably. Auditors, regulators, and incident commanders do not. Here's the real difference — and why it matters more than you think.

Why Confusing These Four Documents Is a Real Problem

It feels like a terminology debate. It isn't. When a compliance auditor asks for your "data handling policy" and you hand them a runbook, they don't accept it — because a runbook is not a policy. When an incident commander grabs what they think is a runbook and starts following it step by step during an outage, but it's actually a general SOP with no real-time escalation steps, the response slows down.

The wrong document used in the right moment can be just as harmful as no document at all. Each of these four types serves a different purpose, lives at a different level of specificity, and is used by different people in different situations.

A policy tells you what must be true. An SOP tells you how to make it true. A runbook tells you what to do right now. A playbook tells you how to handle the whole range of scenarios.

The Four Documents, Defined

📋
Document Type 1
Policy
What must be true — and who is responsible for it
What is it?

A policy is a formal statement of what an organization requires, permits, or prohibits. It says what must happen — not how to make it happen. Policies are usually written for compliance, governance, or risk management purposes. They're approved by leadership and apply to everyone in scope.

Who uses it?

Compliance officers, auditors, legal teams, HR, executives. Engineers reference policies to understand what they're required to do — but they don't execute procedures from a policy document.

Level of detail
High-level — what, not how
Updated when
Annually or with regulatory changes
Owned by
Leadership / compliance / legal
Used during
Audits, onboarding, governance reviews
📄 Example
AI Model Data Retention Policy: All data used to train or evaluate AI models in production must be retained for a minimum of 24 months. Data must be stored in encrypted, access-controlled storage. Access logs must be maintained and available for compliance review within 48 hours of request.
📝
Document Type 2
Standard Operating Procedure (SOP)
How to do something — the same way, every time
What is it?

An SOP is a step-by-step procedure for completing a routine task. It's more detailed than a policy and more general than a runbook. SOPs cover repeated, predictable tasks: how to deploy a new service, how to onboard a new engineer, how to run a security access review. They're designed to produce consistent results across different team members.

Who uses it?

Engineers, operations staff, any team member who needs to complete the procedure. SOPs are often used during planned, scheduled work — not usually during active incidents.

Level of detail
Step-by-step, but not real-time specific
Updated when
Quarterly or after process changes
Owned by
Team lead / process owner
Used during
Planned, routine procedures
📄 Example
SOP: Deploying a New ML Model to Production — Steps for creating the deployment artifact, running validation tests, requesting deployment approval, staging rollout, and monitoring post-deployment metrics for 24 hours.
🔧
Document Type 3
Runbook
What to do right now — during a specific, active event
What is it?

A runbook is a document designed to be followed during an active operational event — typically an incident, alert, or time-sensitive procedure. Runbooks are hyper-specific: they tell the engineer exactly what commands to run, exactly what output to look for, and exactly what to do next. They're written for speed and accuracy under pressure.

Who uses it?

On-call engineers, site reliability engineers (SREs), incident responders. Runbooks are grabbed at the moment an alert fires or an incident begins. They need to be followable by someone who may be tired, stressed, and unfamiliar with this specific system.

Level of detail
Exact commands, real-time specific
Updated when
After every incident that touches it
Owned by
On-call team / SRE team
Used during
Active incidents and alerts
📄 Example
Runbook: AI Inference Service — High Latency Alert — Triggered by: PagerDuty alert when p99 latency > 2s for 5 minutes. Step 1: Run kubectl get pods -n ai-prod. Step 2: Check GPU utilization in Grafana. If GPU > 95%: proceed to Step 4 (scaling). If GPU normal: proceed to Step 6 (model version check)…
📚
Document Type 4
Playbook
The complete guide for handling a whole category of situation
What is it?

A playbook is a collection of procedures, decision trees, and guidelines for handling a broad scenario or category of events. Think of it as the "how we handle X" document — where X is something like "a security breach," "a major outage," or "a compliance audit." A playbook might link to multiple runbooks and SOPs depending on how the situation develops.

Who uses it?

Incident commanders, security teams, business continuity teams. Playbooks are strategic — they help the right person make decisions at each stage of a complex event. They say: given where we are, here's what to do next.

Level of detail
Strategic + tactical, covers multiple scenarios
Updated when
After major incidents or tabletop exercises
Owned by
Incident command / security / leadership
Used during
Complex, multi-stage, or high-severity events
📄 Example
Playbook: AI System Security Incident Response — Covers the full lifecycle of a security incident affecting AI infrastructure: initial detection, containment decision tree, communication protocol, regulatory notification requirements, forensic preservation steps, recovery procedures, and post-incident review requirements.

📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

Policy says what must be true. SOP says how to do something. Runbook says what to do right now. Playbook covers the whole scenario. These aren't stylistic preferences — they're four distinct document types with different audiences, update cycles, and purposes.

Runbooks are designed for use under pressure. They must contain exact commands, specific outputs to look for, and clear decision points — not the general guidance of an SOP. If your "runbook" reads like an SOP, an on-call engineer will lose time during an incident trying to translate general steps into real actions.

When an auditor, regulator, or executive asks for a document by type, they mean the specific type — not the closest thing your team has. Knowing the difference protects your team from compliance failures and ensures the right document is ready before it's urgently needed.
