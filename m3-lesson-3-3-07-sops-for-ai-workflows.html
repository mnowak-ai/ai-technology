Lesson 3.3 — Creating SOPs & Guides

Writing SOPs for AI-Assisted Workflows: The New Document Type Tech Teams Need

When an AI makes a decision in your production system, who is responsible? Where is that documented? Most existing SOP templates weren't designed to answer these questions. Here's how to fix that.

Why Traditional SOPs Fall Short for AI Workflows

Traditional SOPs assume humans do all the steps. Engineer does Step 1. Engineer verifies. Engineer escalates. Every action has a clear human actor and a clear human accountability.

AI-assisted workflows break this assumption. In a modern ML-powered system, an AI model might flag an anomaly, score a risk level, generate a recommendation, or take an automated action — and then a human reviews the result and decides what to do next. The AI is a step in the process.

When an AI step is embedded in a workflow, the SOP must answer three questions traditional templates ignore: What exactly does the AI do? What does a human verify before acting on it? And who is accountable if the AI is wrong?

If your SOP doesn't document who is responsible when the AI is wrong, then nobody is — which means nobody will catch it until the damage is done.

What an AI-Assisted Workflow SOP Needs to Show

Let's look at a real example: an AI fraud detection workflow where an AI model flags potential fraud, and a human analyst reviews and decides on action.

Here's what a traditional SOP might write for this workflow, and what it should actually say:

❌ Traditional SOP (Missing the AI layer)
1. Monitor the fraud detection queue for flagged transactions.
2. Review flagged transactions and determine if they are fraudulent.
3. If fraudulent, initiate the account freeze process.
4. Document the decision and notify the customer.
✅ AI-Aware SOP (Accounts for the AI step)
Step 1 [AI]: The fraud detection model scores each transaction on a 0–100 risk scale every 5 minutes. Transactions scoring >75 are placed in the analyst review queue automatically.

Step 2 [Human — Analyst]: Review each flagged transaction. For each one, verify: (a) the model score and the features that drove it (visible in the fraud console), (b) the customer's transaction history for the last 30 days, (c) any other recent flags on the same account.

Step 3 [Human decision — required]: You decide whether to freeze the account. The model's score is one input — not the decision. Document your decision reason in the fraud console regardless of outcome.

Step 4 [If model was wrong]: If you determine the model flagged a legitimate transaction: mark as false positive in the console, include a note explaining why. This feeds back into model retraining. Do not skip this step — it is how the model improves.

Step 5 [Accountability log]: Every action taken in this workflow must be logged with analyst ID and timestamp. The human analyst, not the AI model, is accountable for each freeze or non-freeze decision.

Five Sections an AI-Aware SOP Must Include

🤖
1. AI Step Description

What exactly does the AI do in this workflow? What input does it receive? What output does it produce? What threshold, score, or category triggers the next step? Don't say "the AI flags anomalies" — say what the AI measures, what the threshold is, and what happens automatically versus what requires human input.

📄 Example
AI Step: The inference model receives the last 24 hours of access log data and assigns a risk score (0–100) to each user session. Sessions scoring above 85 are automatically placed in the Security Review queue. Sessions scoring 60–85 are flagged for optional analyst review. Sessions below 60 are not surfaced.
👤
2. Human Verification Requirements

What must the human check before acting on the AI's output? This section is the most important in an AI-assisted SOP. Specify exactly what the human reviews, what they're looking for, and what should give them pause about trusting the model's output.

📄 Example
Before taking action on a model flag, the analyst must verify: (1) The model's confidence score and which features drove the flag (view in Threat Console → Model Explainability tab). (2) Whether the flagged behavior matches known attack patterns (reference: go/threat-patterns). (3) Whether the user recently made configuration changes that might explain the anomaly (check the audit log).
✍️
3. Human Decision Accountability

Who makes the final call? How is that decision logged? The SOP must make clear that the human — not the AI — is accountable for the outcome. This is a compliance and audit requirement in many regulated industries, and an operational safety requirement in all of them.

📄 Example
The analyst makes the final decision on all flagged sessions. Every decision (action taken, action declined, false positive marked) must be logged in the Security Review Console with: analyst username, timestamp, decision, and a one-sentence reason. Decisions without logged reasons are flagged for supervisor review within 24 hours.
❌
4. "When the AI Is Wrong" Procedure

What happens when the human determines the AI made an error — either a false positive (flagged something safe) or a false negative (missed something harmful)? This section should exist in every AI-assisted SOP and should tie model errors back to a feedback or retraining loop.

📄 Example
If you identify a false positive: mark it in the console. This data is automatically included in the next model evaluation. If you believe the model missed a genuine threat (false negative): immediately flag the session for escalated human review via #security-escalations. Do not wait for the model to catch it. Also submit a False Negative report via go/model-feedback within 24 hours.
📋
5. Model Health Monitoring Reference

How do you know if the AI model itself is degrading? What alerts exist? What does the human do when the model's performance drops? If the model is wrong 30% of the time this week instead of its normal 5%, that matters — and the SOP should tell humans how to notice and what to do.

📄 Example
Model performance is monitored via the ML Observability Dashboard (go/ml-dashboard). If the false positive rate exceeds 15% over a 24-hour window, the model is placed in "assisted mode" — all flags require manual analyst review before any automated action. Analysts are notified via #model-health-alerts. If assisted mode persists for >4 hours, page ML Platform on-call.
🎯 Apply It
What critical element is this AI-assisted SOP missing?

Your team has an AI model that automatically approves low-risk deployments and flags high-risk ones for human review. The SOP says:

1. Submit deployment via the CI/CD pipeline.
2. The risk assessment model evaluates the deployment and assigns a risk score.
3. If risk score < 40: deployment proceeds automatically.
4. If risk score ≥ 40: deployment is paused for human review.
5. Human reviewer approves or rejects the deployment.
🧠 Quick Check
A compliance auditor asks: "Who is responsible for the decision to freeze this customer's account?" The team's SOP says the AI model flags accounts for review and the human "approves the model's recommendation." Who does the auditor hold accountable?
📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

Traditional SOP templates assume every step has a human actor. AI-assisted workflows break this assumption — and if your SOP doesn't explicitly describe what the AI does, what the human verifies, and who is accountable for each outcome, you have a documentation gap that shows up in audits and postmortems.

Five sections AI-assisted SOPs must include: AI step description (what the model does and outputs), human verification requirements (what to check before trusting the model), human decision accountability (who decides and how it's logged), "when the AI is wrong" procedure (false positives, false negatives, feedback loop), and model health monitoring reference (how to know if the model itself is degrading).

Language matters in AI SOPs. "Approves the model's recommendation" is not the same as "reviews the model's output and makes the final decision." The first positions the human as a rubber stamp; the second positions them as an accountable decision-maker. For audit, compliance, and operational clarity, always write the human step as a decision, not an approval.
