Lesson 3.1 — Clear Technical Communication

How to Write for Multiple Audiences: The Same Incident, Three Different Ways

One P1 outage. Three documents. Three completely different readers — each needing something different from you. Here's exactly what changes, and why.

Why the Same Facts Need Different Documents

When a major incident happens, multiple people need to know about it — but they don't all need to know the same things. An SRE needs to know what broke and how to fix it. An executive needs to know what was affected and what decision is needed. A customer needs to know is my thing working, and when will it definitely work.

Writing one document that tries to serve all three audiences fails all three. The engineering details that help an SRE debug a problem are noise to an executive. The business impact that helps an exec make a decision is irrelevant to a customer who just wants to know if the app is down.

This is not about "simplifying" for non-technical readers. It's about understanding that every reader has a job to do, and your document should give them exactly what they need to do their job — nothing more, nothing less.

You don't write three documents because some readers are smarter than others. You write three documents because each reader has a different job to do after reading it.

The Incident

Let's use a real-world scenario. Here are the facts of the incident — everything that happened, as known by the engineering team.

🔴 P1 Incident — Raw Engineering Facts
Payment Processing Failure — November 15
Duration
6 hours, 14 minutes (02:06–08:20 UTC)
Impact
~34% of checkout attempts failed. Estimated 12,400 affected transactions.
Root cause
Memory leak in payment-service v4.1.2 introduced in the Nov 14 deployment. Pod crashed under sustained load when memory exceeded 2GB limit.
Detection
Customer support tickets triggered internal alert at 02:06. PagerDuty alert had misfired 47 minutes earlier (02:06 alert suppressed due to a misconfigured alert rule).
Fix deployed
Rolled back to v4.1.1 at 07:44 UTC. Full recovery confirmed at 08:20 UTC.
Revenue impact
~$187,000 in failed transactions. Some will be recovered via customer retry; estimated net loss ~$52,000.
Open items
Alert rule misconfiguration not yet fixed. Memory leak root cause under investigation. Post-incident review scheduled.
Who was involved
3 SREs, payments team lead, infrastructure team. Customer support escalated 214 tickets.

The Most Common Mistakes

Two failure patterns show up constantly when engineers write multi-audience incident communications.

Mistake 1: Writing one document for everyone. The result is a wall of technical text that executives won't read and customers can't understand. Or a vague, PR-friendly summary that engineers can't act on. One document serving three audiences serves none of them.

Mistake 2: Changing the facts for different audiences. This is different from adapting the level of detail — this is a real problem. If the engineering team knows the outage was caused by an alert misconfiguration that delayed detection by 47 minutes, and the executive summary omits this, that's not audience adaptation. That's information management. Executives may need to know about monitoring gaps to make staffing or process decisions. The test: does adapting the document for this audience omit anything that would change the decisions they need to make? If yes, you've gone too far.

🧠 Quick Check
An engineer writes an executive summary of a security incident. The summary says "the vulnerability was identified and patched within 4 hours" — which is true. It omits that the vulnerability had existed for 11 months before it was discovered, meaning customer data may have been accessible for nearly a year. Is this appropriate audience adaptation?
📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

Different audiences have different jobs. An SRE needs to fix and prevent. An executive needs to decide and communicate. A customer needs to know if they're affected and what to do. Write for the job, not the person's title or assumed technical level.

Adapting level of detail is appropriate. Omitting facts that would change someone's decisions is not. The test: does leaving this out mean the reader might make a decision they'd make differently if they knew? If yes, include it — just frame it at the right level of detail for that audience.

AI tools will often generate a single version of incident communication. Your job is to ask: who else needs to know about this, what do they specifically need, and does the AI draft serve that audience — or just the audience it was prompted to write for?
