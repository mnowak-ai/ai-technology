Lesson 5.4 — Compliance & Policy Awareness

Data Rules You Must Follow: A Plain-Language Guide for Tech Workers Using AI

These aren't suggestions — they're the rules that govern how you handle data when using AI tools at work. This article translates compliance requirements into concrete do/don't pairs you can apply immediately.

Why These Rules Exist

Data rules around AI use didn't come from nowhere. They exist because AI tools create a new and specific risk: data that was previously confined within your company's systems — client information, employee records, proprietary code, financial data — can now leave the building the moment someone types it into a prompt.

The rules in this article address that risk. Some come from laws (HIPAA, GDPR, CCPA). Some come from your employment contract. Some come from your company's internal policies. All of them apply whether or not you personally feel they're necessary in a specific situation. The moment you're handling work data in an AI tool, these rules are in effect.

Compliance rules exist because what seems low-risk in the moment can have high consequences after the fact. The rules protect you by telling you in advance which actions create which exposures.

Rule 1: No Personal Data in Prompts (Without Proper Authorization)

🔴 Rule 1 — Personal Data (PII)
Names, emails, IDs, and any information tied to a real person
❌ Don't
Paste a customer's name, email, address, phone number, or account ID into an AI tool to help write them a personalized email
Example: "Help me write an email to Sarah Johnson (sarah@clientco.com, account #48291) about her account issue..."
✅ Do
Ask the AI for an email template, then add the personal details yourself in your actual email client — never in the AI prompt
Example: "Help me write a professional email template to a customer with a billing issue. I'll fill in the specifics."
❌ Don't
Upload a CSV or spreadsheet containing user records, employee data, or customer information to an AI analysis tool
Example: Uploading "customer_data_Q3.csv" with 5,000 rows of names, emails, and purchase history
✅ Do
Use anonymized or synthetic data for AI-assisted analysis, or use only company-approved AI tools that have proper data processing agreements for this data type
Example: Create a sample dataset with fictional names for testing, or check whether your approved BI tool has AI features that operate within your data environment
❌ Don't
Include an employee's performance history, salary, or personal details in a prompt when drafting an HR document
✅ Do
Ask the AI for the structure and language of an HR document, then populate the actual employee details in your word processor

Rule 2: No Proprietary Information in Unapproved Tools

🔴 Rule 2 — Proprietary Data
Anything your company owns that isn't public
❌ Don't
Paste your company's source code into a free AI coding assistant for debugging help
Example: Copying the authentication module or payment processing logic into ChatGPT's free tier
✅ Do
Describe the problem functionally without the actual code, or use a company-approved AI coding tool that has a data processing agreement
Example: "I have a function that validates JWT tokens. It's failing with a 401 when the token contains special characters. What should I check?"
❌ Don't
Paste internal strategy documents, financial projections, or unreleased product roadmaps into an AI tool to summarize them
✅ Do
Ask the AI for a summary template or structure, then summarize the sensitive document yourself using that framework — keeping the actual content on your secure internal systems
❌ Don't
Share internal server configurations, architecture diagrams, or database schemas in a prompt — even without actual data
✅ Do
Describe the architecture type and the problem you're trying to solve at a generic level. The AI can help you solve most architectural problems without knowing your specific system names

Rule 3: Never Share Credentials — And Rotate Them If You Do by Accident

🔴 Rule 3 — Credentials
Passwords, API keys, tokens, and certificates
❌ Don't
Paste code that contains real API keys, database passwords, or authentication tokens into any AI tool
Example: Sharing a config file that contains STRIPE_SECRET_KEY or DB_PASSWORD with real values
✅ Do
Replace real credentials with clearly fake placeholders before sharing any code. Use "YOUR_API_KEY_HERE" or "EXAMPLE_PASSWORD" — the AI can help you debug the logic without the real values
Example: Replacing sk_live_realkey123 with sk_live_PLACEHOLDER before pasting
❌ Don't
Assume that because you deleted the chat, the credential exposure is undone
✅ Do
If you accidentally share a credential in any AI tool: rotate/revoke it immediately, regardless of whether you think it was seen or stored. Treat it as compromised from the moment of exposure — because it may be.

Rule 4: Understand Your Company's Retention Requirements

🟠 Rule 4 — Retention
How long data must be kept — and how it must be stored
❌ Don't
Use an AI tool to process work communications or documents that are subject to retention requirements if that tool doesn't preserve them in a compliant format
Example: Using an AI to summarize and delete legal communications that must be retained for 7 years under your company's policy
✅ Do
Know which documents in your role are subject to retention requirements, and ensure any AI-assisted processing doesn't interfere with those requirements. When in doubt, ask your legal or compliance team
❌ Don't
Assume that records stored only in an AI tool's conversation history meet your company's official recordkeeping requirements
✅ Do
Keep official records in your company's approved systems (email, document management, CRM). AI tool conversations are working notes — not official records for compliance purposes

A data analyst has a spreadsheet with 2,000 rows of customer purchase history — names, emails, and amounts. They want to use an AI tool to find patterns in the data. What's the most compliant approach?
📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

Four rules that always apply: no personal data in prompts without authorization, no proprietary information in unapproved tools, never share credentials (and rotate immediately if you do by accident), and understand your retention requirements before using AI to process regulated records.

The "do" version is almost always achievable. For personal data: use templates instead of real records. For proprietary code: describe the problem instead of sharing the implementation. For credentials: use placeholders. The AI can help you with almost everything without needing the sensitive details.

File encryption, privacy policies, and data deletion don't protect you from the moment of exposure. The risk is what happens at the instant your data enters the AI company's systems — not what happens to the file afterward. Prevention is the only reliable protection.
