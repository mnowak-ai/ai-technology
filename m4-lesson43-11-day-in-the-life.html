Jordan built their automation stack gradually over 18 months — starting with one simple flow and adding more as they got comfortable. None of the four flows required a developer. All were built in Power Automate over a total of about 6 hours. They collectively save Jordan approximately 3.5 hours per day that would otherwise go to repetitive triage, data entry, and report generation.

This article is a realistic account of what automation actually looks like from the inside — including the parts that still require Jordan's judgment, the morning it breaks, and what the job has actually become as a result of automation.

📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

Automation in a real IT role runs in the background — Jordan's four flows collectively save 3.5 hours per day without Jordan initiating any of them. That time goes directly into the investigative, judgment-intensive work that automation can't do: complex troubleshooting, relationship-building with users, and handling the unexpected. Automation didn't replace the job — it made room for the parts that matter most.

Human oversight is built into Jordan's daily workflow — not as a burden but as a functional check. The 15-minute triage review catches misclassifications before they become delays. The broken authentication token was caught by a failure alert and fixed in minutes. The SLA near-breach was caught with time to recover. None of this happens without active human engagement with what the automation is producing.

The distinction between automating routing (where a ticket goes) and automating resolution (marking it closed) is the difference between automating classification and automating outcomes. Classification errors are correctable in seconds. False resolutions corrupt every metric the team depends on. The rule: automate the preparation for human action; don't automate the action itself unless you have complete confidence in the automation's judgment — which, for complex and variable tasks like ticket resolution, you shouldn't.
