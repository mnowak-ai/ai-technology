Private Data vs. Public Data in Tech: Why the Distinction Changes How You Can Use It

The video's data classification workflow includes "Private" and "Public" as categories — but doesn't explain what makes data one or the other, or why it matters. This article fills that gap with concrete examples and a classification exercise.

It's Not Binary — It's a Spectrum

Most people think of data as either "private" or "public" — two buckets. In practice, organizations use a more nuanced spectrum with at least four levels. Each level determines who can access the data, how it can be used, and what happens if it's improperly shared.

The classification you assign to data determines everything that comes after it: who can see it, which tools it can be connected to, whether it can leave the organization, and what audit trail is required when it's accessed. For AI workflows specifically, misclassifying data as more public than it is produces the privacy violations discussed in the previous article.

The "Public Doesn't Mean Free to Use" Rule

A common misconception: if data is publicly accessible, you can use it for anything. That's wrong in at least three ways.

❌
Misconception: Publicly available data is always free to scrape and use in AI training.
✅
Reality: Many publicly accessible websites have terms of service that prohibit scraping. Using scraped data for AI training may violate copyright, terms of service, or in some jurisdictions privacy law — even if the data was publicly visible. Major AI companies have faced lawsuits over training data from publicly accessible sources.
❌
Misconception: If a customer shared information publicly on social media, I can use it for business analysis without restriction.
✅
Reality: GDPR and some other regulations apply even to publicly available personal data when it's collected and processed for business purposes. The fact that someone posted something publicly doesn't grant organizations unlimited rights to harvest and analyze it. The legal landscape here is evolving — when uncertain, consult your legal team.
❌
Misconception: Internal company data is private, so as long as I don't share it externally, I can use it any way I want.
✅
Reality: Internal data that contains personal information (employee records, customer data) has privacy requirements that govern internal use too — not just external sharing. Analyzing employee performance data without proper authorization or connecting customer records to an unapproved AI tool violates privacy requirements even if nothing leaves the organization.

Why Classification Is the First Decision in Every AI Workflow

In a tech role, you'll regularly encounter situations where someone asks you to connect data to an AI tool, export a dataset for analysis, or include a data source in a report. Before you do any of those things, classification is the first question you need to answer.

The practical decision tree is simple:

Decision Tree — Before Connecting Data to Any AI Tool
Public data? → Connect freely. No restrictions.
Internal data (no PII)? → Can use approved internal AI tools. Verify whether external tools are permitted by policy before connecting.
Confidential data (may contain PII)? → Check tool approval status. Consider anonymizing before connecting. Get explicit approval if sharing externally.
Restricted data (PII, PHI, credentials)? → Do not connect to external tools without explicit legal/compliance authorization. Internally, access only through approved, audited systems.

This decision tree isn't just a compliance exercise. It's also how you protect yourself. A tech worker who knows to ask "what classification is this data?" before connecting it to a new tool will never be the person who accidentally caused a data breach — and that matters for your career.

📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

Data classification is not binary. Most organizations use a four-level spectrum: Public (freely available, no restrictions), Internal (all-employee access, stay within the org), Confidential (limited access, needs approval to share), and Restricted (tightest controls, legal requirements). Each level has specific rules for who can access it and how it can be used in AI workflows.

Publicly accessible does not mean freely usable. Scraping public websites, using public social media data for business analysis, and using publicly available personal information in AI training all have legal constraints that vary by jurisdiction and context. When uncertain, check before proceeding.

Classification determines the first decision in every AI workflow. Before connecting any dataset to an AI tool, the classification of that data determines whether you can proceed, whether you need approval, and whether the data needs to be anonymized first. Building this habit — asking "what is this classified as?" before acting — is one of the most important professional habits for any tech worker who works with data.
